# Conclusion and Future Work

## Conclusion

Pressure-strain work is best supported in the setting it implicitly assumes: a myocardial wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity. In the simulations presented here, the LV and RV free-wall work-density ratio was captured reasonably well by the adjacent-pressure longitudinal proxy, making the RV free wall a meaningful part of the study rather than only a control case. The septum was different. It behaved as shared tissue between two pressure-loaded cavities. In the magnitude tests, septal work-density ratios were better preserved by two-sided pressure choices than by transmural pressure alone, but the correlation result depended on the loading path and metric.

The pressure sweep adds an important metric-dependent caution. Proxy rankings based on correlation across a loading path can change when the loading path changes. The exploratory and corrected sweeps demonstrated this directly: LV-like septal pressure can rank the corrected sweep well, while two-sided pressures better preserve septum/free-wall magnitudes. The safest conclusion is therefore not a universal pressure prescription for the septum, but a mechanical reading: free walls are closest to the adjacent-pressure assumption; septal tissue behaves as a shared wall; and any single-pressure septal proxy should be interpreted with care.

The study should also be read for what it is. It is not a clinical PAH cohort and it does not claim to reproduce disease progression. It is a controlled mechanics test of a common pressure-for-stress simplification. That controlled setting is useful: if the proxy behaves well in the free walls but becomes pressure-choice dependent in the septum even when the model is known exactly, then the septal ambiguity is not just an imaging problem. It is already present in the mechanics.

## Future Work

The most immediate and highest-value extension is to run the same free-wall and septal ratio analysis on the patient-specific meshes. This would test whether the free-wall result survives changes in wall thickness, chamber size, and septal curvature, and whether RV hypertrophy moves the proxy error in the direction suggested by Laplace-type reasoning. A useful design would separate geometry and loading as much as possible: healthy geometry with healthy and high-RV-pressure circulations, PAH or thickened geometry with the same two circulations, and possibly an artificially thickened RV wall to isolate wall thickness from the other patient-specific differences. If these simulations are completed, they should be treated as the main geometry-sensitivity test rather than as a new clinical cohort.

A second extension is a local stress-scale study. The bulk geometry correction tested here did not improve on adjacent pressure, but that does not rule out geometry; it rules out one crude global correction. A more physical proxy would use local wall thickness, curvature, and possibly a transventricular coordinate, especially in the RV free wall and septum. This is the natural place to revisit the through-wall pressure idea: not as a final clinical formula, but as a diagnostic for whether the septal pressure scale moves from LV-like on the LV side toward RV-like on the RV side.

A third extension is a higher-resolution septal mesh or an idealized septal mechanics problem. The present mesh is adequate for integrated regional work, but it is not designed to resolve detailed transmural stress profiles. A refined septum would make it possible to test whether the through-wall weighted pressure is a genuine local approximation or simply a useful coarse average.

A final extension is clinical validation. Within this model, free-wall pressure-strain work indices were more stable than septal indices, which were pressure-choice dependent in RV pressure overload. Testing this clinically would require CMR geometry, reliable RV pressure estimates, and pressure-strain work from echocardiography or feature tracking in the same patients. Without measured hemodynamics, assigning patient-specific circulation parameters from anatomy alone would introduce a new source of uncertainty.
