# Conclusion and Future Work

## Conclusion

Pressure-strain work is best supported in the setting it implicitly assumes: a myocardial wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity. In the simulations presented here, the LV and RV free-wall work-density ratio was captured reasonably well by the adjacent-pressure longitudinal proxy, making the RV free wall a meaningful part of the study rather than only a control case. The septum was different. It behaved as shared tissue between two pressure-loaded cavities. In the tangent-longitudinal magnitude tests, septal work-density ratios were better preserved by LV pressure and nearby LV--RV mixtures than by transmural pressure alone, but the correlation result depended on the loading path and metric.

The pressure sweep adds an important metric-dependent caution. Proxy rankings based on correlation across a loading path can change when the loading path changes, and a pressure choice that ranks cases well can still fail as a magnitude proxy. The corrected sweep demonstrated this directly: transmural pressure ranked the tangent-longitudinal septal cases best, while LV pressure better preserved septum/free-wall magnitudes. The safest conclusion is therefore not a universal pressure prescription for the septum, but a mechanical reading: free walls are closest to the adjacent-pressure assumption; septal tissue behaves as a shared wall; and any single-pressure septal proxy should be interpreted with care.

The study should also be read for what it is. It is not a clinical PAH cohort and it does not claim to reproduce disease progression. It is a controlled mechanics test of a common pressure-for-stress simplification. Late reference-state checks showed that the severe fixed-geometry cases probably overstate absolute RV and RV-side septal stress-strain work densities, because high RV pressure is imposed without RV hypertrophy, curvature remodelling, or regional passive material adaptation. That limitation narrows the claim but does not remove it. The energy balance verifies the internal work accounting of the model, and the reason for the model is the hierarchy of lost mechanics: pressure alone loses wall geometry, Laplace-type estimates still lose local anisotropic and biventricular effects, and pressure-strain work further loses tensor stress and tensor strain. The principal-strain diagnostic showed that this strain reduction is measurable: dominant septal shortening was closer to the fibre direction than to longitudinal, radial, or circumferential strain. However, alternative fixed strain components did not remove the pressure-choice problem. That controlled setting is useful: if the proxy behaves well in the free walls but becomes pressure-choice dependent in the septum even when the model is known exactly, then the septal ambiguity is not just an imaging problem. It is already present in the mechanics.

## Future Work

The most immediate and highest-value extension is a clean patient-specific geometry and reference-state sensitivity study. The late checks already point in the expected direction: increasing RV and septal passive stiffness made the unloaded RV reference less collapsed, and the thicker PAH patient mesh gave lower RV work density than the healthy mesh at similar RV pressure. However, these checks are not yet a controlled result set. The stiffness sweep was unloading-only rather than a full production rerun, the healthy high-pressure patient cases were incomplete, and the patient comparison changes anatomy, loading, tagging, and numerical robustness together. A stronger design would separate geometry and loading as much as possible: healthy geometry with healthy and high-RV-pressure circulations, PAH or thickened geometry with the same two circulations, and possibly an artificially thickened RV wall to isolate wall thickness from the other patient-specific differences. Such simulations should be treated as the main geometry-sensitivity test rather than as a new clinical cohort.

A second extension is a local stress-scale study. The bulk geometry correction tested here did not improve on adjacent pressure, but that does not rule out geometry; it rules out one crude global correction. A more physical proxy would use local wall thickness, curvature, and possibly a transventricular coordinate, especially in the RV free wall and septum. This is the natural place to revisit the through-wall pressure idea: not as a final clinical formula, but as a diagnostic for whether the septal pressure scale moves from LV-like on the LV side toward RV-like on the RV side.

A third extension is a higher-resolution septal mesh or an idealized septal mechanics problem. The present mesh is adequate for integrated regional work, but it is not designed to resolve detailed transmural stress profiles. A refined septum would make it possible to test whether the through-wall weighted pressure is a genuine local approximation or simply a useful coarse average.

A final extension is clinical validation. Within this model, free-wall pressure-strain work indices were more stable than septal indices, which were pressure-choice dependent in RV pressure overload. Testing this clinically would require CMR geometry, reliable RV pressure estimates, and pressure-strain work from echocardiography or feature tracking in the same patients. Without measured hemodynamics, assigning patient-specific circulation parameters from anatomy alone would introduce a new source of uncertainty. A clinical extension should also compare strain inputs explicitly: longitudinal, circumferential, radial, area, and possibly CMR-derived three-dimensional strain. The present radial, circumferential, and principal-strain diagnostics suggest that richer deformation measurements are worth testing, but they do not yet provide a validated replacement for the longitudinal pressure-strain workflow. Recovering the dominant deformation direction would require full local three-dimensional strain information, not only a different fixed scalar component.
