# Conclusion and Future Work

## Conclusion

Pressure-strain work is best supported in the setting it implicitly assumes: a myocardial wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity. In the primary capped-reference simulations presented here, the LV and RV free-wall work-density ratio was captured reasonably well by the adjacent-pressure longitudinal proxy, making the RV free wall a meaningful part of the study rather than only a control case. The septum was different. It behaved as shared tissue between two pressure-loaded cavities. In the tangent-longitudinal magnitude tests, septal work-density ratios were better preserved by LV pressure than by transmural pressure alone, and the model-side fibre diagnostic favoured mean and through-wall weighted pressure.

The pressure sweep adds an important metric-dependent caution. Proxy rankings based on correlation across a loading path can change when the loading path changes, and a pressure choice that ranks cases well can still fail as a magnitude proxy. In the primary capped-reference sweep, transmural pressure did not even rank septal tissue work well; it was also the worst magnitude candidate. The safest conclusion is therefore not a universal pressure prescription for the septum, but a mechanical reading: free walls are closest to the adjacent-pressure assumption; septal tissue behaves as a shared wall; and any single-pressure septal proxy should be interpreted with care.

Reference-state checks showed that severe fixed-geometry RV and RV-side septal work densities depend on how inverse unloading is posed when high RV pressure is imposed without RV hypertrophy, curvature remodelling, or regional passive adaptation. The capped-reference sweep is the most defensible bounded option tested here. That limitation narrows the claim but does not remove it.

The energy balance verifies the internal work accounting and motivates the comparison itself. The cascade has three reductions: fibre projection, pressure-for-stress substitution, and longitudinal-strain reduction. The pressure-for-stress step accounts for the bulk of the magnitude loss; the directional reductions are smaller. The fibre-strain diagnostic showed that this strain reduction is measurable in the septum: changing the strain direction changed which pressure choices were most faithful.

The controlled setting is useful precisely because the model is known exactly. If the proxy still becomes pressure-choice dependent in the septum here, septal ambiguity is not only an imaging problem. It is already present in the mechanics.

## Future Work

The most immediate and highest-value extension is a clean patient-specific geometry and reference-state sensitivity study. The late checks already point in the expected direction. Increasing RV and septal passive stiffness made the unloaded RV reference less collapsed. In the patient-geometry mini-sweep, the thicker PAH mesh gave lower septal stress-strain work density than the healthy mesh at similar or higher RV pressure, while the RV free-wall direction depended on the achieved pressure level. The pressure-strain proxies did not show the same attenuation, which sharpens the question: PAH remodelling may reduce tissue work density by adding wall volume and curvature, while a pressure-strain index continues to track pressure and longitudinal shortening. This extension should also test length-dependent active tension, so that preload and local fibre stretch can feed back into contractile force through a Frank-Starling-type response.

These checks are not yet a controlled result set. The stiffness sweep was unloading-only rather than a full production rerun, and the healthy/PAH comparison changes anatomy, loading, cavity-volume scaling, and reference-state inference together. A stronger design would separate geometry and loading: healthy geometry with healthy and high-RV-pressure circulations, PAH or artificially thickened geometry with the same two circulations. Such simulations should be treated as the main geometry-sensitivity test, not as a new clinical cohort.

A second extension is a local stress-scale study. A more mechanically grounded proxy would use local wall thickness, curvature, and possibly a transventricular coordinate, especially in the RV free wall and septum, rather than treating cavity pressure as a uniform regional stress scale. This is the natural place to revisit the through-wall pressure idea: not as a final clinical formula, but as a diagnostic for whether the septal pressure scale moves from LV-like on the LV side toward RV-like on the RV side.

A third extension is a higher-resolution septal mesh or an idealized septal mechanics problem. The present mesh is adequate for integrated regional work, but it is not designed to resolve detailed transmural stress profiles. A refined septum would make it possible to test whether the through-wall weighted pressure is a genuine local approximation or simply a useful coarse average.

A final extension is clinical validation. Within this model, free-wall pressure-strain work indices were more stable than septal indices, which were pressure-choice dependent in RV pressure overload. Testing this clinically would require CMR geometry, reliable RV pressure estimates, and pressure-strain work from echocardiography or feature tracking in the same patients. Without measured hemodynamics, assigning patient-specific circulation parameters from anatomy alone would introduce a new source of uncertainty. A clinical extension should also compare strain inputs explicitly: longitudinal, circumferential, radial, area, and possibly CMR-derived three-dimensional strain. The model-side fibre diagnostic suggests that richer deformation measurements are worth testing. Recovering the dominant deformation direction would require full local three-dimensional strain information, not only a different fixed scalar component.
