# Calibrating the Circulation Model

## The Parameter Identification Problem

The Regazzoni model contains roughly twenty effective parameters — the contractility and passive stiffness of each chamber, the resistances and compliances of the systemic and pulmonary Windkessel networks, the valve resistances, the activation timings, and the initial blood volume distribution. None of these are directly measurable in a patient; they are engineering constants chosen so that the model's macroscopic output matches clinical observation. Finding values that produce realistic hemodynamics is a coupled, nonconvex inverse problem, and its difficulty is the subject of this chapter.

The coupling is what makes naive iteration fail. Raising systemic vascular resistance lifts mean arterial pressure, which increases LV afterload, which lowers stroke volume, which changes end-diastolic volume, which modifies filling pressure, which alters the next cycle's ejection. In a closed-loop model these perturbations return to their origin on the timescale of a few beats, so adjusting one parameter to fix one target almost always breaks another that was previously acceptable. A further complication is that the Regazzoni model distributes a fixed total blood volume across all compartments according to the local compliance and resistance; ventricular filling pressures emerge from this distribution rather than being prescribed directly. There is no "set LV preload" knob.

## Decoupling EDP and EDV: A Nonlinear EDPVR

Before describing the optimization itself, one structural limitation of the standard Regazzoni formulation had to be resolved. The published model uses a linear time-varying elastance,

$$
P(V, t) = E(t) (V - V_0), \qquad E(t) = E_B + (E_A - E_B) a(t),
$$

where $E_A$ is the active systolic elastance, $E_B$ is the passive diastolic elastance, and $a(t)$ is the activation waveform. In this formulation the end-diastolic pressure and end-diastolic volume are controlled by the same parameter $E_B$: once the unstressed volume $V_0$ is fixed, the diastolic operating point is pinned to the single line $P_\text{ED} = E_B (V_\text{ED} - V_0)$. For a mesh whose geometric cavity volumes are small relative to population averages — the UKB mean biventricular geometry has an LV end-diastolic volume of approximately 111 mL and an RV end-diastolic volume of 77 mL — the optimizer cannot simultaneously match the clinical EDP range of 4–12 mmHg and hit the correct EDV without forcing the passive stiffness too high or too low.

The fix was to replace the passive (diastolic) component of the chamber pressure law with an exponential pressure-volume relationship, following the Klotz EDPVR framework {cite}`klotz2006single`:

$$
P(V, t) = (E_A - E_B) a(t) (V - V_0) + \frac{E_B}{k_E} \bigl(e^{k_E (V - V_0)} - 1\bigr).
$$

The new parameter $k_E$ has units of inverse volume and controls how quickly the chamber stiffens as it approaches its distensibility limit. In the limit $k_E \to 0$ the expression reduces to the original linear model, so the modification is backward-compatible. Near the unstressed volume the passive pressure grows linearly as $E_B (V - V_0)$, keeping the low-pressure filling regime compliant, while at larger volumes the exponential term dominates and prevents overfilling. This decouples the two constraints that the linear model had collapsed onto a single line: the slope at $V_0$ sets the low-pressure filling response and $k_E$ sets the stiffening scale, so EDP and EDV can be matched independently. {numref}`fig-klotz-edpvr` shows the resulting passive pressure curves for the calibrated healthy LV and RV chambers, with the linear EDPVR ($k_E = 0$) drawn for comparison and the calibrated end-diastolic operating point marked on each curve. The modification was applied to the LV and RV chambers only; the thin-walled atria remained linear. The Jacobian used by the stiff ODE integrator had to be updated for the volume-dependent $dP/dV$ when $k_E > 0$, but no other change to the solver was needed.

The decision to introduce $k_E$ was empirical rather than preemptive. Early calibration runs used the standard linear Regazzoni elastance, and the optimizer consistently failed to simultaneously satisfy the pressure targets and the mesh-volume constraints. At the severe end of the sweep, the RV end-systolic and end-diastolic pressure targets could not both be reached without driving the passive stiffness into a regime that distorted the rest of the pressure-volume loop. At the low-pressure end, the linear form tended to over-pressurize the ventricles near their target end-diastolic volumes. Introducing the Klotz-style exponential term resolved these practical failure modes. It should therefore be understood as a calibration device that gives the closed-loop model enough diastolic flexibility to match the mesh volumes and pressure targets, not as a claim that the fitted $k_E$ values are direct measurements of patient myocardial stiffness.

## The Severity Spectrum and Its Single Independent Variable

Rather than calibrating a single high-RV-pressure case in isolation, the simulation framework is calibrated across a spectrum of RV pressure loadings ranging from near-normal pulmonary hemodynamics to cases where right ventricular systolic pressure approaches systemic levels. The motivation is mechanistic rather than epidemiological. The spectrum is not meant to reproduce a patient trajectory; it is a controlled loading path used to test how pressure choices for the longitudinal proxy respond when RV pressure is increased while the anatomy and material model are held fixed. A spectrum is needed because a single operating point can show whether a proxy is wrong, but not whether the ranking of pressure choices is stable when the loading changes.

This framing has a consequence that is worth stating explicitly: the spectrum's severity axis is **RV end-systolic pressure, treated as a prescribed mechanical scenario rather than an epidemiological severity class**. This departs from the earlier draft of this chapter, which treated each severity label as a clinical category with its own cluster of literature-derived pressure targets. Two contemporary sources motivate the reframing. The 2022 ESC/ERS PH guidelines state explicitly that mean pulmonary arterial pressure "provides little prognostic information, except in acute vasodilator responders" {cite}`humbert2022esc`. The REVEAL registry of 2716 prospectively enrolled PAH patients reaches the same conclusion in its multivariable survival analysis: mPAP was not retained as an independent predictor once RAP, CI, and functional variables were controlled for {cite}`benza2010reveal`. Classifying severity by RV_ESP therefore does not map onto a published clinical severity ladder. What it does map onto is the pressure-loading change that the 3D analysis actually uses. The spectrum is presented accordingly.

Every anchor target in the spectrum now carries a citation to a specific table in a specific paper. The full row-by-row audit is archived separately (`cardiac-work/results/docs/target_grounding_audit.md`) and summarised in the remainder of this section. The coupled 3D analysis in the results chapter uses 16 achieved pressure loadings: the original pressure targets plus intermediate cases inserted to make the correlation analyses less dependent on a sparse set of points.

### RV_ESP as the independent variable

RV_ESP varies smoothly across the spectrum, with the main anchors pinned to primary measurements and the remaining targets honestly treated as interpolations:

- The low-pressure anchor, RV_ESP = 22 mmHg, comes from Kovacs et al.'s systematic review of 1187 healthy subjects at rest supine: systolic PAP mean 20.8 ± 4.4 mmHg {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`.
- The upper-normal anchor, RV_ESP = 30 mmHg, uses the mean+2SD upper limit of normal from the same Kovacs table (ULN 29.6 mmHg).
- The severe-pressure anchor, RV_ESP = 75 mmHg, uses the median PASP from Tello et al.'s invasive severe-PH cohort of 52 patients: PASP 75 ± 24 mmHg {cite}`tello2019tapse`.
- The remaining pressure targets are interpolated or extrapolated between these anchors. They are explicitly not registry-cited per-bracket values; the contemporary registries do not publish a per-severity sPAP ramp because sPAP is not a prognostic variable.

### Held-constant targets

The remaining five pressure targets (LV_ESP, LV_EDP, Ao_DBP, LA_P_MEAN) are held at their healthy-normal values throughout the spectrum. The rationale is that PAH is a *pre-capillary* disease: the pulmonary arteriolar resistance rises, the RV adapts or fails against the elevated afterload, but the left heart is not the primary pressure-loaded chamber. Benza et al.'s REVEAL registry makes this concrete with a systemic systolic blood pressure of 116 ± 17 mmHg across the full 2716-patient PAH cohort, with no severity-stratified decline reported {cite}`benza2010reveal`. Accordingly:

- **LV_ESP = 120 mmHg** for all severities (aortic systolic ≈ LV peak in the absence of aortic stenosis; consistent with Benza's SBP 116 ± 17).
- **LV_EDP = 8 mmHg** for all severities (Kovacs PAWP 8.0 ± 2.9 mmHg, n=882).
- **Ao_DBP = 80 mmHg** for all severities (conventional adult normal consistent with Benza preserved SBP).
- **LA_P_MEAN = 8 mmHg** for all severities (Kovacs PAWP; the pre-capillary diagnostic boundary PAWP ≤ 15 mmHg from Humbert Table 5 is a ceiling, not a target).

An earlier version of the optimizer varied all four of these across the spectrum (LV_ESP declining 118 → 90, LV_EDP declining 8 → 4, Ao_DBP declining 80 → 60, LA_P_MEAN sloping 8 → 7). None of those declines has a source in the primary literature this study cites; they were introduced during an LLM-assisted build of the target table and carried forward uncorrected until the end-to-end source audit described below. Holding them constant is both a correction and a simplification.

### ESC-banded physiological guardrails

Three of the remaining quantities — RV_EDP, the RVEF floor, and the CI floor — are targeted using the three-strata risk model in Table 16 of the 2022 ESC/ERS guidelines {cite}`humbert2022esc`. The lower-RV-pressure targets use the ESC *low-risk* values (RAP < 8 mmHg, CI ≥ 2.5 L/min/m², RVEF > 54%). The middle part of the spectrum uses the *intermediate-risk* band (RAP 8–14, CI 2.0–2.4, RVEF 37–54%). The highest-RV-pressure targets use the *high-risk* band (RAP > 14, CI < 2.0, RVEF < 37%), with an extrapolated below-band floor for the most severe case. These bands are used as physiological guardrails, not as proof that the pressure sweep is a clinical severity ladder.

The LV ejection fraction floor is held constant at 55% across the whole spectrum. The motivation for a per-severity decline, if one existed, would be to encode contractile failure; the meta-analytic evidence is that LV EF is *not* an independent prognostic variable in PAH (pooled hazard ratio 1.06 per 5% decrease, 95% CI 0.95–1.18, *p* = 0.301 across 539 patients in 8 studies) {cite}`baggen2016cmr`. The thing that does decline as RV failure progresses is LV *volume*, not LV ejection — the septal shift underfills the LV — and that effect is captured already by the mesh-geometric LV_EDV constraint.

### Cardiac index rather than cardiac output

The floor on systemic forward flow uses cardiac *index* (CI, L/min/m²) rather than absolute cardiac output (L/min), following Humbert Table 16. This requires the optimizer to know the body surface area of the patient whose hemodynamics are being calibrated: the simulated CO is divided by the patient's BSA before being compared to the risk-stratum floor. For the UKB mean mesh we take BSA = 1.75 m² as a canonical adult mean; for patient-specific cases the BSA is taken from the patient's own anthropometric record.

## Calibration Honesty: How the Targets Got Here

The target table above is the output of an end-to-end audit of every number in the optimizer against the primary literature. That audit was not the first version of the table; it is the third, and the first two are worth describing because the reasoning that led to the current numbers is inseparable from the history.

The first attempt pinned each spectrum row to a clinical severity label (`mild`, `moderate`, and so on) drawn from the ESC risk table, and then asked an LLM assistant to fill in plausible per-row values for each of the six pressure targets. The LLM produced a smoothly varying table that looked internally consistent — every column monotonic across severity, every row within a defensible physiological envelope — and the whole table was referenced to the 2022 ESC/ERS guidelines {cite}`humbert2022esc` and the Kovacs systematic review {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. What went unchecked at that stage was whether the individual numbers actually appeared in those sources. They did not. The ESC guideline's Table 16 stratifies prognosis by right atrial pressure, cardiac index, RVEF, SvO₂, 6-minute walk distance, and NT-proBNP — not by mPAP, and not by LV systolic or diastolic pressures. The Kovacs review reports healthy normals only. The per-severity ramps for LV_ESP (118 → 90), LV_EDP (8 → 4), Ao_DBP (80 → 60), LA_P_MEAN (8 → 7), and LV_EF floor (55 → 20%) had therefore been extrapolated by the LLM and passed through the build process under a single top-level citation that did not actually contain the numbers. The error was not caught until the full table was re-examined row by row against the actual PDFs.

The mechanistic consequence of leaving these ramps in place is worth naming. The central quantity of this thesis is the end-systolic transmural pressure $P_\text{LV,ES} - P_\text{RV,ES}$. An LV_ESP target that declines from 118 to 90 across the spectrum does half of the work of collapsing this transmural load. A reader watching transmural pressure fall from 88 mmHg at the healthy end to near zero at the end-stage end, with no countervailing signal, would attribute the collapse to the rise of RV_ESP alone — but in the uncorrected table the collapse was driven equally by LV_ESP being manually pulled downward. The finding is not invalidated by this — RV_ESP was the dominant driver, and with the LV held constant the transmural collapse is still sharp, since RV_ESP still rises from 22 to 95 mmHg — but the uncorrected table was attributing half the collapse to a target that had no primary source.

The present audit fixes this. Every row of every column is either pinned to a specific table in one of six primary sources (Humbert 2022, Kovacs 2009, Benza 2010, Benza 2012, Tello 2019, Baggen 2016) or is flagged as an interpolation between two such anchors. The full provenance table is in the archival audit document referenced at the top of this section; the citation density of this chapter is correspondingly higher than it would have been had the original ramps been retained without scrutiny. A second consequence, more structural, is that the spectrum is no longer claimed to map onto an epidemiological severity ladder — it is a mechanical pressure scenario sweep, as described at the start of this section.

A residual caveat carried over from earlier attempts is worth keeping: even with the revised targets, the optimizer must balance the RV pressure targets against the geometric constraint that cavity volumes match the mesh. For the UKB mean mesh the RV cavity is 77 mL, and the volume weight in the cost function is twice the RV_ESP weight. This means the lowest-pressure target, RV_ESP = 22 mmHg, may still land at an achieved RV pressure somewhat above target, since defending the 77 mL RV cavity pushes the model toward a slightly elevated pulmonary pressure. Where this happens in the achieved hemodynamics, the thesis reports the achieved values directly rather than hiding the mismatch behind the nominal label.

## Cost Function with Dynamic Weight Relaxation

Each trial of the optimizer runs the 0D model to periodic steady state and extracts last-beat metrics — peak and end-diastolic pressures for both ventricles, aortic diastolic pressure, mean left atrial pressure, end-diastolic volumes, ejection fractions, cardiac index, and the LV–RV stroke volume imbalance that serves as a closed-loop mass conservation check. The cost is a weighted sum of relative errors, but with the weights modulated by how close each target already is:

$$
w_\text{eff}(\theta) = \begin{cases}
w_\text{base} \cdot (|\text{err}| / \tau) & |\text{err}| < \tau, \\
w_\text{base} & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

with relaxation threshold $\tau = 5\%$. The effect is that once a target is within five percent of its clinical value, its weight is progressively reduced, freeing the optimizer to focus on the remaining gaps. Without this relaxation, the optimizer oscillates: fixing one target unbalances another that was previously within tolerance, and the cost function stops decreasing.

The base weights encode the mechanical priorities described above. End-systolic pressures receive a weight of 200: for RV_ESP this is because it is the independent variable of the spectrum, and for LV_ESP because it is one half of the end-systolic transmural load the thesis is measuring. End-diastolic volumes receive 400, the largest per-target weight, because they constrain the coupling with the mesh geometry and must be hit tightly for the boundary conditions that feed the 3D model to be self-consistent. End-diastolic pressures receive 150. The LA pressure target receives 150 to enforce the pre-capillary phenotype (PAWP safely below the 15 mmHg ESC bound). The aortic diastolic target receives 100. The stroke-volume balance between LV and RV, serving as a closed-loop mass conservation check, receives 300. The one-sided barriers on ejection fraction and cardiac index activate only when the simulation falls below the ESC-band floor and carry weights of 400, 300, and 500 respectively; above the floor they contribute zero. Infeasible trials — NaN outputs, solver divergence, pressures outside physical range — return graded penalty values in the 5000–9000 range rather than being pruned outright. The graded penalty gives the optimizer gradient information about where the feasible region ends, which is more useful than the flat wall of a hard constraint.

## From TPE to CMA-ES

The first attempts used the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampler from Optuna {cite}`akiba2019optuna`, which fits separate kernel density estimates to the parameter distributions that lead to good and bad objective values and samples new points from the ratio of the two. TPE is a sensible default and produced workable calibrations for the healthy and mild cases. It struggled as the severity increased. The underlying reason is structural: TPE treats parameters independently, and the parameters of a closed-loop circulation model are not independent. Raising the pulmonary arterial resistance without a compensating adjustment of its compliance moves the RV operating point to an infeasible corner of parameter space; the correct direction in the twenty-two-dimensional search is a correlated move along a particular ridge, and a coordinate-wise sampler has no way to learn this ridge.

Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), introduced by Hansen and Ostermeier {cite}`hansen2001completely`, addresses exactly this limitation. CMA-ES maintains a population of candidate solutions drawn from a multivariate Gaussian and adapts the full covariance matrix of that Gaussian from the selection of successful offspring. As the optimizer learns which directions in parameter space are productive, it stretches the sampling distribution along those directions and contracts it across the unproductive ones, so successive generations concentrate on the ridges that matter. Switching from TPE to the `CmaEsSampler` in Optuna produced convergence improvements of roughly a factor of two to five in the objective value across the spectrum at the same trial budget. The warm-starting story below shows the more dramatic case: for the very_severe configuration, CMA-ES initialized from a random seed reached an objective of 67, while CMA-ES warm-started from the neighbouring severe solution reached 11.4 — a six-fold improvement for the same compute.

The 22 parameters tuned in the final optimization span all of the quantities that matter for biventricular hemodynamics: active and passive elastances, unstressed volumes, contraction and relaxation timings, and the nonlinear stiffening coefficient $k_E$ for both ventricles; the left atrial elastances and mitral valve resistance; the systemic arterial and venous resistances and compliances; the pulmonary arterial and venous resistances and compliances; and a global blood volume offset that acts as an overall preload control. The total is large enough that purely local methods have no chance and the correlated search of CMA-ES is not a luxury but a requirement.

## Warm-Starting the Spectrum

CMA-ES is a local method in the sense that its initial covariance matrix is isotropic, and its early iterations spend effort learning the local geometry of the cost function. For high-RV-pressure configurations, a random initialization landed the optimizer in basins far from any physiologically plausible solution, and tens of generations were spent climbing out. The remedy was to exploit the structure of the spectrum itself. Neighbouring pressure scenarios are not identical, but their good parameter sets are close enough that one can initialize the next. Computationally, this means that the optimum for `moderate_severe` is a good starting point for `severe`, the optimum for `severe` is a good starting point for `very_severe`, and so on.

The pipeline was chained accordingly. The `moderate_severe` case was optimized first from random initialization. Its optimum was then read back, $R_\text{AR,PUL}$ was inflated by roughly fifty percent to push the case into the severe regime, and the result was enqueued as the first CMA-ES trial for severe. The severe optimum seeded very_severe, and very_severe seeded end_stage. Each step inherits a covariance matrix and a good point, so CMA-ES begins its search in a basin that is already close to the global optimum and needs only to adapt locally. The improvement in the objective values across the chain was dramatic: the very_severe configuration improved by a factor of six relative to the random-start baseline, and end_stage — which was essentially unreachable from random initialization — converged to a physiologically clean configuration within a single warm-started run.

Simulation settings matter for the validity of the optimization loop. Each trial runs the 0D model for twenty beats at $\Delta t = 10^{-3}$ s, long enough for the venous compartment transients to decay (the venous time constant $\tau = R_\text{ven} C_\text{ven}$ is of order twenty seconds, which at 75 bpm corresponds to roughly twenty-five beats). Trials shorter than this report spurious drift in the last-beat metrics because the system has not actually reached steady state. Final parameter sets were re-solved at fifty beats before being written to disk.

## Calibration Achievements and Limits

The corrected coupled runs used in the results chapter achieved the main feature the sweep was designed to isolate: RV pressure rises while LV pressure remains comparatively stable. Across the last beat of the 16 simulated cases, peak LV pressure stayed in the range 102 to 111 mmHg, while peak RV pressure rose from about 31 mmHg to about 88 mmHg. The exact labels on the cases should therefore be read as nominal loading labels, not as precise clinical categories. The lowest case sits above the ideal Kovacs-normal systolic pulmonary pressure target, and the highest cases do not reach the full nominal 95 mmHg target in the coupled mechanics run. The important point for the proxy analysis is the achieved pressure path, and that path is used directly in all results computations.

The clearest summary of this calibration is the pressure-volume loop family in {numref}`fig-pv-loops-spectrum`. The figure shows why the sweep is useful for the thesis even though it is not a patient trajectory: the same biventricular geometry is driven through a controlled range of RV pressure loading, while the LV side remains comparatively preserved. This is the hemodynamic stage on which the pressure-strain proxy is tested.

This is also where the old and corrected sweeps differ most clearly. In the earlier handover calibration, LV pressure fell systematically as RV pressure rose. That artificial LV-pressure decline made the transmural pressure collapse partly because the LV side was being pulled down, not only because the RV side was being pushed up. In the corrected sweep, LV pressure is preserved and the transmural reduction is driven mainly by the RV pressure rise. This change is not a small bookkeeping detail: it changes which pressure-strain proxy wins a correlation table for the septum. That is why the results chapter treats the sweep as a loading-path sensitivity test rather than as a single final answer about clinical PAH.

It is worth being clear about what this calibration means. The circulation model is a lumped-parameter engineering abstraction, and its parameters are effective constants with no direct anatomical counterpart. A calibrated pulmonary resistance that is several times the healthy value does not measure any particular small vessel in any particular lung. It says that an effective resistance of that magnitude, embedded in this Windkessel circulation and coupled to this finite-element heart, produces the desired macroscopic pressure loading. The calibration is therefore not a diagnostic statement about disease severity. It provides pressure and volume boundary conditions for a controlled mechanics experiment.

## Patient-Specific Meshes

Patient-specific healthy and PAH meshes are available in the wider project and were part of the motivation for building the calibration machinery. They are not used as the primary evidence in this thesis because the present results already show that pressure-choice conclusions can depend on the loading path. Adding patient-specific geometry without measured and well-constrained hemodynamics would introduce another source of uncertainty: wall thickness, cavity volume, stiffness, contractility, and pressure would all change at once. That extension is still valuable, but it should be treated as a geometry-sensitivity study or as a patient-specific validation study with measured pressures, not as a simple continuation of the fixed-geometry sweep.
