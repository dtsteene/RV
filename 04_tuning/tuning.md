# Calibrating the Circulation Model

## The Parameter Identification Problem

The Regazzoni model contains roughly twenty effective parameters — the contractility and passive stiffness of each chamber, the resistances and compliances of the systemic and pulmonary Windkessel networks, the valve resistances, the activation timings, and the initial blood volume distribution. None of these are directly measurable in a patient; they are engineering constants chosen so that the model's macroscopic output matches clinical observation. Finding values that produce realistic hemodynamics is a coupled, nonconvex inverse problem, and its difficulty is the subject of this chapter.

The coupling is what makes naive iteration fail. Raising systemic vascular resistance lifts mean arterial pressure, which increases LV afterload, which lowers stroke volume, which changes end-diastolic volume, which modifies filling pressure, which alters the next cycle's ejection. In a closed-loop model these perturbations return to their origin on the timescale of a few beats, so adjusting one parameter to fix one target almost always breaks another that was previously acceptable. A further complication is that the Regazzoni model distributes a fixed total blood volume across all compartments according to the local compliance and resistance; ventricular filling pressures emerge from this distribution rather than being prescribed directly. There is no "set LV preload" knob.

## Decoupling EDP and EDV: A Nonlinear EDPVR

Before describing the optimization itself, one structural limitation of the standard Regazzoni formulation had to be resolved. The published model uses a linear time-varying elastance,

$$
P(V, t) = E(t) (V - V_0), \qquad E(t) = E_B + (E_A - E_B) a(t),
$$

where $E_A$ is the active systolic elastance, $E_B$ is the passive diastolic elastance, and $a(t)$ is the activation waveform. In this formulation the end-diastolic pressure and end-diastolic volume are controlled by the same parameter $E_B$: once the unstressed volume $V_0$ is fixed, the diastolic operating point is pinned to the single line $P_\text{ED} = E_B (V_\text{ED} - V_0)$. For a mesh whose geometric cavity volumes are small relative to population averages — the UKB mean biventricular geometry has an LV end-diastolic volume of approximately 111 mL and an RV end-diastolic volume of 77 mL — the optimizer cannot simultaneously match the clinical EDP range of 4–12 mmHg and hit the correct EDV without forcing the passive stiffness too high or too low.

The fix was to replace the passive (diastolic) component of the chamber pressure law with an exponential pressure-volume relationship, following the Klotz EDPVR framework {cite}`klotz2006single` that is standard in lumped-parameter cardiac mechanics and used in models like CircAdapt {cite}`arts2005adaptation`:

$$
P(V, t) = (E_A - E_B) a(t) (V - V_0) + \frac{E_B}{k_E} \bigl(e^{k_E (V - V_0)} - 1\bigr).
$$

The new parameter $k_E$ has units of inverse volume and controls how quickly the chamber stiffens as it approaches its distensibility limit. In the limit $k_E \to 0$ the expression reduces to the original linear model, so the modification is backward-compatible. Near the unstressed volume the passive pressure grows linearly as $E_B (V - V_0)$, keeping the low-pressure filling regime compliant, while at larger volumes the exponential term dominates and prevents overfilling. This decouples the two constraints that the linear model had collapsed onto a single line: the slope at $V_0$ sets the low-pressure filling response and $k_E$ sets the stiffening scale, so EDP and EDV can be matched independently. The modification was applied to the LV and RV chambers only; the thin-walled atria remained linear. The Jacobian used by the stiff ODE integrator had to be updated for the volume-dependent $dP/dV$ when $k_E > 0$, but no other change to the solver was needed.

## The Severity Spectrum and Clinical Targets

Rather than calibrating a single PAH case in isolation, the simulation framework was calibrated across a spectrum of eight disease severities ranging from normal hemodynamics to near-equalization of ventricular pressures. The motivation is twofold. First, a continuous spectrum exposes the mechanism that drives the central result of the thesis: as RV systolic pressure rises, the transmural load $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ across the septum falls toward zero, and the error incurred by the LV-only convention should grow monotonically. A single PAH point can only show that the error exists; a spectrum shows how it scales. Second, calibrating adjacent severities allows warm-starting the optimizer from nearby solutions, which turns out to be essential for the severe cases.

The hemodynamic targets for each severity were derived from the 2022 ESC/ERS guidelines for pulmonary hypertension {cite}`humbert2022esc` and the Kovacs et al. systematic review of normal pulmonary hemodynamics {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. The guidelines define PAH by the triple of mean pulmonary arterial pressure above 20 mmHg, pulmonary vascular resistance above 2 Wood units, and pulmonary arterial wedge pressure at or below 15 mmHg, with the last condition distinguishing the pre-capillary phenotype from post-capillary elevation driven by left heart failure. The spectrum starts from a normal baseline with $\text{sPAP} < 25$ mmHg and $\text{mPAP} \approx 14$ mmHg (Kovacs et al., $n = 1187$), passes through the borderline and mild categories defined by the guideline thresholds, and extends through moderate and severe PAH to an end-stage point where the RV end-systolic pressure approaches that of the LV and the septal transmural pressure is nearly zero. The LV end-systolic target decreases along the spectrum to reflect ventricular interdependence: as RV pressure rises the septum shifts leftward, LV filling is impaired, and systolic pressure falls — the same mechanism that produces the echocardiographic D-sign {cite}`ryan1985echocardiographic,vonk2017relationship`. Pre-capillary character is enforced by a mean left atrial pressure target of 7–9 mmHg, which keeps the pulmonary venous return clear of post-capillary elevation. Ejection fraction floors decline from 55% at healthy to 20% at end-stage, and cardiac output minima fall from 4.0 to 1.5 L/min, reflecting forward failure as the disease progresses.

## Cost Function with Dynamic Weight Relaxation

Each trial of the optimizer runs the 0D model to periodic steady state and extracts last-beat metrics — peak and end-diastolic pressures for both ventricles, aortic diastolic pressure, mean left atrial pressure, end-diastolic volumes, ejection fractions, cardiac output, and the LV–RV stroke volume imbalance that serves as a closed-loop mass conservation check. The cost is a weighted sum of squared relative errors, but with the weights modulated by how close each target already is:

$$
w_\text{eff}(\theta) = \begin{cases}
w_\text{base} \cdot (|\text{err}| / \tau) & |\text{err}| < \tau, \\
w_\text{base} & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

with relaxation threshold $\tau = 5\%$. The effect is that once a target is within five percent of its clinical value, its weight is progressively reduced, freeing the optimizer to focus on the remaining gaps. Without this relaxation, the optimizer oscillates: fixing one target unbalances another that was previously within tolerance, and the cost function stops decreasing. The base weights encode clinical priority. End-systolic pressures receive a weight of 200 because they define the severity classification; end-diastolic volumes receive 400 because they constrain the coupling with the mesh geometry; the LA pressure target receives 150 to enforce the pre-capillary phenotype; and the stroke volume imbalance receives 300 as a hard consistency check. Infeasible trials — NaN outputs, solver divergence, pressures outside physical range — return graded penalty values in the 5000–9000 range rather than being pruned outright. The graded penalty gives the optimizer gradient information about where the feasible region ends, which is more useful than the flat wall of a hard constraint.

## From TPE to CMA-ES

The first attempts used the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampler from Optuna {cite}`akiba2019optuna`, which fits separate kernel density estimates to the parameter distributions that lead to good and bad objective values and samples new points from the ratio of the two. TPE is a sensible default and produced workable calibrations for the healthy and mild cases. It struggled as the severity increased. The underlying reason is structural: TPE treats parameters independently, and the parameters of a closed-loop circulation model are not independent. Raising the pulmonary arterial resistance without a compensating adjustment of its compliance moves the RV operating point to an infeasible corner of parameter space; the correct direction in the twenty-two-dimensional search is a correlated move along a particular ridge, and a coordinate-wise sampler has no way to learn this ridge.

Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), introduced by Hansen and Ostermeier {cite}`hansen2001completely`, addresses exactly this limitation. CMA-ES maintains a population of candidate solutions drawn from a multivariate Gaussian and adapts the full covariance matrix of that Gaussian from the selection of successful offspring. As the optimizer learns which directions in parameter space are productive, it stretches the sampling distribution along those directions and contracts it across the unproductive ones, so successive generations concentrate on the ridges that matter. Switching from TPE to the `CmaEsSampler` in Optuna produced convergence improvements of roughly a factor of two to five in the objective value across the spectrum at the same trial budget. The warm-starting story below shows the more dramatic case: for the very_severe configuration, CMA-ES initialized from a random seed reached an objective of 67, while CMA-ES warm-started from the neighbouring severe solution reached 11.4 — a six-fold improvement for the same compute.

The 22 parameters tuned in the final optimization span all of the quantities that matter for biventricular hemodynamics: active and passive elastances, unstressed volumes, contraction and relaxation timings, and the nonlinear stiffening coefficient $k_E$ for both ventricles; the left atrial elastances and mitral valve resistance; the systemic arterial and venous resistances and compliances; the pulmonary arterial and venous resistances and compliances; and a global blood volume offset that acts as an overall preload control. The total is large enough that purely local methods have no chance and the correlated search of CMA-ES is not a luxury but a requirement.

## Warm-Starting the Spectrum

CMA-ES is a local method in the sense that its initial covariance matrix is isotropic, and its early iterations spend effort learning the local geometry of the cost function. For severe PAH configurations, a random initialization landed the optimizer in basins far from any physiologically plausible solution, and tens of generations were spent climbing out. The remedy was to exploit the structure of the spectrum itself. Physiologically, PAH worsens as pulmonary vascular resistance rises, and most other parameters adapt continuously along the disease trajectory. Computationally, this means that the optimum for `moderate_severe` is a good starting point for `severe`, the optimum for `severe` is a good starting point for `very_severe`, and so on.

The pipeline was chained accordingly. The `moderate_severe` case was optimized first from random initialization. Its optimum was then read back, $R_\text{AR,PUL}$ was inflated by roughly fifty percent to push the case into the severe regime, and the result was enqueued as the first CMA-ES trial for severe. The severe optimum seeded very_severe, and very_severe seeded end_stage. Each step inherits a covariance matrix and a good point, so CMA-ES begins its search in a basin that is already close to the global optimum and needs only to adapt locally. The improvement in the objective values across the chain was dramatic: the very_severe configuration improved by a factor of six relative to the random-start baseline, and end_stage — which was essentially unreachable from random initialization — converged to a physiologically clean configuration within a single warm-started run.

Simulation settings matter for the validity of the optimization loop. Each trial runs the 0D model for twenty beats at $\Delta t = 10^{-3}$ s, long enough for the venous compartment transients to decay (the venous time constant $\tau = R_\text{ven} C_\text{ven}$ is of order twenty seconds, which at 75 bpm corresponds to roughly twenty-five beats). Trials shorter than this report spurious drift in the last-beat metrics because the system has not actually reached steady state. Final parameter sets were re-solved at fifty beats before being written to disk.

## Calibration Achievements and Limits

The calibrated parameters reproduce the clinical targets to within a few percent on the primary hemodynamic variables across the full spectrum, and the achieved hemodynamics collapse cleanly against the severity axis: the transmural pressure $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ at end-systole correlates with RV end-systolic pressure at $r = -0.989$, the LV peak pressure tracks the ventricular interdependence expected from D-sign physiology at $r = -0.877$, and cardiac output decreases with $r = -0.837$ as forward failure progresses. The full table of achieved values is given in the results chapter, where it forms the starting point for the 3D simulations.

It is worth being clear about what this calibration means. The circulation model is a lumped-parameter engineering abstraction, and its parameters are effective constants with no direct anatomical counterpart. A calibrated pulmonary resistance that is five times the healthy value does not measure any particular small vessel in any particular lung; it says that an effective resistance of that magnitude, embedded in a two-compartment Windkessel with the other parameters as given, produces macroscopic hemodynamics consistent with the clinical targets. The calibration is therefore not a diagnostic statement about disease severity. What it does provide is pressure and volume boundary conditions for the three-dimensional finite element model that are consistent with the observed disease state. The mechanical environment of the septal myocardium — the pressure differential across it, the valve timing, the peak ventricular pressures — matches the hemodynamic targets that define the severity category, and the conclusions about septal work estimation drawn from the 3D simulations can therefore be attributed to a recognizable hemodynamic regime rather than to a generic elevated-pressure scenario.

## The Patient-Specific Case

The patient-specific PAH geometry was handled with the same framework but with targets drawn from the individual patient's measured hemodynamics rather than from the guideline-based spectrum. The parameter set was smaller — pulmonary arterial resistance, pulmonary compliance, RV contractility, RV stiffness, systemic resistance, and a blood volume offset — because the remaining parameters could be held at their healthy values without violating the patient's measurements. The LA pressure target again received a large weight to enforce pre-capillary character: without this, the optimizer can reach a correct RV systolic pressure by inflating LV filling pressure and back-pressuring the pulmonary circulation, producing a PAH-like waveform for the wrong physiological reason. The final parameters matched the patient's measured hemodynamics to within ten to fifteen percent on all recorded quantities.
