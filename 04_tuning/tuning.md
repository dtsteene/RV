# Calibrating the Circulation Model

## The Parameter Identification Problem

The zero-dimensional circulation model of Regazzoni et al. {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac` is governed by roughly fifteen parameters: the contractility and passive stiffness of each of the four cardiac chambers, the resistances and compliances of the systemic and pulmonary Windkessel networks, and the initial blood volume distribution. These parameters are not directly measurable quantities. They are effective constants in an engineering model, chosen to make the model reproduce the physiological behavior of the cardiovascular system at the macroscopic level. Finding values that give rise to the correct hemodynamics is a parameter identification problem — and it is one that is considerably more difficult than it might initially appear.

The difficulty is that the system is coupled. Every parameter influences multiple output quantities, and no output quantity is controlled by a single parameter. Increasing systemic vascular resistance raises mean arterial pressure, which increases LV afterload, which reduces stroke volume, which changes the LV end-diastolic volume, which affects filling pressure, which modifies the next cycle's ejection. The coupling is particularly strong in a closed-loop model, where changes in one compartment propagate around the circuit and return to their origin on the timescale of a few beats. Naive iterative adjustment of individual parameters rarely converges, because fixing one target quantity disrupts others that were previously acceptable.

A further complication arises from the total blood volume. The Regazzoni model distributes a fixed blood volume across all compartments according to the compliance and resistance values; the filling pressures of the ventricles, and therefore their end-diastolic volumes, emerge from this distribution rather than being prescribed directly. The model does not have a "set LV preload" control. To adjust ventricular filling pressure, one must modify the venous compliance or the initial pressure in the systemic venous reservoir, both of which also affect multiple other quantities.

Given these interdependencies, parameter identification requires numerical optimization: a systematic search over the parameter space that minimizes a scalar objective encoding how far the model output is from the clinical targets.

## Clinical Targets and the Cost Function

The clinical targets for the healthy baseline correspond to the hemodynamics of a resting adult at 75 beats per minute. The primary targets are the LV end-systolic pressure of approximately 120 mmHg, the LV end-diastolic pressure of approximately 10 mmHg, a stroke volume of 70 mL, the RV end-systolic pressure of approximately 25 mmHg, the RV end-diastolic pressure of 4 mmHg, and an aortic diastolic pressure of 80 mmHg. Additional soft constraints on the mean left atrial pressure (8–10 mmHg), the LV end-diastolic volume (150 mL), and the RV end-diastolic volume (150 mL) prevent the optimizer from finding hemodynamically degenerate solutions — for example, a configuration where stroke volume is correct but achieved through an unrealistically large and weakly contracting ventricle.

The cost function is a weighted sum of squared relative errors:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_i w_i \left( \frac{m_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - t_i}{t_i} \right)^2,
$$

where $m_i(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the $i$-th simulated metric produced by running the model with parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to steady state, $t_i$ is the corresponding target value, and $w_i$ is a weight that reflects the relative clinical importance of each quantity and the numerical sensitivity of the cost function. Stroke volume and LV end-diastolic pressure receive higher weights because they are the most diagnostically important quantities and because they are the most sensitive indicators of whether the heart is correctly filled. The aortic diastolic pressure receives an asymmetric barrier treatment: a large penalty is applied if it falls below 70 mmHg, reflecting the fact that numerical problems arise when arterial pressure drops too low, while the cost for exceeding the target transitions to a normal quadratic term above this threshold. Each evaluation of the cost function requires running the model for ten beats to reach hemodynamic steady state and extracting the last-beat metrics from the pressure and volume waveforms.

## Optimization Strategy: From Nelder-Mead to Differential Evolution

The optimization was carried out in stages of increasing sophistication, driven by the practical observation that simpler methods had difficulty with the nonconvexity and coupling of the problem.

The first approach was the Nelder-Mead simplex method, a derivative-free local optimizer that maintains a simplex of parameter points and iteratively deforms it toward lower cost by reflection, expansion, and contraction operations. Nelder-Mead converges reliably for smooth, convex cost functions, and it was the natural starting point given the moderate parameter dimensionality (seven to nine free parameters) and the cost of each function evaluation (approximately one to two seconds for a ten-beat 0D simulation). For the healthy baseline, the method found solutions that reproduced some targets acceptably but struggled with others — in particular, the simultaneous satisfaction of the stroke volume and filling pressure constraints, which are coupled through the total blood volume in a way that the local simplex geometry does not capture well.

The second approach replaced the global exploration phase with differential evolution, a population-based stochastic algorithm that maintains a population of parameter vectors and evolves them through crossover and mutation operations. Unlike Nelder-Mead, differential evolution explores the parameter space globally rather than locally, making it substantially more robust to the presence of multiple local minima. The algorithm was run with a population of ten individuals using the `best1bin` mutation strategy and parallelized across all available CPU cores, with each population member evaluated in a separate subprocess. The global search was run for fifteen generations, after which the best individual was passed to Nelder-Mead for local refinement. This two-stage strategy proved considerably more reliable than single-stage Nelder-Mead and produced the calibrated healthy baseline used in the final simulations. The achieved targets for the healthy baseline case were within 5–10% of the clinical targets across all quantities.

For the pulmonary arterial hypertension case, the parameter identification was substantially more involved. PAH is not simply a matter of increasing pulmonary vascular resistance; the disease involves a constellation of physiological changes that interact in ways that constrain what combinations of parameters are physically admissible. The critical constraint for pre-capillary PAH — the group of patients with pulmonary vascular disease rather than left heart failure — is that the left atrial pressure must remain normal (below 15 mmHg). This requirement defines the boundary between Group 1 PAH, in which the elevated pulmonary pressure is caused by pulmonary vascular disease, and heart failure with elevated LV filling pressures, which drives up pulmonary pressure through a different mechanism entirely. If the optimizer is allowed to simply minimize pulmonary arterial pressure error, it can cheat by reducing LV compliance or increasing systemic venous pressure, producing elevated pulmonary pressure through back-pressure rather than through increased vascular resistance. This would generate a correct RV pressure waveform for the wrong physiological reason.

To enforce the pre-capillary constraint, the LA pressure term in the cost function was given a weight approximately three times larger than the other terms:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{PAH} = 40 \left(\frac{p_\text{RV,sys} - 60}{60}\right)^2 + 20 \left(\frac{p_\text{RV,dia} - 10}{10}\right)^2 + 50 \left(\frac{\bar{p}_\text{LA} - 9}{9}\right)^2 + \cdots
$$

The large coefficient on the LA pressure term functioned as a soft constraint that penalized solutions with elevated LA pressure far more severely than solutions that missed secondary targets. In practice, this forced the optimizer toward physiologically correct PAH configurations in which the elevated pulmonary pressure is caused by increased pulmonary resistance rather than by back-pressure from the left side.

The PAH optimization tuned six parameters: the pulmonary arterial resistance (the primary cause of PAH), the pulmonary arterial compliance (reduced in PAH due to vascular remodeling), the RV contractility (increased as the RV hypertrophies to compensate for the elevated afterload), the RV passive stiffness (increased by fibrosis in the chronically loaded RV), the systemic arterial resistance (modestly elevated in PAH due to compensatory vasoconstriction), and a total volume offset that captures the fluid retention characteristic of advanced right heart failure. The targets were set to match the clinical hemodynamics of the PAH patient whose geometry was used in the simulation: RV end-systolic pressure of 60 mmHg, RV end-diastolic pressure of 10 mmHg, stroke volume of 55 mL, LV end-systolic pressure of 110 mmHg, aortic diastolic pressure of 75 mmHg, mean LA pressure of 9 mmHg, and RV end-diastolic volume of 190 mL.

The most effective approach for both cases proved to be Bayesian optimization via the Optuna framework {cite}`akiba2019optuna` with the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampler. Bayesian optimization uses the history of past evaluations to build a probabilistic surrogate model of the cost function, and proposes new evaluation points by maximizing an acquisition function that balances exploration of uncertain regions against exploitation of known-good areas. Unlike differential evolution, which maintains a fixed population and evolves it through mutation and crossover, the TPE sampler builds separate kernel density estimates of the parameter distributions that lead to good and bad objective values, and samples new points from the distribution that maximizes the expected improvement.

The healthy baseline optimization used 2,000 Optuna trials with parallel evaluation across all available CPU cores, producing a well-explored cost function landscape. The parameter space for the healthy case included fifteen free parameters: the contractility and stiffness of both ventricles and the left atrium, the LV unstressed volume and contraction/relaxation timing, the mitral valve resistance, the systemic and pulmonary resistances and compliances, the pulmonary venous compliance, and a total blood volume offset. A timing constraint was enforced: the sum of contraction onset, contraction duration, and relaxation duration could not exceed the beat period, ensuring that the activation waveform remained physiologically consistent. Trials that violated this constraint or that caused the circulation solver to diverge were pruned automatically. The TPE sampler was initialized with 100 random startup trials before switching to the surrogate-guided search.

The PAH optimization used the same framework with six parameters focused on the disease-specific changes: pulmonary arterial resistance, pulmonary compliance, RV contractility, RV stiffness, systemic resistance, and blood volume. The pre-capillary LA pressure constraint received the heaviest weight in the cost function. The final parameters for both cases produced hemodynamics matching the clinical targets to within 10–15% on all quantities.

## Discussion of the Calibration Approach

It is worth acknowledging what this calibration achieves and what it does not. The circulation model is a zero-dimensional system whose parameters are effective engineering constants with no direct physiological counterpart. When we say that the calibrated pulmonary resistance is five times the healthy value, this does not mean that the small vessel resistance in the patient's lungs is exactly five times normal; it means that an effective resistance parameter of that magnitude in the lumped model produces the right macroscopic hemodynamics. The calibration is therefore not a measurement of disease severity; it is a parameter estimation that produces boundary conditions consistent with the observed hemodynamics.

What the calibration does achieve is that the pressure boundary conditions driving the three-dimensional finite element model are physiologically consistent with the observed disease state. The LV and RV pressure waveforms that result from the coupled simulation match the clinical target hemodynamics, which means that the mechanical environment of the septal myocardium — the pressure differential across it during systole, the timing of valve events, the peak ventricular pressures — is consistent with the actual patient. The conclusions about septal work estimation drawn from these simulations therefore apply to a hemodynamic state that resembles the actual disease, not merely a generic elevated-RV-pressure scenario.
