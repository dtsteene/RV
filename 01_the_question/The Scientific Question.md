# The Scientific Question

The standard approach to quantifying ventricular mechanical work relies on the pressure-volume relationship. Over a complete cardiac cycle, the area enclosed by the pressure-volume loop gives the net external work done by the ventricle on the blood it ejects:

$$
W_\text{ext} = \oint P \, dV.
$$

This metric has a long and validated history in left ventricular physiology, grounded in the observation by Suga that the PV-loop area correlates closely with myocardial oxygen consumption {cite}`suga1979total`. It is measurable invasively through catheterization and, increasingly, can be approximated non-invasively using echocardiographic strain measurements combined with pressure estimates from Doppler imaging — a technique known as myocardial work imaging or the pressure-strain loop method {cite}`russell2012novel`. The clinical pressure-strain index computes the area of a loop formed by plotting chamber pressure against longitudinal fiber strain, approximating the regional contribution of each myocardial segment to the total stroke work.

There is good reason to use pressure as a surrogate for stress: myocardial stress cannot be measured directly in an intact heart {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`, and chamber pressure is the only continuous mechanical measurement routinely available. For the left ventricle, the approximation is reasonable — pressure varies more or less uniformly across the LV wall under normal conditions, and the correlation between pressure-strain loop area and true fiber stress-strain work has been examined computationally by Finsberg et al. using personalized finite element models {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`. That study found that, while the pressure-strain proxy severely underestimates the absolute magnitude of myocardial work (by roughly an order of magnitude), it captures the same trends in regional efficiency and correctly identifies dysfunctional segments in patients with left bundle branch block. The full tensor stress-strain integral, by contrast, agreed quantitatively with the PV-loop stroke work, falling within a few percent for both a healthy volunteer and an LBBB patient.

For the right ventricle, the same logic is applied routinely in clinical practice and increasingly in research. But the anatomical situation is fundamentally different from the LV. The two ventricles do not operate in separate chambers; they share a wall. The interventricular septum forms the medial boundary of the LV cavity and simultaneously the medial boundary of the RV cavity, meaning it is mechanically loaded from both sides at once. This shared boundary creates a situation in which the right ventricular pressure cannot be the only relevant mechanical load on the septal tissue.

The mechanical argument runs as follows. During systole in a healthy heart, the left ventricle generates a peak pressure of approximately 120 mmHg, while the right ventricle generates roughly 25 mmHg {cite}`bastos2020invasive`. The net pressure across the septum in the healthy case is therefore strongly biased toward the LV side, and the septum deforms primarily in response to LV pressure, bowing into the right ventricular cavity. From the perspective of energy accounting, this means the left ventricle is performing work that results in a reduction of the RV cavity volume — a contribution that appears in the RV pressure-volume loop as stroke volume but was not generated by the RV free wall myocardium alone. Standard PV-loop analysis cannot distinguish this contribution from work done by the RV itself.

In pulmonary arterial hypertension, the right ventricular systolic pressure rises progressively. When RV systolic pressure approaches LV systolic pressure, the net transmural load across the septum diminishes, and the septum flattens — the echocardiographic D-sign familiar to any cardiologist who has seen severe RV pressure overload {cite}`vonk2013right`. When, in severe cases, RV pressure transiently exceeds LV pressure, the septum can bow into the left ventricular cavity, actively impeding LV filling. In this state, the assumption that LV pressure drives septal mechanics breaks down entirely: the RV pressure is now the dominant force on the RV face of the septum, actively resisting the septal motion generated by the LV, and the mechanics of the septal tissue are governed by the net pressure difference across the wall rather than either pressure in isolation.

This has a direct implication for myocardial work imaging in PAH patients. If a clinician estimates septal myocardial work using the pressure-strain loop with LV pressure as the driving pressure, they are assuming that the full LV pressure is acting on the septal tissue in the direction of its shortening. But the RV pressure on the other face of the septum is opposing that shortening. The net mechanical load on the septum is more closely related to the transmural pressure $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ than to $P_\text{LV}$ alone. Using LV pressure will therefore overestimate the mechanical work performed by the septal myocardium when RV pressure is elevated — precisely the pathological state of greatest clinical interest.

Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` explicitly identified this as a limitation of their study, noting that "modeling the presence of a right ventricle would influence the regional work calculations, and should be investigated in future studies." The present thesis is that investigation. We extend the framework from left ventricular to biventricular geometry, introduce a closed-loop circulatory model to produce physiologically realistic pressure boundary conditions for both chambers simultaneously, and use the resulting stress and strain fields to compare several pressure definitions as proxies for septal mechanical work.

The ground-truth quantity against which we compare these proxies is the internal mechanical work computed directly from the stress and strain tensor fields of the finite element simulation. Following the formulation of Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`, the total internal work in a myocardial region $\Omega_j$ over a cardiac cycle is

$$
W_\text{int}[\Omega_j] = \int_0^T \int_{\Omega_j} \mathbf{S}(t, \mathbf{X}) : \dot{\mathbf{E}}(t, \mathbf{X}) \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor, $\mathbf{E}$ is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor, and the colon denotes the double contraction $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} = S_{ij} \dot{E}_{ij}$. In discrete form, using the trapezoidal approximation across $N$ time steps, this becomes

$$
W_\text{int}[\Omega_j] \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N} \int_{\Omega_j} \bar{\mathbf{S}}(t_i, \mathbf{X}) : d\mathbf{E}(t_i, \mathbf{X}) \, d\mathbf{X},
$$

where $\bar{\mathbf{S}}(t_i) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{S}(t_i) + \mathbf{S}(t_{i-1}))$ is the average stress and $d\mathbf{E}(t_i) = \mathbf{E}(t_i) - \mathbf{E}(t_{i-1})$ is the strain increment at step $i$ {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`. The fiber-direction component of this work — the work done specifically by normal stress along the fiber axis — is obtained by replacing the full tensors with their fiber-direction projections, $S_{ff} = \mathbf{S} : (\mathbf{f}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0)$ and $dE_{ff} = d\mathbf{E} : (\mathbf{f}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0)$.

The proxy we compare against this ground truth is inspired by the clinical pressure-strain index {cite}`russell2012novel`. For a given region, the pressure-strain work is approximated as

$$
W_\text{PS}[\Omega_j] \approx \bar{V}_j \sum_{i=1}^{N} \bar{P}(t_i) \, dE_{ff,j}(t_i),
$$

where $\bar{V}_j$ is the reference volume of the region, $dE_{ff,j}$ is the volume-averaged fiber strain increment in that region, and $P$ is a pressure assigned to represent the mechanical load. For the LV free wall, $P = P_\text{LV}$ is the natural choice. For the septum, the question of which pressure to use is the hypothesis under investigation. We compare three choices: $P_\text{LV}$, $P_\text{RV}$, and the transmural pressure $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$.

The scientific question can then be stated precisely: for the interventricular septum, which of these three pressure definitions produces a pressure-strain work estimate $W_\text{PS}$ that best approximates the ground-truth internal work $W_\text{int}$, and does the answer depend on whether the patient is healthy or has pulmonary arterial hypertension?

The hypothesis motivating the study is that in healthy physiology all three proxies should perform similarly — since $P_\text{RV}$ is small relative to $P_\text{LV}$, the transmural pressure is nearly identical to the LV pressure. In the PAH case, however, where $P_\text{RV}$ is substantially elevated, the transmural pressure should outperform the LV pressure proxy because it accounts for the opposing mechanical load that RV pressure places on the septal tissue. This hypothesis connects directly to the earlier finding of Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` that the fiber stress-strain integral is a better measure of absolute work than the pressure-strain proxy, and extends their framework to the biventricular case where the choice of pressure proxy becomes genuinely ambiguous.

Answering this question requires a model that simultaneously produces realistic cavity pressures and volume dynamics — which a stand-alone 0D circulation model can provide — and resolves the stress and strain fields within the septal tissue — which only a spatially resolved 3D model can achieve. The coupled finite element and circulation framework described in the following chapter is built specifically to provide both.
