# RV Work vs. PV-Loops

The primary goal of this project is to quantify when and by how much Right Ventricular (RV) pressure–volume (PV) loop stroke work diverges from the actual mechanical work generated by the RV myocardium. This is motivated by principle of systolic vectricular independence: that the Right Ventricle (RV) is not an independent pump; it is often a passenger on the Left Ventricle's (LV) powerful ride. This is a consiquense of the heart geometry, the RV shares a dynamic common boundary (the septum) with the LV, creating complex stress distrobutions that standard hemodynamics can not capture.

:::{admonition} The Problem with PV Loops
:class: warning

In clinical practice and experimental physiology, the area inside a pressure-volume loop is the gold standard for measuring ventricular stroke work:

$$
W_{ext} = \oint P \ dV
$$

For the left ventricle (LV), this metric works exceptionally well. However, the right ventricle is not an isolated chamber. It shares a wall—the interventricular septum—with the high-pressure LV, and both are enclosed within the pericardium.
:::

### The "Hidden Hero" Hypothesis

We hypothesize that standard PV-loop analysis systematically misrepresents RV function because it accounts only for external hydraulic work (work done on the blood) while missing the complex energy transfer that occurs across the septum.

Specifically, we aim to determine if the RV's "effective workload" is significantly offloaded (or loaded) by the LV via the septum, and how disease states like pulmonary hypertension or heart failure alter this relationship.
