# Implementation and Challenges

The chapters preceding this one describe the model as it exists in its final form. That description is necessarily clean: the equations follow from one another logically, the algorithmic choices appear motivated, and the implementation appears coherent. The actual process of building the simulation was considerably messier, and the purpose of this chapter is to document that process honestly. Each challenge described here required substantive investigation — careful reasoning about the underlying physics and numerics and nontrivial changes to the code — and together they represent a significant portion of the actual work. They are documented in full because understanding them is necessary to evaluate the results critically, and because the failure modes they illustrate are common enough to be relevant to anyone building similar coupled simulations.

## Prestressing: Starting in a Loaded State

The mesh generated by `cardiac-geometries` represents the biventricular heart in a particular geometric shape, but that shape does not correspond to the stress-free reference configuration that the constitutive model assumes. The real heart at end-diastole is already under pressure: the left ventricular end-diastolic pressure is typically 8–12 mmHg and the right ventricular end-diastolic pressure 4–8 mmHg. If we simply take the mesh as the reference configuration and apply these pressures, we are implicitly assuming that the mesh represents the unloaded state — which it does not. The result is that the deformation computed during the simulation is measured relative to a configuration that is already stressed, introducing errors in the stress field that propagate through the entire cycle.

The correct approach is to find the stress-free reference configuration that, when loaded with the end-diastolic pressures, produces exactly the mesh geometry. This is an inverse problem: given a deformed shape and applied loads, find the undeformed shape. Several methods for solving this problem exist in the computational biomechanics literature {cite}`gee2010computational,bols2013computational`; the method we apply is the backward displacement method of Bols et al. {cite}`bols2013computational`, which formulates the problem as a fixed-point iteration. Starting from the mesh geometry as an initial guess for the reference configuration, the algorithm applies the end-diastolic pressures and solves the forward problem to find the resulting deformation. Defining the iterate $\mathbf{X}_{n+1} = \mathbf{X}^\mathscr{I} - \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{X}_n)$, where $\mathbf{X}^\mathscr{I}$ is the image-based (loaded) geometry and $\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{X})$ is the displacement field computed by applying the end-diastolic pressure to the geometry with coordinates $\mathbf{X}$, the sequence converges to the unloaded reference by the Banach fixed-point theorem, provided the deformation gradient has a spectral radius below unity {cite}`bols2013computational`. The computed displacement is then used to update the reference coordinates, shifting them in a direction that would bring the deformed configuration closer to the original mesh. This process is repeated until the deformed configuration under the applied pressures matches the mesh geometry to within a specified tolerance.

The prestressing step is numerically demanding. The inverse problem does not have the same convexity properties as the forward problem, and the iteration can diverge if the initial guess for the reference configuration is poor or if the applied pressures are large relative to the material stiffness. In practice, convergence required approximately 10–15 iterations for the geometries used here, with the end-diastolic pressures applied gradually over several sub-steps to maintain stability. The boundary conditions during prestressing must be handled carefully: the same Robin springs used in the main simulation are present during the inverse solve, but their reference positions must be updated consistently with the reference coordinates so that they correctly represent the unloaded state.

Once the stress-free reference configuration is found, the simulation proper begins from this configuration. The heart deforms under end-diastolic pressures, recovering the original mesh geometry, and then continues through systole and diastole driven by the coupled 0D model. This sequence ensures that the stress and strain computed throughout the cycle are referenced to a genuinely stress-free state, and that the constitutive model is being evaluated in the regime for which its parameters were identified.

## A Stress Bug in the Compressible Formulation

The most consequential implementation problem encountered during this project was a bug in how the fenicsx-pulse library computed stresses in the compressible material formulation. It was consequential not because it was difficult to fix once identified, but because it was difficult to identify, and because its symptoms were physically plausible enough that the results it produced could have been accepted without scrutiny.

The Holzapfel-Ogden strain energy function depends on the full Green-Lagrange strain tensor $\mathbf{E}$, which includes both a volumetric component — associated with the change in volume of the element — and a deviatoric component — associated with shape change at constant volume. In a nearly incompressible material, the volumetric strains are small (because $J \approx 1$), but they are not negligible, and they contribute to the stress through the volumetric penalty term $\Psi_\text{vol}$. The deviatoric strains contribute through the isotropic and anisotropic terms.

The bug was that in the compressible implementation, the stress computation was receiving only the deviatoric component of the strain rather than the full strain. This had the effect of systematically underestimating the stress, because the volumetric contribution to the Piola-Kirchhoff stress was being dropped. The magnitude of the error depended on how far the material was from perfect incompressibility — in the nearly incompressible limit with $\kappa = 10$ kPa, the volumetric strains are small but the pressure they generate is not, and the missing contribution was significant.

The symptoms were subtle. The simulation ran to completion without numerical warnings. The pressure-volume loops had the correct qualitative shape — a rapid rise during isovolumic contraction, a roughly rectangular ejection plateau, a rapid fall during isovolumic relaxation, and a slow rise during filling. The ejection fraction was plausible. What betrayed the problem was the stress magnitude: when we extracted mean fiber stresses from the simulation results and compared them against published values for cardiac tissue at comparable deformation levels, the simulated values were substantially too low. This prompted a systematic check of the stress computation chain, which eventually identified the deviatoric-only issue. The fix required modifying the library code to pass the full strain tensor to the stress calculation routines, after which the stress magnitudes came into the expected range and the pressure-volume loops remained qualitatively unchanged but with corrected energy budgets.

The lesson from this is general: in computational mechanics, plausible results are not the same as correct results. The validation of a cardiac simulation must include checks of field-level quantities — stress magnitudes, strain rates, energy densities — against physiologically expected values, not only of integrated quantities like stroke volume or ejection fraction. Integrated quantities are relatively insensitive to the stress magnitude because they are driven by the displacement field, which is determined primarily by the volume constraint rather than the constitutive law.

## Metrics Calculation and the DG1 Projection Problem

The internal mechanical work is computed by integrating the double contraction $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ over the myocardial volume and over time. Both $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ are computed at the quadrature points of the finite element mesh, which is where the constitutive law is evaluated during the Newton solve. Storing these fields for post-processing requires projecting them from the quadrature space onto a function space that can be written to disk and later read for analysis.

The natural choice for a projection target is a first-order discontinuous Galerkin (DG1) space, which allows discontinuities between elements (appropriate for stress, which is not required to be continuous across element boundaries in standard finite elements) while providing linear variation within each element. This projection worked without numerical complaints and produced smooth-looking stress and strain visualizations. The problem appeared only when we attempted to compute the integrated work from the projected fields: the result was approximately three times smaller than the external work $\oint P \, dV$, which should provide an upper bound on the internal work in the absence of significant energy dissipation.

An energy deficit of this magnitude was alarming. It suggested either that a large fraction of the external work was being absorbed somewhere in the simulation — by the boundary spring conditions, perhaps — or that there was a systematic error in the work calculation. We tested the boundary spring hypothesis by reducing the spring stiffness by a factor of two and rerunning the simulation; the resulting change in the integrated work was less than 0.3%, making this explanation untenable. The error had to be in the work computation itself.

The root cause turned out to be the DG1 projection. Near the boundaries between anatomical regions — particularly at the LV-septum and septum-RV interfaces — the stress and strain fields have steep gradients, because the material properties and activation levels change discontinuously at these boundaries. When the DG1 projection attempts to fit linear polynomials across elements that straddle these interfaces, the resulting nodal values can exhibit spurious oscillations: the field overshoots on one side and undershoots on the other, with oscillations that can be large in magnitude relative to the local value. When the work integrand $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ is formed from two oscillating fields and integrated over a thin region like the septum, contributions with opposite signs can cancel, producing an integral that is much smaller than the true value.

Switching to a piecewise-constant DG0 projection eliminated the oscillations entirely. In DG0, each element is assigned a single average value, so there is no room for spurious nodal oscillations. The trade-off is that local spatial variation within each element is lost, which reduces the accuracy of the projected field for pointwise comparisons. For integrated quantities over anatomical regions, however, DG0 is actually more accurate than DG1 in the presence of sharp interface gradients, because the averaging operation smooths out the oscillations rather than representing them faithfully. After switching to DG0 projections, the computed internal work increased substantially and came into a more physically consistent ratio with the external work.

It is worth being precise about what "more physically consistent" means here, because the ratio is still not exactly one. The external PV-loop work and the internal stress-strain work are not the same quantity even in a perfect computation, and their ratio reflects genuine physics: energy absorbed by the boundary springs, the distinction between fiber work and cross-fiber deformation, and the different reference frames of the two calculations. The energy budget is discussed in detail in the results chapter.

## Calibrating the Heart Rate

The 0D circulation model of Regazzoni et al. was originally calibrated at a heart rate somewhat different from our target of 75 beats per minute. Adapting the model to the correct rate required adjusting not only the cycle length but the activation timing parameters of the four cardiac chambers, the duration of the isovolumic phases, and the time constants of the pressure recovery dynamics. A naive scaling of all time parameters by the ratio of cycle lengths produced reasonable hemodynamics but distorted the shapes of the pressure waveforms — in particular, the isovolumic contraction and relaxation phases, which are governed by the material stiffness and valve dynamics rather than purely by timing, did not scale linearly with the cycle length.

The adjustment was performed iteratively by comparing the simulated pressure waveforms against the clinical targets for a resting 75 bpm adult: LV peak pressure approximately 120 mmHg, RV peak pressure approximately 25 mmHg, aortic mean pressure approximately 95 mmHg, and stroke volumes consistent with a cardiac output of around 5 L/min. The activation timing in the ventricular model — the contraction phase duration $T_C$ and the relaxation phase duration $T_R$ in the Blanco model — was adjusted first, followed by the compliance and resistance parameters of the Windkessel circuits to achieve the correct diastolic pressure decay shape. The process required roughly a dozen simulation runs and manual inspection of the resulting waveforms before the outputs were within acceptable range of the clinical targets.

For the pulmonary arterial hypertension case, the adjustments were substantially more extensive. PAH is characterized by elevated pulmonary vascular resistance, which in the model corresponds to an increase in the distal resistance of the pulmonary Windkessel. But matching the clinical hemodynamics of PAH required adjusting multiple parameters simultaneously — pulmonary resistance, pulmonary compliance, the RV activation amplitude, and the RV material parameters — because the disease affects not only the vascular load but also the structure and function of the right ventricular myocardium. The optimization of these parameters is described in the following chapter.
