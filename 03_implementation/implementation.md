# Implementation and Challenges

The previous chapter describes the model as a set of equations. This chapter describes the less polished but equally important part of the work: making those equations produce trustworthy stress, strain, and work fields in a large-deformation biventricular simulation. The details matter because the thesis does not only use pressure-volume loops as outputs; it uses the full tensor work density as the reference against which the clinical pressure-longitudinal-strain proxy is judged.

## Prestressing: Starting in a Loaded State

The mesh generated by `cardiac-geometries` represents the biventricular heart in a particular geometric shape, but that shape does not correspond to the stress-free reference configuration that the constitutive model assumes. The real heart at end-diastole is already under pressure: the left ventricular end-diastolic pressure is typically 8–12 mmHg and the right ventricular end-diastolic pressure 4–8 mmHg. If we simply take the mesh as the reference configuration and apply these pressures, we are implicitly assuming that the mesh represents the unloaded state — which it does not. The result is that the deformation computed during the simulation is measured relative to a configuration that is already stressed, introducing errors in the stress field that propagate through the entire cycle.

The correct approach is to find the stress-free reference configuration that, when loaded with the end-diastolic pressures, produces exactly the mesh geometry. This is an inverse problem: given a deformed shape and applied loads, find the undeformed shape. The method we apply is the backward displacement method of Bols et al. {cite}`bols2013computational`, which formulates the problem as a fixed-point iteration. Starting from the mesh geometry as an initial guess for the reference configuration, the algorithm applies the end-diastolic pressures and solves the forward problem to find the resulting deformation. Defining the iterate $\mathbf{X}_{n+1} = \mathbf{X}^\mathscr{I} - \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{X}_n)$, where $\mathbf{X}^\mathscr{I}$ is the image-based (loaded) geometry and $\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{X})$ is the displacement field computed by applying the end-diastolic pressure to the geometry with coordinates $\mathbf{X}$, the sequence converges to the unloaded reference by the Banach fixed-point theorem, provided the deformation gradient has a spectral radius below unity {cite}`bols2013computational`. The computed displacement is then used to update the reference coordinates, shifting them in a direction that would bring the deformed configuration closer to the original mesh. This process is repeated until the deformed configuration under the applied pressures matches the mesh geometry to within a specified tolerance.

The prestressing step is numerically demanding. The inverse problem does not have the same convexity properties as the forward problem, and the iteration can diverge if the initial guess for the reference configuration is poor or if the applied pressures are large relative to the material stiffness. In practice, convergence required approximately 10–15 iterations for the geometries used here, with the end-diastolic pressures applied gradually over several sub-steps to maintain stability. The boundary conditions during prestressing must be handled carefully: the same Robin springs used in the main simulation are present during the inverse solve, but their reference positions must be updated consistently with the reference coordinates so that they correctly represent the unloaded state.

Once the stress-free reference configuration is found, the simulation proper begins from this configuration. The heart deforms under end-diastolic pressures, recovering the original mesh geometry, and then continues through systole and diastole driven by the coupled 0D model. This sequence ensures that the stress and strain computed throughout the cycle are referenced to a genuinely stress-free state, and that the constitutive model is being evaluated in the regime for which its parameters were identified.

## A Stress Bug in the Compressible Formulation

The most consequential implementation problem encountered during this project was a bug in how the fenicsx-pulse library computed stresses in the compressible material formulation. It was consequential not because it was difficult to fix once identified, but because it was difficult to identify, and because its symptoms were physically plausible enough that the results it produced could have been accepted without scrutiny.

The Holzapfel-Ogden strain energy function depends on the full Green-Lagrange strain tensor $\mathbf{E}$, which includes both a volumetric component — associated with the change in volume of the element — and a deviatoric component — associated with shape change at constant volume. In a nearly incompressible material, the volumetric strains are small (because $J \approx 1$), but they are not negligible, and they contribute to the stress through the volumetric penalty term $\Psi_\text{vol}$. The deviatoric strains contribute through the isotropic and anisotropic terms.

The bug was that in the compressible implementation, the stress computation was receiving only the deviatoric component of the strain rather than the full strain. This had the effect of systematically underestimating the stress, because the volumetric contribution to the Piola-Kirchhoff stress was being dropped. The magnitude of the error depended on how far the material was from perfect incompressibility — in the nearly incompressible limit with $\kappa = 1000$ kPa, the volumetric strains are small but the pressure they generate is not, and the missing contribution was significant.

The symptoms were subtle. The simulation ran to completion without numerical warnings. The pressure-volume loops had the correct qualitative shape — a rapid rise during isovolumic contraction, a roughly rectangular ejection plateau, a rapid fall during isovolumic relaxation, and a slow rise during filling. The ejection fraction was plausible. What betrayed the problem was the stress magnitude: when we extracted mean fiber stresses from the simulation results and compared them against published values for cardiac tissue at comparable deformation levels, the simulated values were substantially too low. This prompted a systematic check of the stress computation chain, which eventually identified the deviatoric-only issue. The fix required modifying the library code to pass the full strain tensor to the stress calculation routines, after which the stress magnitudes came into the expected range and the pressure-volume loops remained qualitatively unchanged but with corrected energy budgets.

Integrated quantities like stroke volume or ejection fraction are relatively insensitive to stress magnitude because they are driven by the displacement field, which is determined primarily by the volume constraint rather than the constitutive law. Field-level checks — stress magnitudes against published values for cardiac tissue — catch bugs that loop shapes hide.

## Metrics Calculation and the DG1 Projection Problem

The internal mechanical work is computed by integrating the double contraction $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ over the myocardial volume and over time. Both $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ are computed at the quadrature points of the finite element mesh, which is where the constitutive law is evaluated during the Newton solve. Storing these fields for post-processing requires projecting them from the quadrature space onto a function space that can be written to disk and later read for analysis.

The natural choice for a projection target is a first-order discontinuous Galerkin (DG1) space, which allows discontinuities between elements (appropriate for stress, which is not required to be continuous across element boundaries in standard finite elements) while providing linear variation within each element. This projection worked without numerical complaints and produced smooth-looking stress and strain visualizations. The problem appeared only when we attempted to compute the integrated work from the projected fields: the result was approximately three times smaller than the boundary work computed from cavity pressure, volume change, and Robin support work, which should close against the full internal tensor work when evaluated consistently.

An energy deficit of this magnitude was alarming. It suggested either that a large fraction of the external work was being absorbed somewhere in the simulation — by the boundary spring conditions, perhaps — or that there was a systematic error in the work calculation. We tested the boundary spring hypothesis by reducing the spring stiffness by a factor of two and rerunning the simulation; the resulting change in the integrated work was less than 0.3%, making this explanation untenable. The error had to be in the work computation itself.

The root cause turned out to be the DG1 projection. Near the boundaries between anatomical regions — particularly at the LV-septum and septum-RV interfaces — the stress and strain fields have steep gradients because wall geometry, fibre orientation, pressure loading, and regional integration masks all change rapidly there. When the DG1 projection attempts to represent these steep local fields with low-order polynomials, the resulting nodal values can exhibit spurious oscillations: the field overshoots on one side and undershoots on the other, with oscillations that can be large in magnitude relative to the local value. When the work integrand $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ is formed from two oscillating fields and integrated over a thin region like the septum, contributions with opposite signs can cancel, producing an integral that is much smaller than the quadrature-level value.

Switching to a piecewise-constant DG0 projection eliminated the oscillations entirely. In DG0, each element is assigned a single average value, so there is no room for spurious nodal oscillations. The trade-off is that local spatial variation within each element is lost, which reduces the accuracy of the projected field for pointwise comparisons. For integrated quantities over anatomical regions, however, DG0 is actually more accurate than DG1 in the presence of sharp interface gradients, because the averaging operation smooths out the oscillations rather than representing them faithfully. After switching to DG0 projections, the computed internal work increased substantially and came into a more physically consistent ratio with the external work.

It is worth being precise about what "more physically consistent" means here. The full internal tensor work is expected to balance the total boundary work when the same run, pressure history, cavity-volume change, and Robin support formulation are used. Cavity PV work alone is not the complete boundary budget in this model, and projected fibre-only diagnostics are not the same object as the full tensor contraction. The relevant validation target is therefore closure of the full tensor work against cavity work plus Robin work, not equality between every reduced diagnostic and the PV loop.

The final resolution of the projection problem was to avoid projecting the current stress altogether. Instead of interpolating $\mathbf{S}$ into a finite element space and then integrating the interpolated field, the work integral uses the raw symbolic UFL expression $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{C})$ directly. When FEniCSx assembles the form $\int \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{C}) : d\mathbf{E} \, dV$, it evaluates $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{C})$ at each quadrature point from the displacement degrees of freedom — the same evaluation that the solver itself performs during the Newton iteration. There is no interpolation, no projection, and no smoothing; the stress is computed exactly (up to quadrature accuracy) from the nonlinear constitutive law at every integration point. The previous-timestep stress $\mathbf{S}(t_{i-1})$, which must be stored for the trapezoidal rule, is still represented as an interpolated function in the DG1 space. The trapezoidal integrand $\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{S}_\text{UFL} + \mathbf{S}_\text{prev}) : d\mathbf{E}$ is therefore a hybrid: exact for the current state, interpolated for the previous. This is the tightest possible approximation — the only remaining projection error is in the stored previous stress, and its contribution to the work integral is second-order in the time step.

## Matching the Solver's Boundary Formulations

Once the internal work integral was correct, closing the energy balance required that the external work terms — cavity pressure work and Robin spring work — be computed using exactly the same mathematical formulations as the solver. Two discrepancies between the post-processing work formulae and the solver's variational forms had to be corrected.

### Robin spring work and the Nanson transform

The Robin boundary condition in fenicsx-pulse models the pericardial constraint as a spring that resists outward displacement of the epicardial surface. The solver's variational form implements this spring in the current (deformed) configuration using the Nanson formula to map the reference normal to the deformed normal:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{J \, \mathbf{F}^{-T} \mathbf{N}}{|J \, \mathbf{F}^{-T} \mathbf{N}|},
$$

where $\mathbf{N}$ is the reference outward normal, $\mathbf{F}$ is the deformation gradient, and $J = \det \mathbf{F}$. The spring force is applied only in the direction of this deformed normal — the projection $(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n})(\mathbf{n} \otimes \mathbf{n})$ — and the surface element is scaled by the cofactor norm $|J \, \mathbf{F}^{-T} \mathbf{N}|$ to account for the change in surface area under deformation. The Robin work in the post-processing must therefore be

$$
W_\text{robin} = -\sum_i \int_{\Gamma_\text{robin}} k \, (\bar{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \mathbf{n})(\Delta \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}) \, |J \, \mathbf{F}^{-T} \mathbf{N}| \, dS_0,
$$

where $k$ is the spring stiffness, $\bar{\mathbf{u}}$ is the midpoint displacement, and $\Delta \mathbf{u}$ is the displacement increment. The original post-processing formula had used the simpler expression $k \, \mathbf{u} \cdot \Delta \mathbf{u} \, dS_0$ — resisting displacement in all directions, integrating over the reference surface element, with the reference normal implicit in $dS_0$. This overestimated the Robin work by including tangential displacement components that the solver does not penalize.

### Boundary pressure work and linearization

The work done by cavity pressure on the endocardial surface was originally computed as

$$
W_\text{boundary} \approx \bar{P} \int_{\Gamma_\text{endo}} \mathbf{N} \cdot \Delta \mathbf{u} \, dS_0,
$$

which approximates the volume change $\Delta V$ by linearizing the deformation: $\Delta V \approx \int \mathbf{N} \cdot \Delta \mathbf{u} \, dS_0$. At finite deformations, this linearization systematically overestimates the actual volume change because it neglects the nonlinear dependence of the cavity volume on the displacement field. The correct approach computes the actual cavity volume at each configuration using the divergence theorem,

$$
V(\mathbf{u}) = -\frac{1}{3} \int_{\Gamma_\text{endo}} J \, (\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{u}) \cdot (\mathbf{F}^{-T} \mathbf{N}) \, dS_0,
$$

and takes the difference $\Delta V = V(\mathbf{u}_\text{new}) - V(\mathbf{u}_\text{old})$ to obtain the exact volume change. The boundary work is then $W_\text{boundary} = \bar{P} \cdot \Delta V$ with the trapezoidal pressure. The linearization error was largest at peak systole, where the deformation is most severe — precisely the phase that dominates the total cycle work.

With all four corrections in place — direct UFL stress evaluation, DG1 storage for previous state, Nanson-consistent Robin work, and divergence-theorem boundary volumes — the energy balance closed to sub-percent accuracy in the checked validation runs, and to the $10^{-4}$ relative level in the final UKB replay used for the energy-balance figure.

## The 0D–3D Pressure Mismatch and Energy Conservation

The simulation saves displacement checkpoints at every time step, allowing all mechanical quantities — stress, strain, work — to be recomputed offline from the saved deformation history without rerunning the forward solve. This postprocessing pipeline was designed to allow rapid iteration on the metrics computation without the expense of a full simulation. The internal work is computed by integrating the stress-strain double contraction over the myocardium, and the external (boundary) work is computed from the product of cavity pressure and volume change at each step. In a correct implementation, these two quantities should satisfy an energy balance: the internal work plus the work absorbed by the Robin boundary springs should equal the external work done by the endocardial pressure loading.

During the forward simulation, the cavity pressure used for the boundary work calculation is the Lagrange multiplier from the volume constraint — the exact surface traction needed to achieve the prescribed volume. When computing boundary work on-the-fly during the simulation, the energy balance closed to within 0.0004%, confirming that the implementation was correct. However, when the same calculation was performed offline from checkpoints, the energy balance showed a 29% discrepancy.

The source of this discrepancy was the pressure used in the offline boundary work calculation. The postprocessing script did not have access to the Lagrange multiplier (which exists only as an internal variable of the Newton solver during the live simulation), and instead used the cavity pressure from the 0D circulation model's state history. As discussed in the model chapter, the 0D pressure and the Lagrange multiplier pressure differ by 30–60% throughout the cardiac cycle because the time-varying elastance is only a coarse approximation of the 3D pressure-volume relationship.

The fix was to save the Lagrange multiplier pressure at each time step alongside the displacement checkpoint. With the solver's cavity pressures available to the postprocessing pipeline, the offline energy balance recovered the same tight closure achieved during the live simulation. For existing simulation runs that were performed before this fix, the 0D pressures remain the only available pressure history, and the energy balance for those runs should be interpreted with this caveat in mind.

This experience reinforces a general point about coupled simulations: similarly named quantities are not automatically interchangeable when they come from different model levels or different stages of the pipeline. The 0D and 3D pressures both represent "cavity pressure," but they are answers to different questions — one from a lumped-parameter elastance, the other from a three-dimensional variational principle — and conflating them introduces errors that are large enough to corrupt energy conservation checks and pressure-strain proxy calculations.

## Calibrating the Heart Rate

The 0D circulation model of Regazzoni et al. was originally calibrated at a heart rate somewhat different from our target of 75 beats per minute. Adapting the model to the correct rate required adjusting not only the cycle length but the activation timing parameters of the four cardiac chambers, the duration of the isovolumic phases, and the time constants of the pressure recovery dynamics. A naive scaling of all time parameters by the ratio of cycle lengths produced reasonable hemodynamics but distorted the shapes of the pressure waveforms — in particular, the isovolumic contraction and relaxation phases, which are governed by the material stiffness and valve dynamics rather than purely by timing, did not scale linearly with the cycle length.

The adjustment was performed iteratively by comparing the simulated pressure waveforms against the clinical targets for a resting 75 bpm adult: LV peak pressure approximately 120 mmHg, RV peak pressure approximately 25 mmHg, aortic mean pressure approximately 95 mmHg, and stroke volumes consistent with a cardiac output of around 5 L/min. In the stand-alone 0D model, the ventricular elastance timing — the contraction phase duration $T_C$ and the relaxation phase duration $T_R$ in the Blanco model — was adjusted first, followed by the compliance and resistance parameters of the Windkessel circuits to achieve the correct diastolic pressure decay shape. The process required roughly a dozen simulation runs and manual inspection of the resulting waveforms before the outputs were within acceptable range of the clinical targets.

For the pulmonary arterial hypertension cases, the adjustments were substantially more extensive. PAH is characterized by elevated pulmonary vascular resistance, which in the model corresponds primarily to an increase in the distal resistance of the pulmonary Windkessel. But matching the target hemodynamics required adjusting multiple 0D parameters simultaneously — pulmonary resistance, pulmonary compliance, chamber elastance parameters, timing constants, and blood-volume distribution — because the closed-loop circulation responds as a coupled system. The three-dimensional myocardial material parameters and peak active tension were held fixed in the coupled mechanics runs; the pressure-loading spectrum is therefore a controlled loading experiment rather than a tuned model of RV remodelling. The optimization of the 0D parameters is described in the following chapter.
