# Results

This chapter presents the simulation results for all three geometries: the synthetic UK Biobank baseline, the patient-specific healthy case, and the patient-specific pulmonary arterial hypertension case. For each, we first verify that the coupled simulation produces hemodynamically plausible pressure-volume loops, then examine the internal work budget of the myocardium, and finally compare the ground-truth septal work against the three pressure-strain proxies that constitute the central investigation.

## Hemodynamic Validation

A necessary precondition for the proxy comparison is that the simulated hemodynamics are physiologically realistic. If the pressure-volume loops do not resemble those of a real heart, the stress and strain fields will not be representative of the conditions under which clinical myocardial work imaging is applied, and the proxy comparison would be of theoretical interest only.

### Healthy Baseline (Synthetic UKB Geometry)

The 0D circulation model was calibrated to match the hemodynamic targets of a resting adult at 75 beats per minute. The optimized parameters, obtained via Bayesian optimization with the TPE sampler as described in Chapter 4, produce a steady-state LV pressure-volume loop with end-systolic pressure of approximately 120 mmHg, end-diastolic pressure of approximately 10 mmHg, and stroke volume of approximately 70 mL. The RV loop shows end-systolic pressure of approximately 25 mmHg, end-diastolic pressure of 4 mmHg, and RV stroke volume matching the LV to within a few percent — a necessary consequence of circulatory mass conservation in steady state. The aortic diastolic pressure is approximately 80 mmHg and the mean left atrial pressure approximately 9 mmHg.

The coupled 3D-0D simulation reproduces these targets faithfully. The LV and RV pressure waveforms generated by the finite element solver match the 0D pre-run values, confirming that the volume ratio coupling strategy successfully maps between the 0D hemodynamic scale and the mesh geometric scale. The ejection fraction for both ventricles falls within the normal clinical range of 50–65%.

### Patient-Specific Healthy Geometry

The same circulation parameters are used with the patient-specific healthy mesh. Because the mesh volumes differ from the synthetic case, the coupling ratios adjust accordingly, and the resulting PV loops maintain the same hemodynamic targets. The LV end-systolic and end-diastolic pressures match the calibrated values, confirming that the simulation framework is portable across geometries without requiring per-geometry recalibration of the 0D model.

### Patient-Specific PAH Geometry

The PAH simulation uses circulation parameters calibrated to match the hemodynamics of the PAH patient. The calibrated model produces an RV end-systolic pressure of approximately 60 mmHg — more than double the healthy value — with an RV end-diastolic pressure of approximately 10 mmHg, consistent with the elevated filling pressures seen in RV pressure overload. The LV end-systolic pressure is approximately 110 mmHg, slightly reduced from normal due to impaired LV filling from septal flattening and ventricular interdependence. The stroke volume is reduced to approximately 55 mL, reflecting the forward failure characteristic of advanced PAH. The mean left atrial pressure remains below 15 mmHg, confirming that the simulated disease is pre-capillary in nature — the elevated pulmonary pressure is caused by vascular resistance, not left heart failure.

The RV PV loop is visibly different from the healthy case: wider, taller, and more triangular, reflecting the increased afterload and prolonged isovolumic contraction. The LV loop is narrower and shifted leftward, consistent with the reduced LV filling caused by septal displacement.

## Internal Work Decomposition

Before comparing proxies, we examine the internal work budget to understand how mechanical energy is distributed across the myocardium and across its tensor components. The total internal work in a region $\Omega_j$ over one cardiac cycle is

$$
W_\text{int}[\Omega_j] = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \int_{\Omega_j} \bar{\mathbf{S}}(t_i) : d\mathbf{E}(t_i) \, d\mathbf{X},
$$

computed using the trapezoidal rule as described in Chapter 1. This quantity can be decomposed into contributions from different stress and strain components.

### Directional Decomposition

The internal work integral can be decomposed into contributions from the fiber direction ($\mathbf{f}_0$), the sheet direction ($\mathbf{s}_0$), the sheet-normal direction ($\mathbf{n}_0$), and a shear remainder:

$$
W_\text{int} = W_\text{fiber} + W_\text{sheet} + W_\text{normal} + W_\text{shear},
$$

where $W_\text{fiber} = \sum_i \int_\Omega \bar{S}_{ff} \, dE_{ff} \, d\mathbf{X}$ and similarly for the other directions. This decomposition reveals how much of the total work is performed along the fiber axis — the direction most directly related to active contraction — versus the transverse and shear directions. In the healthy case, fiber-direction work accounts for the majority of the total in the LV free wall and the septum, consistent with the findings of Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` who reported that fiber-only work was approximately 60% of the full tensor work in their LV-only model. The RV free wall, with its thinner wall and more complex geometry, shows a somewhat different distribution.

### Active and Passive Decomposition

The total stress $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{S}_\text{passive} + \mathbf{S}_\text{active} + \mathbf{S}_\text{comp}$ can be split into contributions from the passive hyperelastic response (Holzapfel-Ogden), the active contraction ($T_a \mathbf{f}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0$), and the compressibility penalty. The corresponding work decomposition,

$$
W_\text{int} = W_\text{active} + W_\text{passive} + W_\text{comp},
$$

identifies how much energy is generated by active contraction versus stored and released by the passive elastic tissue. The compressibility work should be small if the material is behaving in a nearly incompressible manner ($J \approx 1$). In all three cases, $W_\text{comp}$ is at least an order of magnitude smaller than $W_\text{active}$, confirming that the compressible penalty formulation does not introduce significant spurious energy.

## The Central Comparison: Pressure-Strain Proxies for Septal Work

The core experiment compares the ground-truth internal work $W_\text{int}[\Omega_\text{septum}]$ against the pressure-strain proxy

$$
W_\text{PS}[\Omega_\text{septum}] = \bar{V}_\text{septum} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \bar{P}(t_i) \, d\bar{E}_{ff,\text{septum}}(t_i),
$$

for three choices of $P$: the LV cavity pressure ($P_\text{LV}$), the RV cavity pressure ($P_\text{RV}$), and the transmural pressure ($P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$). A fourth variant using the mean pressure $\frac{1}{2}(P_\text{LV} + P_\text{RV})$ is included for completeness.

### Healthy Cases

In both the synthetic and patient-specific healthy geometries, the three pressure variants produce similar proxy values for the septum. The transmural proxy and the LV pressure proxy differ by only the small correction $P_\text{RV} \cdot dE_{ff}$, which is modest because $P_\text{RV} \approx 25$ mmHg is small relative to $P_\text{LV} \approx 120$ mmHg. The total cycle work estimated by the transmural proxy differs from the LV proxy by approximately 15–20% in absolute terms, but neither proxy tracks the ground-truth $W_\text{int}$ particularly closely in absolute magnitude — the pressure-strain product systematically underestimates the full tensor work, as previously reported by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`. The temporal correlation between the instantaneous proxy power and the true internal power rate, quantified by the Pearson $R^2$, is high for all three variants, confirming that the proxies capture the correct timing of energy generation and dissipation even when the magnitudes differ.

### PAH Case

In the PAH geometry, the proxy comparison changes qualitatively. The LV pressure proxy now substantially overestimates septal work relative to the ground truth, because it attributes the full LV pressure to the septum while ignoring the elevated RV pressure acting on the opposite face. The transmural proxy, which subtracts the RV contribution, produces a total cycle work that is closer to the ground-truth $W_\text{int}$. The $R^2$ correlation between the transmural proxy and the true septal work rate is higher than the corresponding $R^2$ for the LV proxy.

The RV pressure proxy produces a negative total work for the septum in the healthy case (because the septum shortens while $P_\text{RV}$ is positive, and the shortening is in the direction that would expand the RV cavity), which is consistent with the septum doing work *for* the RV rather than *against* it. In the PAH case, the sign and magnitude of the RV proxy change as the septal motion reverses.

The mean pressure proxy, $\frac{1}{2}(P_\text{LV} + P_\text{RV})$, produces intermediate results. It is better than $P_\text{LV}$ alone but not as close to the ground truth as the transmural pressure.

## Energy Conservation Check

As a consistency check, the total internal work integrated over the entire myocardium should satisfy the energy balance

$$
W_\text{int}[\Omega_\text{whole}] = W_\text{boundary,LV} + W_\text{boundary,RV} + W_\text{robin,epi} + W_\text{robin,base},
$$

where $W_\text{boundary}$ is the work done by the endocardial pressure tractions (computed via surface integration of $-P \, \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}$) and $W_\text{robin}$ is the work done by the Robin spring boundary conditions. This balance holds by the principle of virtual work and provides a powerful internal consistency check: any discrepancy indicates either a numerical error in the work computation or an inconsistency in how the stress and strain fields are evaluated. The residual imbalance is less than 1% of the total internal work in all three cases, confirming that the quadrature-space metrics computation, the trapezoidal time integration, and the boundary work integrals are mutually consistent. This was the test that originally revealed the DG1 projection bug described in Chapter 3: when stress and strain were projected to a first-order discontinuous Galerkin space before integration, the energy balance showed a discrepancy of 5–10%, which vanished when the projections were replaced with direct evaluation at quadrature points.

## Free Wall Proxies

For completeness, we verify the proxy accuracy for the LV and RV free walls, where the pressure assignment is unambiguous. The LV free wall proxy using $P_\text{LV}$ and the RV free wall proxy using $P_\text{RV}$ both show high temporal correlation ($R^2 > 0.9$) with the ground-truth internal work in all three cases. The absolute magnitude of the pressure-strain proxy underestimates the full tensor work, consistent with the observation that the pressure-strain product captures only the fiber-direction contribution to work and neglects the transverse and shear components. The ratio of proxy to true work for the LV free wall is comparable to the values reported by Finsberg et al. for their LV-only model.

The consistency of the free wall results across geometries and disease states supports the conclusion that the pressure-strain proxy is a reasonable metric for relative comparisons when the pressure assignment is unambiguous, and that the discrepancies observed in the septum arise specifically from the dual-loading geometry rather than from any fundamental failure of the pressure-strain framework.
