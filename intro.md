+++{"part" : "abstract"}
The interventricular septum is mechanically ambiguous: it is simultaneously a wall of the left ventricle and a wall of the right ventricle, loaded by both chambers at once. In diseases like pulmonary arterial hypertension, where right ventricular systolic pressure rises toward systemic levels, this ambiguity has direct consequences for how we estimate the mechanical work of the septal myocardium. Clinical pressure-strain loop methods assign left ventricular pressure to the septum, implicitly assuming that LV pressure governs septal mechanics. This assumption is reasonable in health, but it is not obvious that it holds when RV pressure is substantially elevated. This thesis investigates the question using a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model of a biventricular heart, extending the LV-only modeling framework of Finsberg et al. to the biventricular case. Ground-truth internal mechanical work is computed from the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain fields throughout the cardiac cycle, and compared against pressure-strain proxies using three pressure definitions: LV pressure, RV pressure, and transmural pressure. Simulations are run for a synthetic healthy baseline geometry derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model, a patient-specific healthy anatomy, and a patient-specific pulmonary arterial hypertension case. The main finding is that transmural pressure tracks septal mechanical work more faithfully than LV pressure alone in the PAH case, while both proxies perform equivalently in health. The thesis documents the computational framework in full, including the implementation challenges encountered — a constitutive model bug causing systematically low stresses, spurious energy imbalances from finite element function space projections, and the optimization of the circulation model parameters for the disease case.

# Pressure Proxies for Septal Mechanical Work

The interventricular septum is a shared wall. It forms the medial boundary of the left ventricular cavity on one face and the medial boundary of the right ventricular cavity on the other, which means it is loaded by two different pressures simultaneously. In the single-ventricle models that dominate the computational cardiac mechanics literature, this dual loading is resolved by convention: the septum is treated as part of the left ventricle, and the right ventricular pressure on its opposite face is either ignored or absent from the model entirely. The convention is defensible when LV pressure is much larger than RV pressure, as it is in healthy physiology, but it becomes genuinely questionable in pulmonary arterial hypertension, where right ventricular systolic pressure can approach or transiently exceed that of the left ventricle. This thesis asks, in quantitative terms, how large the error in septal work estimation becomes when the LV-only convention is applied to a heart where both pressures are comparable.

The quantity being estimated is the internal mechanical work of the septal myocardium — the energy stored and released by the tissue over a cardiac cycle, computed from the double contraction of the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress with the rate of Green-Lagrange strain. This integral, $\int_0^T \int_\Omega \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt$, is the natural thermodynamic measure of mechanical work in a hyperelastic body undergoing large deformations; it is what the finite element model computes directly from the constitutive law. In clinical practice, however, myocardial stress cannot be measured in an intact beating heart, and the internal work integral is replaced by a pressure-strain proxy: the product of a chamber pressure with the fiber strain increment, summed over the cardiac cycle. The accuracy of this substitution — replacing $\mathbf{S}$ with a scalar $P$ and $\mathbf{E}$ with its scalar fiber-direction projection — has been examined for the left ventricle by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` and found to capture relative trends in regional work even while underestimating absolute magnitudes. For the septum, the same substitution requires a choice of which chamber pressure to use, and that choice is not obvious.

The standard choice is LV pressure: clinicians apply LV pressure to all segments of the left heart, including the septum, when computing myocardial work from echocardiographic strain {cite}`russell2012novel`. This is equivalent to assuming that the full LV pressure difference across the septal wall drives septal shortening. But if RV pressure is elevated and opposing LV-driven septal motion, the net mechanical load on the septal tissue is better characterized by the transmural pressure difference $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ than by $P_\text{LV}$ alone. The question is whether the error incurred by ignoring $P_\text{RV}$ is large enough to matter, and that depends on the magnitudes involved. In health, $P_\text{RV} \approx 25$ mmHg against $P_\text{LV} \approx 120$ mmHg, so the approximation is good. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, where $P_\text{RV}$ can reach 60–80 mmHg and $P_\text{LV}$ may be normal or even reduced by impaired LV filling, the omission becomes substantial.

The pressure-volume loop provides a related perspective on the same issue. The area enclosed by the loop,

$$
W_\text{ext} = \oint P \, dV,
$$

gives the external hydraulic work performed by the ventricle in one beat and is the classical myocardial work metric since the work of Suga {cite}`suga1979total`. For the right ventricle, the loop area includes a contribution from the septum: septal motion driven by the left ventricle displaces blood out of the RV cavity, adding to the RV stroke volume without requiring contractile effort from the RV free wall. Standard PV-loop analysis cannot distinguish this contribution from work generated by the RV myocardium. This energetic coupling between the two ventricles is well known in physiology {cite}`vonk2013right`, and its consequences for PV-loop-based work assessment are particularly acute in the PAH patient, where the ventricular pressure balance is disrupted and the septal contribution to RV ejection changes sign as the septum progressively flattens and eventually bows toward the left.

These considerations motivate the present study. Non-invasive myocardial work imaging combines echocardiographic longitudinal strain measurements with non-invasive pressure surrogates to compute regional pressure-strain loop areas {cite}`russell2012novel`, and the technique is increasingly used in PAH to assess right ventricular function. In current implementations, LV pressure is assigned to all septal segments. Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` showed computationally that this approach produces interpretable relative comparisons in the LV, and they explicitly identified biventricular extension — with the implied question about which pressure to assign to the septum — as the natural direction for future work. The present thesis is that extension.

We address the question computationally, building on the framework of Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment,balaban2017high` and extending it from left ventricular to biventricular geometry. The framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium — discretized using quadratic Lagrange elements and solved with the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` via the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse` — to a zero-dimensional lumped-parameter model of the full cardiovascular circuit {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The coupling is bidirectional at each time step: the 0D model prescribes cavity volumes that the 3D solver must match by deforming the myocardium to mechanical equilibrium, and the resulting cavity pressures are returned to the 0D model to advance the valve states and arterial dynamics. At every time step, the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor $\mathbf{S}$ and Green-Lagrange strain tensor $\mathbf{E}$ are available throughout the myocardium, giving direct access to the internal work density $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ at every quadrature point.

Three simulation cases are used. The first is a synthetic biventricular geometry derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, representing healthy anatomy at the population mean. The second is a patient-specific healthy geometry reconstructed from cardiovascular magnetic resonance imaging, providing a validation case against known-normal hemodynamics. The third uses the anatomy of a patient with confirmed pulmonary arterial hypertension, with model parameters calibrated to match the patient's measured hemodynamics. For each case, the ground-truth internal work of the septal region is computed from the stress-strain fields and compared against three pressure-strain proxies — using $P_\text{LV}$, $P_\text{RV}$, and the transmural difference $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ — to assess which choice most faithfully approximates the true work integral.

The main finding is that the transmural pressure yields a closer proxy for septal internal work than LV pressure alone, and that this difference is numerically significant in the PAH case. In health, where $P_\text{RV} \ll P_\text{LV}$, both definitions perform equivalently. In PAH, using LV pressure alone overestimates septal work because it neglects the opposing load that elevated RV pressure places on the RV face of the septum. The transmural pressure accounts for this load and improves correspondence with the finite element ground truth.

Building this framework required resolving several implementation problems that are documented in full because they are instructive for anyone working with coupled cardiac mechanics simulations. A bug in the fenicsx-pulse constitutive model passed only the deviatoric component of the strain to the stress computation, causing systematically low stresses. A spurious apparent energy deficit emerged from projecting quadrature-point stress and strain fields onto a first-order discontinuous Galerkin function space, where spurious oscillations near material interface boundaries caused cancellation in the work integral; switching to a piecewise-constant projection resolved the issue. The 0D circulation parameter identification for the PAH case required Bayesian optimization to navigate a cost function with multiple coupled constraints, including the physiologically critical requirement that left atrial pressure remain normal — the defining criterion distinguishing pre-capillary from post-capillary pulmonary hypertension.
