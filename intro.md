title: Pressure Proxies for Septal Mechanical Work
---

+++{"part" : "abstract"}
The interventricular septum is mechanically ambiguous: it is simultaneously a wall of the left ventricle and a wall of the right ventricle, loaded by both chambers at once. This thesis uses a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model as a controlled mechanical system for testing how well clinical pressure-strain proxies recover model-resolved myocardial work. The finite-element model provides the stress and strain fields that are hidden in patients, so internal work can be computed from the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain throughout the cardiac cycle. That tensor-work reference is compared with pressure-strain proxies that use LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure, mean pressure, or a through-wall weighted pressure. The geometry is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters. The main finding is that adjacent cavity pressure works reasonably well for preserving the LV/RV free-wall work-density ratio, but the septum is different. Transmural pressure is meaningful for septal force balance and shape, but it does not preserve septal work magnitudes well in these simulations. Two-sided pressure choices, especially mean or through-wall weighted pressure, give a more stable septal magnitude proxy, while correlation rankings across pressure sweeps depend strongly on the chosen loading path.
+++

# Introduction

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. Each beat displaces roughly 70 mL of blood against an arterial pressure near 100 mmHg, which amounts to about one joule of mechanical work per stroke. Multiply by a resting heart rate and the ventricle does something on the order of $10^5$ J of work over the course of a day, continuously, for a lifetime. Understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it is therefore not a peripheral question about cardiac function. It is close to the centre of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

The basic pump calculation is pressure-volume work. Pressure-volume diagrams have been used since Clapeyron's graphical treatment of thermodynamic cycles, where the area enclosed by a closed pressure-volume curve represents mechanical work {cite}`clapeyron1834memoire`. For a ventricle, the corresponding quantity is the area enclosed by the cavity pressure-volume loop,

$$
W_\text{ext} = -\oint P\,dV,
$$

where the minus sign gives positive stroke work during ejection under the sign convention used here. This is the external hydraulic work done by the ventricle on the blood. It is a boundary quantity: pressure is measured at the cavity, and volume is the volume enclosed by that cavity. The question in this thesis begins when the same work is asked about inside the wall: where in the myocardium is work generated, and can a simple pressure-strain proxy recover that distribution?

In a finite-element model the same mechanical work can be written as a field quantity. The work done inside a deforming elastic body is the volume integral of stress against the rate of strain, so for the myocardium over one beat,

$$
W_\text{int} = \int_0^T \!\! \int_\Omega \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor and $\mathbf{E}$ is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`. This stress-strain pair is used because the finite-element model is written on the reference mesh, and $\mathbf{S}:\dot{\mathbf{E}}$ is the corresponding mechanical power density for large deformations. The next chapter defines these tensors and derives the energy identity connecting the tensor integral to the cavity pressure-volume work, including the boundary terms introduced by the external support of the numerical model.

This distinction between boundary work and field work is the computational core of the thesis. The chamber loop $\oint P\,dV$ is available from pressure and volume alone. The tensor integral $\int \mathbf{S}:\dot{\mathbf{E}}\,d\mathbf{X}$ is available only if the stress and strain fields inside the tissue are known. A finite-element simulation provides those fields at quadrature points throughout the myocardium, which means that the whole-heart work can be decomposed into LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum, and also into fiber, sheet, and wall-normal contributions. The model therefore gives access to a mechanical quantity that cannot be measured directly in a patient.

The clinical pressure-strain proxy is attractive precisely because this tensor field is not available in vivo. Pressure-length loops were already used in the 1970s as approximate regional work indices {cite}`tyberg1974segmental,forrester1974pressure_length`. Urheim et al. {cite}`urheim2005regional` introduced the echocardiographic pressure-strain version by combining LV pressure with regional strain, and Russell et al. {cite}`russell2012novel` made the method clinically practical by replacing invasive LV pressure with a non-invasive pressure estimate based on valve timing and cuff pressure. The rationale is clear: if the pressure-volume loop measures whole-ventricle work, then pressure plotted against local deformation may give a regional work index. The limitation is just as clear, and it was present from the beginning. Chamber pressure is not local myocardial stress. A more mechanical stress estimate would need information about wall thickness, curvature, and geometry, while clinical pressure-strain work deliberately uses a measurable pressure signal instead.

This is where the finite-element model becomes useful. The point is not to replace clinical measurement with a more elaborate black box. It is to test the proxy in a setting where the hidden mechanical variables are known. The model used here is idealized, but it is mechanically consistent: the same coupled system produces the pressures, volumes, strains, stresses, and internal work. This makes it a favourable test of the pressure-strain approximation. If the proxy cannot reproduce the model-resolved work distribution in this controlled setting, where there is no image-tracking error, no uncertain pressure scaling, and no patient-to-patient variation in geometry or material properties, then it is unlikely to recover true regional tissue work more reliably in vivo. Conversely, if it performs well in a region, the model identifies where the proxy's mechanical assumptions are at least internally plausible.

Regional stress-strain work is already an established modelling quantity. Delhaas et al. {cite}`delhaas1994regional` estimated regional fiber stress and fiber strain in canine left ventricles and compared stress-strain loop area with regional oxygen demand. Finite-element studies have since used fiber or tensor work to analyse MRI-based LV mechanics, coronary perfusion, biventricular geometry and fiber architecture, LVAD interaction, and CRT response {cite}`wang2012myocardial,namani2020effects,pluijmert2017determinants,ahmadbakir2018multiphysics,craine2024successful`. Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` is the closest predecessor for the proxy-versus-model comparison: a patient-specific LV mechanics model was used to compare pressure-strain loops with finite-element stress-strain work in one healthy volunteer and one patient with left bundle branch block. The proxy preserved useful relative information, but underestimated absolute work and could even disagree in loop orientation in the septum of the dyssynchronous case. More recently, Craine et al. {cite}`craine2024successful` used patient-specific models of dyssynchronous heart failure to compare regional stress-strain work with simplified work estimates that use ventricular pressure as a stress surrogate. Regional work is therefore not a new modelling object, and pressure-based work proxies have been tested before; the open question is how the pressure approximation behaves in a biventricular finite-element model, where the septum is loaded by two chambers rather than one.

That septal question is what makes the problem biventricular rather than simply regional. In an LV-only setting, LV pressure is the natural pressure scale. In the RV free wall, RV pressure is the corresponding choice. The interventricular septum is different: it is one piece of tissue with an LV-facing surface and an RV-facing surface. Under healthy loading, RV systolic pressure is much lower than LV systolic pressure, so the LV pressure dominates and the septal pressure choice may be a small correction {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, RV pressure rises and the septum flattens as the balance between the two ventricles changes {cite}`humbert2022esc,tello2019tapse,finsberg2019computational`. Then LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure, mean pressure, and through-wall weighted pressure no longer represent the same mechanical assumption.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow a fully orthotropic Holzapfel-Ogden active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. The coupling is bidirectional: the 0D model supplies cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the circulation. This gives physiologically realistic LV and RV pressure-volume dynamics while retaining access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The geometry used throughout is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters.

The thesis computes model-resolved tensor work throughout this system, decomposes it by anatomical region, and compares it with pressure-strain proxies that differ in the pressure assigned to each region. For the free walls, the natural proxy uses the adjacent chamber pressure. For the septum, the tested choices include LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, mean pressure, and a through-wall weighted pressure. This is deliberately a controlled computational comparison. The pressure sweep should not be read as a full simulation of clinical PAH progression, because real disease changes pressure, volume, wall thickness, geometry, stiffness, activation, and remodelling together. Here the purpose is narrower: isolate the pressure-for-stress approximation and ask where it remains mechanically meaningful.

The main result is that the pressure-strain proxy behaves best where its mechanical assumptions are simplest. In the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio reasonably well. In the septum, the pressure choice is not a harmless detail. LV pressure can rank cases well along one corrected pressure-loading sweep, but it does not give the most stable septal magnitude estimate. Transmural pressure captures the pressure difference that helps determine septal flattening, but it performs poorly when the question is how much septal work is present relative to the free walls. Two-sided pressure choices such as mean pressure or a through-wall weighted pressure better reflect the septum as shared tissue loaded by both cavities. The pressure sweep should therefore be read as a loading-path sensitivity test, not as a direct simulation of clinical PAH progression.

The document is organized as follows. This introduction motivates the computational comparison and explains why the septum makes the pressure-strain approximation difficult. The scientific-question chapter states the comparison in mathematical form, defines the two work quantities being compared, and derives the whole-heart energy identity used to verify the computation. The model chapters describe the geometry, fiber architecture, Holzapfel-Ogden hyperelastic law, active stress formulation, zero-dimensional circulation model, and coupling strategy. The implementation chapter recounts the numerical and software challenges encountered in building the model. The calibration chapter describes the parameter identification used to generate the controlled pressure-loading path. The results chapter compares the pressure-strain proxies with the model-resolved tensor work. The discussion chapter interprets the findings and their limitations, and the final chapter gives the conclusion and future work.
