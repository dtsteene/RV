---
title: Pressure Proxies for Biventricular Myocardial Work
---

+++{"part" : "abstract"}
Clinical pressure-strain work indices are increasingly used to interpret regional ventricular function, but their mechanical meaning in the right ventricle (RV) and interventricular septum is uncertain. This thesis uses a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model as a controlled test system for the pressure-strain approximation. The model provides the stress and strain fields that are hidden in patients, allowing myocardial work density to be computed from the tensor contraction between second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain. This model-resolved tensor work density is compared with clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain indices in the left-ventricular (LV) free wall, RV free wall, and septum during a controlled RV pressure-loading sweep. The free walls test the setting in which the proxy should work best: one wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity pressure. The septum tests the harder biventricular case, because it is shared tissue loaded by LV pressure on one side and RV pressure on the other. The results show that adjacent cavity pressure gives a mechanically meaningful proxy in the free walls, including the RV free wall, where the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index tracks model-resolved tensor work density far better than longitudinal strain alone. The septum is different. Its tensor work contains a larger non-fibre contribution than the free walls, and the pressure assigned to the septum is not a harmless convention. LV pressure can rank one corrected pressure sweep well, but two-sided pressure choices, especially mean or through-wall weighted pressure, preserve septal work-density magnitudes more robustly. Transmural pressure is meaningful for septal force balance and shape, but it is a poor proxy for septal work-density magnitude in these simulations. The main conclusion is therefore not a universal septal pressure formula, but a mechanical distinction: pressure-strain indices are most defensible in one-pressure free walls, while septal work in RV pressure overload should be interpreted as a shared-wall problem whose proxy ranking depends on the loading path and on the quantity being validated.

# Introduction

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. Each beat the left ventricle displaces roughly 70 mL of blood against a systemic arterial pressure near 100 mmHg, which amounts to about one joule of mechanical work per stroke; the right ventricle moves the same volume into the pulmonary circulation in parallel, at a fraction of that pressure. At a resting heart rate the heart does something on the order of $10^5$ J of work over the course of a day, continuously, for a lifetime. That sounds like a lot, but in food-energy terms it is roughly the calories in a quarter of a banana — a small daily budget for a muscle that never rests. Small as that daily budget is, understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it is not a peripheral question about cardiac function. It is close to the centre of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

Before reducing that beat to equations, it is useful to fix the anatomical language used throughout the thesis. Figure {numref}`fig-anatomy` shows the four-chamber heart, with the left ventricle, right ventricle, and interventricular septum forming the biventricular regions that are central here.

The heart wall is layered. The *endocardium* lines the blood-facing cavity surface, the *epicardium* covers the outside, and the *myocardium* is the thick muscular layer between them {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. When this thesis refers to myocardial work, it means work in that muscular wall tissue. The thin endocardial and epicardial layers are important anatomical surfaces, but they are not treated as separate volumetric materials in the finite-element model. They appear as boundary surfaces where pressure, support conditions, and through-wall fibre directions are defined.

With those labels in place, the object modeled in this thesis is not only a pressure-volume circuit. It is a deforming biventricular finite-element heart, driven through filling, contraction, ejection, and relaxation by a coupled circulation model. The two parts exchange volumes and pressures at every step: the circulation prescribes the target cavity volumes that the ventricles must match, and the finite-element solver returns the cavity pressures that enforce those volumes, which in turn advance the circulation. On top of those chamber-level signals, the finite-element model also gives the stress and strain fields inside the myocardial wall.

Returning to the heart as a pump, the basic mechanical calculation is pressure-volume work. The pressure-volume (PV) diagram, originally an engineering indicator diagram introduced by James Watt and John Southern in 1796 to measure steam-engine work, was later brought into cardiac physiology by Otto Frank's volume-pressure description of ventricular work {cite}`cardwell1971fromwatt,frank1899grundform,lohff1999first`.

Frank's pressure-volume description applies to either ventricle, because the heart is more accurately a small set of pumps in series than a single one. The left ventricle drives oxygenated blood through the systemic circulation against a high arterial pressure, while the right ventricle drives the same volume per beat through the pulmonary circulation against a much lower pressure. The two atria act as smaller priming chambers that fill the ventricles before each beat, and every chamber traces its own pressure-volume loop. The atria are not the focus of this thesis, and the systemic and pulmonary flows have to balance over time, so two loops, one per ventricle, are enough to carry the chamber-level argument.

The beat in each panel of {numref}`fig-intro-pv-loops` starts in the bottom right corner and moves counter-clockwise. The ventricle first fills at low pressure as blood arrives from its atrium, then contracts at nearly fixed volume so pressure rises rapidly, then ejects blood as the outlet valve opens and volume falls, and finally relaxes back to low pressure before the next filling phase begins. Both ventricles trace this same four-phase cycle, but the left-ventricular peak pressure reaches roughly five times the right-ventricular peak because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. The positive area enclosed by each loop is the stroke work of that ventricle,

$$
SW = \left|\oint P\,dV\right|,
$$

the mechanical work transferred from the ventricular wall to the blood during one beat. These are chamber-level numbers: they tell us how much work each ventricle did as a pump, but not where in the wall that work was carried.

Where in the wall that work is actually being done — how it is distributed across the tissue rather than just summed at the cavity boundary — is a harder question, because work inside tissue is a local stress-strain process. Stress is force per area carried inside the myocardium: how strongly a small piece of tissue is being pulled, compressed, or sheared by its neighbours. Strain is deformation: how much that same piece of tissue has shortened, stretched, thickened, or sheared relative to a reference shape.

In one dimension, mechanical work per unit volume is stress times a change in strain. In three dimensions, the same idea becomes a tensor contraction: local mechanical power is stress acting through the local rate of deformation. If the true in-vivo myocardial stress and strain fields could be measured perfectly, this is the physical quantity one would use to describe local mechanical work.

Pressure-volume work and stress-strain work are two ways of writing the same mechanical exchange at different levels. Pressure-volume work describes the chamber boundary: pressure acting while cavity volume changes. Stress-strain work describes the tissue: internal force per area acting while the material deforms. The finite-element formulation used later is the large-deformation version of this same idea {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`.

The finite-element model lets us ask the corresponding local question by estimating stress and strain at points throughout the myocardial mesh. Because the stress-power quantity is local, it can be added up over any chosen region. In this thesis, those regions include the whole myocardium, the LV free wall, the RV free wall, and the septum. In the model, the work in a region $\Omega_j$ is written as

$$
W_\text{int}[\Omega_j] = \int_0^T \!\! \int_{\Omega_j} \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, dV_0 \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ are the model's stress and strain tensors, and the colon means that matching tensor components are multiplied and summed {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`. This stress-strain pair is used because it is energy-conjugate in the reference configuration: its contraction gives the stress power on the finite-element mesh. The next chapter explains this through the stress-power balance.

The inner integral adds the local stress-strain power density over the chosen tissue region, and the outer integral accumulates it through the cardiac cycle. Choosing a different region $\Omega_j$ therefore gives the work carried by a different part of the wall.

This regional stress-strain work is the model-derived reference used in the thesis. Its value is that it exposes the local stress and strain fields hidden clinically, including fibre-direction effects and the two-sided loading of the septum.

Clinically, however, neither full field is directly visible. Stress is the harder one: measuring it would require force sensors throughout the wall, at multiple depths and orientations, and that instrumentation would damage the tissue and alter the mechanics it was meant to measure.

Strain is more accessible because imaging can track motion, but recovering the full strain field is still hard. Strain is not just where the wall moved; it is how neighbouring tissue points moved relative to each other. Speckle-tracking echocardiography can in principle resolve all three principal strain directions — longitudinal, circumferential, and radial — and tagged cardiac MRI can recover an even richer regional strain tensor {cite}`tee2013imaging,voigt2015definitions`. In current clinical practice, however, only longitudinal strain is routinely endorsed: the recent ASE/EACVI consensus reports that radial strain performs poorly in inter-vendor reproducibility studies and that circumferential strain has no evidence base for routine clinical use {cite}`thomas2025clinical`.

These measurements therefore remain incomplete relative to a local tensor strain field, which would require accurate tracking through the wall, through the cardiac cycle, and in all relevant directions including shear and transmural variation. In routine pressure-strain myocardial-work analysis the deformation input is consequently longitudinal strain, meaning how much a segment shortens or lengthens along the long axis {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive,thomas2025clinical`. Longitudinal strain is a defensible default because most of the visible wall motion is long-axis shortening, even though the fibres themselves spiral well off the long axis. The other measurable strain components are not absent from the heart's mechanics, only from the clinical work index, because there is no settled consensus on how to combine them. **[TO ADD: forthcoming results-chapter analysis of how circumferential, radial, and through-wall strain might enrich the pressure-strain work proxy.]**

The clinical pressure-strain proxy is attractive because it uses these measurable pieces. Building on earlier pressure-length loop ideas {cite}`forrester1974pressure_length,tyberg1974segmental`, the established pressure-strain myocardial-work method was developed mainly for the LV. It combines measured or estimated LV pressure with longitudinal strain from strain Doppler or speckle tracking {cite}`urheim2005regional,russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`. Recent RV applications follow the same basic construction, using RV pressure and RV longitudinal or free-wall longitudinal strain {cite}`wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`.

The resulting loop is a practical regional work index, but it carries two mechanical substitutions. Chamber pressure stands in for local myocardial stress, and one measured strain component stands in for the full deformation state.

The pressure substitution is already non-trivial. Cavity pressure is a scalar boundary traction. Myocardial stress is a tensor field inside a curved, thick, actively contracting wall, so some internal stress components can be several times larger than cavity pressure without violating force balance. The biventricular setting adds the irregular RV shape and a septum loaded from both sides. The central question is therefore: how much of the model-resolved regional stress-strain work does this measurable pressure-longitudinal-strain approximation retain?

This is where the finite-element model becomes useful. It creates a controlled setting where the hidden mechanical variables are available and the mechanical simplifications can be separated. A proxy that fails in this setting is more fundamentally limited by mechanics, not only by image-tracking error, pressure scaling, or patient-to-patient variation. A proxy that performs well identifies a region where the mechanical assumptions are at least internally plausible.

The relevant background therefore has two strands. On the clinical side, pressure-strain methods make regional myocardial work measurable from pressure and longitudinal deformation, with a mature LV literature and a newer RV literature in pulmonary hypertension {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive,wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`. On the modeling side, finite-element studies have used fibre or tensor stress-strain loops to compute regional myocardial work in LV, biventricular, and dyssynchrony settings {cite}`finsberg2019assessment,wang2012myocardial,ahmadbakir2018multiphysics,craine2024successful`. These studies build on established descriptions of myocardial anisotropy, fibre architecture, and biventricular mechanics {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive,bayer2012novel,finsberg2018efficient,regazzoni2022cardiac`.

The gap addressed here is the bridge between these strands: how a clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain proxy behaves when it is checked against model-resolved tensor work in the RV free wall and in the septum. The RV free wall matters because RV pressure-strain indices are now being proposed clinically as more informative than RV strain alone {cite}`lakatos2024right`. The septum matters because no single chamber pressure is an unambiguous stress surrogate there.

To create this test, the simulations raise RV systolic pressure while retaining the same biventricular geometry and material model. This pressure-loading sequence is motivated by pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), where the pulmonary circulation becomes abnormally high-resistance and the RV must pump against a higher pressure load {cite}`humbert2022esc`. As RV pressure rises, the balance between the two ventricles changes and the septum can flatten or shift toward the LV {cite}`tello2019tapse,finsberg2019computational`. The sequence represents an isolated mechanical feature of PAH, not full clinical PAH progression.

Those two regional questions make the problem biventricular rather than simply a LV repeat. The RV free wall tests whether adjacent RV pressure works in a thinner, more load-sensitive chamber. The septum is different again. It is one piece of tissue with an LV-facing surface and an RV-facing surface. Under healthy loading, LV systolic pressure is several times higher than RV systolic pressure because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. As RV pressure is raised, the tested septal pressures no longer represent the same mechanical assumption.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow a transversely isotropic Holzapfel-Ogden active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. The coupling is bidirectional: the 0D model supplies cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the circulation. This gives physiologically realistic LV and RV pressure-volume dynamics while retaining access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The geometry used throughout is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters.

The thesis computes model-resolved tensor work density throughout this system, decomposes it by anatomical region, and compares it with pressure choices for the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy. For the free walls, the natural proxy uses the adjacent chamber pressure. For the septum, the tested choices include LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, mean pressure, and a through-wall weighted pressure. Because geometry, material properties, and activation are held fixed, the comparison isolates the pressure-for-stress approximation and asks where it remains mechanically meaningful.

The main result is that the pressure-strain proxy behaves best where its mechanical assumptions are simplest. In the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio reasonably well. In the septum, the pressure choice is not a harmless detail. LV pressure can rank cases well along one corrected pressure-loading sweep, but it does not give the most stable septal density estimate. Transmural pressure captures the pressure difference that helps determine septal flattening, but it performs poorly when the question is how much septal work density is present relative to the free walls. Two-sided pressure choices such as mean pressure or a through-wall weighted pressure better reflect the septum as shared tissue loaded by both cavities. The pressure sweep is therefore a loading-path sensitivity test, not a direct simulation of clinical PAH progression.
