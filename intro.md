+++{"part" : "abstract"}
Clinical pressure-strain work combines an estimated cavity pressure with regional longitudinal strain to form a myocardial work index. The method is well established for the left ventricle, but its mechanical interpretation in the right ventricle (RV) and interventricular septum is less clear. This thesis tests the approximation in a controlled biventricular finite-element model coupled to a zero-dimensional circulation. The model supplies a reference stress-strain work density, computed from the local contraction of second Piola-Kirchhoff stress with the time derivative of Green-Lagrange strain, and compares it with clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain loop areas.

The comparison is performed in the LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum across a 16-case RV pressure-loading sweep. The sweep caps the RV end-diastolic pressure used during inverse unloading at 5 mmHg to avoid an unphysically small RV reference in the severe cases, while keeping the imaged end-diastolic geometry as the unloading target. In the free walls, the adjacent-pressure longitudinal proxy preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio and tracks RV free-wall stress-strain work across the sweep.

The septum behaves differently. As shared tissue with LV pressure on one side and RV pressure on the other, it has no unambiguous scalar pressure. Transmural pressure is mechanically meaningful for the net pressure difference but performs poorly as a work-density proxy — it gives the weakest ranking correlation and the largest septum/free-wall magnitude error in the primary sweep. LV pressure gives the best fixed-candidate magnitude result for the longitudinal proxy; the fibre-aligned diagnostic favours two-sided pressure choices. The thesis therefore reaches a shared-wall conclusion, not a universal septal pressure formula: pressure-strain work is most defensible in one-pressure free walls, and septal work in RV pressure overload depends on the validation metric and reference-state assumptions.
+++

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. For each beat the left ventricle displaces roughly 70 mL of blood against a systemic arterial pressure near 100 mmHg, which amounts to about one joule of mechanical work per stroke; the right ventricle moves the same volume into the pulmonary circulation in parallel, at a fraction of that pressure.

At a resting heart rate the heart does something on the order of $10^5$ J of work over the course of a day, continuously, for a lifetime. That sounds like a lot, but in food-energy terms it is roughly the calories in a quarter of a banana — a small daily budget for a muscle that never rests. Small as that daily budget is, understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it is not a peripheral question about cardiac function. It is close to the centre of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

Before reducing that beat to equations, it is useful to fix the anatomical language used throughout the thesis. Figure {numref}`fig-anatomy` shows the four-chamber heart, with the left ventricle, right ventricle, and interventricular septum forming the biventricular regions that are central here.

The heart wall is layered. The *endocardium* lines the blood-facing cavity surface, the *epicardium* covers the outside, and the *myocardium* is the thick muscular layer between them {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. When this thesis refers to myocardial work, it means work in that muscular wall tissue. The endocardium and epicardium are not treated as separate materials in the finite-element model. Instead, they are used as anatomical surfaces: pressure acts on the endocardium, external support acts on the epicardium, and the two surfaces help define both the model regions — LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum — and the local muscle-fibre directions.

With those labels in place, what this thesis models is not only a pressure-volume circuit. It is a deforming biventricular finite-element heart, driven through filling, contraction, ejection, and relaxation by a coupled circulation model. The two parts exchange volumes and pressures at every step: the circulation prescribes the target cavity volumes that the ventricles must match, and the finite-element solver returns the cavity pressures that enforce those volumes, which in turn advance the circulation. On top of those chamber-level signals, the finite-element model also gives the stress and strain fields inside the myocardial wall.

{numref}`fig-intro-beating-heart` shows what this looks like in practice: ten snapshots spaced through one simulated beat at the baseline calibration, read left-to-right along the top row and continued along the bottom row. The mesh is cut open so both cavities and the septum between them are visible. Colour is the local displacement from the end-diastolic position, so blue marks tissue that has barely moved — where the cycle starts and ends — and warm colours mark the regions undergoing the largest wall motion at peak contraction in the middle of the sequence. The geometry and cycle phases shown here are exactly the ones the pressure-volume loops below are computed on, and the regional work densities reported later in the thesis are integrals over the same mesh.

Returning to the heart as a pump, the basic mechanical calculation is pressure-volume work. The pressure-volume (PV) diagram, originally an engineering indicator diagram introduced by James Watt and John Southern in 1796 to measure steam-engine work, was later brought into cardiac physiology by Otto Frank's volume-pressure description of ventricular work {cite}`cardwell1971fromwatt,frank1899grundform,lohff1999first`.

Frank's pressure-volume description applies to either ventricle, because the heart is more accurately a small set of pumps in series than a single one. The left ventricle drives oxygenated blood through the systemic circulation against a high arterial pressure, while the right ventricle drives the same volume per beat through the pulmonary circulation against a much lower pressure. The two atria act as smaller priming chambers that fill the ventricles before each beat, and every chamber traces its own pressure-volume loop. The atria are not the focus of this thesis, and the systemic and pulmonary flows have to balance over time, so two loops, one per ventricle, are enough to carry the chamber-level argument.

The beat in each panel of {numref}`fig-intro-pv-loops` starts in the bottom right corner and moves counter-clockwise. The ventricle first fills at low pressure as blood arrives from its atrium, then contracts at nearly fixed volume so pressure rises rapidly, then ejects blood as the outlet valve opens and volume falls, and finally relaxes back to low pressure before the next filling phase begins. Both ventricles trace this same four-phase cycle, but the left-ventricular peak pressure reaches roughly five times the right-ventricular peak because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. The positive area enclosed by each loop is the stroke work of that ventricle,

$$
SW = \left|\oint P\,dV\right|,
$$

the mechanical work transferred from the ventricular wall to the blood during one beat. These are chamber-level numbers: they tell us how much work each ventricle did as a pump, but not where in the wall that work was carried.

Where in the wall that work is actually being done — how it is distributed across the tissue rather than just summed at the cavity boundary — is a harder question, because work inside tissue is a local stress-strain process. Stress is force per area carried inside the myocardium: how strongly a small piece of tissue is being pulled, compressed, or sheared by its neighbours. Strain is deformation: how much that same piece of tissue has shortened, stretched, thickened, or sheared relative to a reference shape. In one dimension, mechanical work per unit volume is stress times a change in strain. In three dimensions, the same idea becomes a tensor contraction: local mechanical power — the time derivative of work — is stress acting through the local rate of deformation. If myocardial stress and the complete strain tensor field could be measured throughout the wall, this is the physical quantity describing local mechanical work.

Pressure-volume work and stress-strain work are two ways of writing the same mechanical exchange at different levels: pressure-volume work describes the chamber boundary, while stress-strain work describes the tissue. The finite-element model used in this thesis is the large-deformation version of this tissue-level description {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`, and it lets us ask the corresponding local question by estimating stress and strain at points throughout the myocardial mesh. Because stress power is local, it can be accumulated over a chosen tissue region and over one cardiac cycle. Changing the region changes whether the reported value is whole-myocardium work, LV free-wall work, RV free-wall work, or septal work. The mathematical definition, including the reference-configuration notation and sign convention, is given in {ref}`sec-work-definitions`.

This regional stress-strain work is the model-side reference used in the thesis. It is not patient-level ground truth, but it gives an internally consistent mechanical reference within the simulation. The model exposes the stress field, the full deformation state, fibre-direction effects, and the two-sided loading of the septum in a way that routine clinical measurements do not.

Clinically, however, neither full field is directly visible. Stress is the harder one: measuring it would require force sensors throughout the wall, at multiple depths and orientations, and that instrumentation would damage the tissue and alter the mechanics it was meant to measure. Strain is more accessible because imaging can track motion, but recovering the full local strain tensor — through the wall, through the cycle, and in all directions — is still beyond routine practice. Clinical pressure-strain work therefore takes longitudinal strain, the long-axis-shortening component, as its deformation input {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive,thomas2025clinical`. The strain-direction landscape and the longitudinal-strain definition used in this thesis are developed in {ref}`sec-work-definitions`.

The clinical pressure-strain proxy is attractive because it uses these measurable pieces. Building on earlier pressure-length loop ideas {cite}`forrester1974pressure_length,tyberg1974segmental`, the established pressure-strain myocardial-work method was developed mainly for the LV. It combines measured or estimated LV pressure with longitudinal strain from strain Doppler or speckle tracking {cite}`urheim2005regional,russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`. Recent RV applications follow the same basic construction, using RV pressure and RV longitudinal or free-wall longitudinal strain {cite}`wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`.

The resulting loop is a practical regional work index, but it carries two mechanical substitutions. Chamber pressure stands in for local myocardial stress, and one measured strain component stands in for the full deformation state.

The pressure substitution is already non-trivial. Cavity pressure is a scalar boundary traction. Myocardial stress is a tensor field inside a curved, thick, actively contracting wall, so some internal stress components can be several times larger than cavity pressure without violating force balance. The biventricular setting adds the irregular RV shape and a septum loaded from both sides. The central question is therefore: how much of the finite-element regional stress-strain work does this measurable pressure-longitudinal-strain approximation retain?

This is where the finite-element model becomes useful. It creates a controlled setting where quantities not available from routine clinical measurements are available from the simulation, and where the mechanical simplifications can be separated. Within this simulation, a proxy that fails points to a mechanical limitation in the approximation, not only to image-tracking error, pressure scaling, or patient-to-patient variation. A proxy that performs well identifies a region where the mechanical assumptions are at least internally plausible.

The relevant background therefore has two strands. On the clinical side, pressure-strain methods make regional myocardial work measurable from pressure and longitudinal deformation, with a mature LV literature and a newer RV literature in pulmonary hypertension {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive,wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`. On the modeling side, finite-element studies have used fibre and full stress-strain loops to compute regional myocardial work in LV, biventricular, and dyssynchrony settings {cite}`finsberg2017phd,wang2012myocardial,ahmadbakir2018multiphysics,craine2024successful`. These studies build on established descriptions of myocardial anisotropy, fibre architecture, and biventricular mechanics {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive,bayer2012novel,finsberg2018efficient,regazzoni2022cardiac`.

The gap addressed here is the bridge between these strands: how a clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain proxy behaves when it is checked against model-side stress-strain work in the RV free wall and in the septum. The RV free wall matters because RV pressure-strain indices are now being proposed clinically as more informative than RV strain alone {cite}`lakatos2024right`. The septum matters because no single chamber pressure is an unambiguous stress surrogate there.

To create this test, the simulations raise RV systolic pressure while retaining the same biventricular geometry and material model. This pressure-loading sequence is motivated by pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), where the pulmonary circulation becomes abnormally high-resistance and the RV must pump against a higher pressure load {cite}`humbert2022esc`. As RV pressure rises, the balance between the two ventricles changes and the septum can flatten or shift toward the LV {cite}`tello2019tapse,finsberg2019computational`. The sequence represents an isolated mechanical feature of PAH, not full clinical PAH progression.

That distinction matters for the absolute high-pressure magnitudes and for the inferred unloaded reference configuration. The model's reference configuration — the stress-free state from which strain is measured — has to be inferred from the imaged end-diastolic geometry by an inverse-unloading procedure: a hypothetical zero-pressure shape that, when re-pressurized to the assumed end-diastolic pressure, reproduces the imaged geometry. The inferred reference is therefore sensitive to the assumed end-diastolic pressure — a higher value forces the procedure to make the stress-free cavity smaller. Because the sweep does not include RV hypertrophy, curvature remodelling, or regional passive material adaptation, severe RV loading can otherwise force the inverse-unloading step to explain the pressure rise by making the stress-free RV cavity unrealistically small. The primary analysis therefore caps the RV end-diastolic pressure used during inverse unloading at 5 mmHg, while leaving the end-diastolic mesh target, geometry, material law, and coupled beat simulation fixed. The result is a bounded fixed-geometry loading experiment, not a corrected patient-level PAH model.

Those two regional questions make the problem biventricular rather than simply a LV repeat. The RV free wall tests whether adjacent RV pressure works in a thinner, more load-sensitive chamber. The septum is different again. It is one piece of tissue with an LV-facing surface and an RV-facing surface. Under healthy loading, LV systolic pressure is several times higher than RV systolic pressure because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. As RV pressure is raised, the tested septal pressures no longer represent the same mechanical assumption.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow a transversely isotropic Holzapfel-Ogden active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. The coupling is bidirectional: the 0D model supplies cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the circulation. This gives physiologically realistic LV and RV pressure-volume dynamics while retaining access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The geometry used throughout is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`. The stress-free reference configuration used by the mechanics solver is obtained by inverse-unloading of this end-diastolic shape, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters.

The thesis computes stress-strain work density throughout this system, decomposes it by anatomical region, and compares it with pressure choices for the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy. For the free walls, the natural proxy uses the adjacent chamber pressure. For the septum, the tested choices include LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, mean pressure, nearest-side pressure, and a through-wall weighted pressure. Because geometry, material properties, and activation are held fixed, the comparison isolates the pressure-for-stress approximation and asks where it remains mechanically meaningful.

The main result is that the pressure-strain proxy behaves best where its mechanical assumptions are simplest. In the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio reasonably well. In the septum, the pressure choice is not a harmless detail. Transmural pressure captures the pressure difference that helps determine septal flattening, but it performs poorly as a scalar work-density pressure in the primary capped-reference sweep. LV pressure gives the best fixed-candidate longitudinal magnitude result, while model-side fibre strain favours mean and through-wall weighted pressure.
