title: Pressure Proxies for Septal Mechanical Work

+++{"part" : "abstract"}
The interventricular septum is mechanically ambiguous: it is simultaneously a wall of the left ventricle and a wall of the right ventricle, loaded by both chambers at once. In diseases like pulmonary arterial hypertension, where right ventricular systolic pressure rises toward systemic levels, this ambiguity has direct consequences for how we estimate the mechanical work of the septal myocardium. Clinical pressure-strain loop methods assign left ventricular pressure to the septum, implicitly assuming that LV pressure governs septal mechanics. This assumption is reasonable in health, but it is not obvious that it holds when RV pressure is substantially elevated. This thesis investigates the question using a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model of a biventricular heart, extending the LV-only modeling framework of Finsberg et al. to the biventricular case. Ground-truth internal mechanical work is computed from the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain fields throughout the cardiac cycle, and compared against pressure-strain proxies using three pressure definitions: LV pressure, RV pressure, and transmural pressure. Simulations are run for a synthetic healthy baseline geometry derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model, a patient-specific healthy anatomy, and a patient-specific pulmonary arterial hypertension case. The main finding is that transmural pressure tracks septal mechanical work more faithfully than LV pressure alone in the PAH case, while both proxies perform equivalently in health.

# Pressure Proxies for Septal Mechanical Work

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. Each beat displaces roughly seventy milliliters of blood against an arterial pressure near a hundred millimeters of mercury, which amounts on the order of one joule of mechanical work performed on the blood per stroke. Multiply by a resting heart rate and the ventricle is doing something on the order of a hundred kilojoules of work over the course of a day, and it has to do so continuously for a lifetime. Understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it, is therefore not a peripheral question about cardiac function - it is close to the center of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

The modern framework for thinking about cardiac work quantitatively begins with Suga, who showed that the area enclosed by the left ventricular pressure-volume loop, $W_\text{ext} = \oint P \, dV$, corresponds closely to the oxygen consumed by the myocardium over the same beat {cite}`suga1979total`. The PV-loop area had been understood for decades as the external hydraulic work done by the ventricle on the blood, but Suga's observation elevated it to something more fundamental. Mechanical work became the currency in which cardiac energetics could be expressed: the quantity that links what the muscle is doing mechanically to what it is costing metabolically. Efficiency, oxygen demand, the energetic cost of contraction — all of these could be formulated as statements about work. For the left ventricle in standard conditions, this framework has been the basis of cardiac energetics ever since.

There is, however, a quieter way of describing the same quantity. In the language of continuum mechanics, the work done by a deforming elastic body is the volume integral of stress against the rate of strain. For the myocardium over one beat,

$$
W_\text{int} = \int_0^T \!\! \int_\Omega \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor, $\mathbf{E}$ is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor, and the colon denotes the tensor double contraction $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} = S_{ij} \dot{E}_{ij}$. Energy balance for the ventricle as a whole then guarantees that this integral is, up to boundary terms accounting for how the ventricle is supported externally, the same quantity as the cavity PV-loop work. What a catheter measures at the endocardial surface as $\oint P \, dV$, and what a mechanics calculation computes inside the tissue as $\int \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X}$, are two views of a single thing: mechanical work done by the myocardium over a beat. Suga's scalar is the boundary reading of a field.

This equivalence is the reason regional work is a sensible thing to talk about at all. If the chamber-level number is the integral of a density distributed through the tissue, then restricting that integral to part of the wall gives the fraction of the total work generated in that part. A finite element simulation, which carries $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ at every quadrature point, has direct access to this density and can therefore decompose the whole-heart work along whatever anatomical partition is of interest — and, because the tensor double contraction carries work in every deformation direction, along whichever components of stress and strain the decomposition calls for.

In clinical practice the mechanics that would give access to such a field is not available: myocardial stress cannot be measured in an intact beating heart. What is measurable non-invasively is regional strain, most reliably the longitudinal component, obtained from speckle-tracking echocardiography. Combining this with a non-invasive estimate of chamber pressure yields the pressure-strain loop method, in which the regional contribution to stroke work is approximated as the area of a loop traced in pressure–strain space {cite}`russell2012novel`. This is Suga's construction carried from the whole chamber down to the segment, with strain standing in for the local volume change and the chamber pressure standing in for the full stress tensor. It is, by construction, a proxy for the tensor integral — useful in the many clinical situations where one is the only accessible thing and the other is not, but a proxy all the same.

Whether that proxy is faithful is an empirical question, and for the left ventricle it has been answered computationally by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`. Their analysis establishes two things that matter here. First, the full tensor integral computed throughout the LV myocardium agrees with the cavity PV-loop area to within modeling error, an empirical confirmation — in real tissue rather than only in principle — of the boundary–field equivalence stated above. Second, the pressure-strain proxy, while it captures relative regional trends and correctly flags dysfunctional segments in patients with left bundle branch block, underestimates the absolute tensor work by roughly an order of magnitude, a ratio that held near-identically across their healthy and diseased cases. Two separate approximations sit inside the proxy: a scalar chamber pressure standing in for the full stress tensor, and a single strain component (in clinical practice the longitudinal one from speckle-tracking echocardiography) standing in for the full Green-Lagrange strain. The present thesis addresses the first — which pressure — while keeping the strain-component reduction that the clinical proxy already uses. Finsberg et al. also identified what was naturally missing from their study, and what the present work is about: the right ventricle, and in particular its shared boundary with the left.

The most natural regional partition of the biventricular heart is the one anatomy suggests — a left ventricular part and a right ventricular part. But the two chambers are not mechanically isolated from each other. The wall between them is a single piece of tissue with a left ventricular face and a right ventricular face, and it is mechanically loaded from both sides at once. Deciding how to assign this wall to an LV/RV regional decomposition is not a technicality; it determines which pressure one associates with it and, in consequence, which pressure the proxy formula applies across it. In healthy physiology, where the LV systolic pressure is roughly five times higher than the RV systolic pressure {cite}`bastos2020invasive`, the decision is mostly cosmetic — the full LV pressure drives deformation across the wall, and the RV pressure on the other face is a small perturbation. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, RV systolic pressure rises toward LV systolic pressure and in severe cases can approach or exceed it, the wall flattens and may bow toward the left, and the question of what pressure actually represents the mechanical load on the tissue stops being cosmetic {cite}`vonk2013right,naeije2017pulmonary`.

This is what the thesis is about. Using a coupled computational model of the biventricular heart, we compute the tensor work density throughout the myocardium, take Suga's chamber-level scalar apart into its regional contributions, and ask how faithfully the clinical pressure-strain proxy — which is the only way this same decomposition could be attempted in a patient — tracks the tensor ground truth across a regional partition of the heart into left and right, with the shared wall between them. The work quantity is well defined in simulation; the proxy is the object under test. The principal question is whether the proxy reproduces the tensor work across the full biventricular anatomy, and if not, where the mismatch concentrates.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow the Holzapfel-Ogden orthotropic active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue. The two models are coupled bidirectionally at each time step: the 0D model prescribes cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match at equilibrium, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the 0D valve and arterial dynamics. This produces physiologically realistic pressure and volume waveforms for both ventricles simultaneously while giving full access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The framework is a biventricular extension of the left-ventricular work of Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment,balaban2017high`, constructed specifically so that the regional decomposition of work across the shared wall becomes a quantitative question rather than a modeling convention. Three simulation cases are studied: a synthetic healthy baseline built from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, a patient-specific healthy anatomy reconstructed from cardiovascular magnetic resonance, and a patient with confirmed pulmonary arterial hypertension whose 0D parameters are tuned to the measured hemodynamics.

The main result is that the correspondence between proxy and tensor work is strong across most of the LV and RV free walls in all three cases, but concentrates its error on the wall shared between the two chambers. That error is small in health, where the LV pressure and the transmural pressure difference are numerically close, and becomes significant in pulmonary arterial hypertension, where they are not. A proxy that uses the transmural pressure rather than the LV pressure across this shared wall tracks the tensor work more faithfully in the diseased case and is equivalent to the LV-pressure proxy in the healthy case, suggesting a concrete modification of the standard clinical formula that is specific to RV pressure overload.

The document is organized as follows. The first chapter states the scientific question in its formal mathematical form, defining the two work quantities being compared and the proxy approximation under study. The second chapter describes the computational model: geometry and fiber architecture, the Holzapfel-Ogden hyperelastic law, the active stress formulation, and the zero-dimensional circulation with its coupling strategy. The third chapter recounts the implementation and the numerical challenges encountered in building the model. The fourth chapter covers the parameter identification used to match the circulation model to each case's measured hemodynamics. The fifth chapter presents the simulation results and the comparison between proxy and tensor work across the regional decomposition. The sixth chapter discusses the findings in their mathematical and clinical context, the limitations of the present study, and the directions the framework opens up for further work.
