title: Pressure Proxies for Biventricular Myocardial Work
---

+++{"part" : "abstract"}
Clinical pressure-strain work indices are increasingly used to interpret regional ventricular function, but their mechanical meaning in the right ventricle and interventricular septum is uncertain. This thesis uses a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model as a controlled test system for the pressure-strain approximation. The model provides the stress and strain fields that are hidden in patients, allowing myocardial work density to be computed from the tensor contraction between second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain. This model-resolved tensor work density is compared with clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain indices in the LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum during a controlled RV pressure-loading sweep. The free walls test the setting in which the proxy should work best: one wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity pressure. The septum tests the harder biventricular case, because it is shared tissue loaded by LV pressure on one side and RV pressure on the other. The results show that adjacent cavity pressure gives a mechanically meaningful proxy in the free walls, including the RV free wall, where the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index tracks model-resolved tensor work density far better than longitudinal strain alone. The septum is different. Its tensor work contains a larger non-fibre contribution than the free walls, and the pressure assigned to the septum is not a harmless convention. LV pressure can rank one corrected pressure sweep well, but two-sided pressure choices, especially mean or through-wall weighted pressure, preserve septal work-density magnitudes more robustly. Transmural pressure is meaningful for septal force balance and shape, but it is a poor proxy for septal work-density magnitude in these simulations. The main conclusion is therefore not a universal septal pressure formula, but a mechanical distinction: pressure-strain indices are most defensible in one-pressure free walls, while septal work in RV pressure overload should be interpreted as a shared-wall problem whose proxy ranking depends on the loading path and on the quantity being validated.

# Introduction

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. Each beat displaces roughly 70 mL of blood against an arterial pressure near 100 mmHg, which amounts to about one joule of mechanical work per stroke. Multiply by a resting heart rate and the ventricle does something on the order of $10^5$ J of work over the course of a day, continuously, for a lifetime. Understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it is therefore not a peripheral question about cardiac function. It is close to the centre of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

Before reducing that beat to equations, it is useful to see the object being studied. The model in this thesis is not only a pressure-volume circuit. It is a deforming biventricular finite-element heart, driven through filling, contraction, ejection, and relaxation by a coupled circulation model. The pressure and volume traces are the visible bookkeeping of the beat; the hidden part is the stress and strain field moving through the wall.

The basic pump calculation is pressure-volume work. This way of seeing work began in engineering before it entered cardiology. The pressure-volume (PV) diagram—originally called an "indicator diagram"—was invented in 1796 by James Watt and his engineer John Southern to measure the efficiency of steam engines. The diagram was valuable because it showed how much useful work the engine produced during a stroke. Because this tool provided such a massive competitive advantage, Watt kept it a closely guarded trade secret for decades {cite}`cardwell1971fromwatt`. Otto Frank later brought the same pressure-volume idea into cardiac physiology through his volume-pressure description of ventricular work {cite}`frank1899grundform,lohff1999first`.


The beat starts in the bottom right corner of {numref}`fig-intro-lv-pv-loop` and moves counter-clockwise. The left ventricle (LV), the main chamber that pumps oxygenated blood to the body, first fills at low pressure. It then contracts at nearly fixed volume, so pressure rises rapidly until the aortic valve opens. Blood is ejected into the systemic circulation as volume falls, and finally the ventricle relaxes at low volume before the next filling phase begins. The positive area enclosed by this loop is the left-ventricular stroke work,

$$
SW = \left|\oint P\,dV\right|,
$$

which is the mechanical work transferred from the ventricular wall to the blood during one beat. Inside the wall, the corresponding question involves stress and strain: force carried by the tissue, and local change in length or shape. A pressure-strain index keeps only the measurable parts of that picture, using chamber pressure and regional deformation. The question in this thesis is whether such an index can recover the corresponding regional work-density distribution.

In a finite-element model the same myocardial stress work can be written as a field quantity. The work inside a deforming elastic body is the volume integral of stress against the rate of strain, so for the myocardium over one beat,

$$
W_\text{int} = \int_0^T \!\! \int_\Omega \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor and $\mathbf{E}$ is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`. Cauchy stress is the more intuitive force-per-current-area stress, but the finite-element model is written on the reference mesh. Pulling the same stress power back to that mesh gives $\boldsymbol{\sigma}:\mathbf{d}\,dv = \mathbf{S}:\dot{\mathbf{E}}\,dV$, where $\mathbf{d}$ is the rate-of-deformation tensor. Thus $\mathbf{S}:\dot{\mathbf{E}}$ is not a different kind of work; it is the reference-configuration form of the same mechanical power. The next chapter defines these tensors and derives the energy identity connecting the tensor integral to the cavity pressure-volume work, including the boundary terms introduced by the external support of the numerical model.

This distinction between boundary work and field work is the computational core of the thesis. The chamber loop is available from pressure and volume alone, and its enclosed area gives stroke work. The tensor integral $\int \mathbf{S}:\dot{\mathbf{E}}\,d\mathbf{X}$ is available only if the stress and strain fields inside the tissue are known. A finite-element simulation provides those fields at quadrature points throughout the myocardium, which means that the whole-heart work can be decomposed into LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum, and also into fiber, sheet, and wall-normal contributions. The model therefore gives access to a mechanical quantity that cannot be measured directly in a patient.

The clinical pressure-strain proxy is attractive precisely because this tensor field is not available in vivo. Building on earlier pressure-length loop ideas {cite}`forrester1974pressure_length,tyberg1974segmental`, pressure-strain methods combine an estimated chamber pressure with regional strain measured non-invasively, usually as a longitudinal strain component rather than as the full strain tensor or a fibre-projected strain {cite}`urheim2005regional,russell2012novel,voigt2015definitions,abawi2022noninvasive`. The resulting loop is a practical regional work index, but it carries two mechanical compromises: chamber pressure is not local myocardial stress, and a single measured strain component is not the full deformation state. The next chapter gives the clinical and computational background for these approximations; here the important point is that the proxy is useful because it is measurable, not because it is identical to tensor work.

This is where the finite-element model becomes useful. The point is not to replace clinical measurement with a more elaborate black box. It is to test the proxy in a setting where the hidden mechanical variables are known. The model used here is idealized, but it is mechanically consistent: the same coupled system produces the pressures, volumes, strains, stresses, and internal work. This makes it a favourable test of the pressure-strain approximation. If the proxy cannot reproduce the model-resolved work-density distribution in this controlled setting, where there is no image-tracking error, no uncertain pressure scaling, and no patient-to-patient variation in geometry or material properties, then it is unlikely to recover true regional tissue work density more reliably in vivo. Conversely, if it performs well in a region, the model identifies where the proxy's mechanical assumptions are at least internally plausible.

Clinical pressure-strain work indices and finite-element stress-strain work have both been studied before. The specific question here is how the pressure approximation behaves in a biventricular model when the RV free wall and septum are treated as explicit regional targets. The RV free wall is important because RV pressure-strain indices are now being proposed clinically as more informative than RV strain alone {cite}`lakatos2024right`. The septum is important because no single chamber pressure is the obvious stress surrogate there.

Those two regional questions are what make the problem biventricular rather than simply an LV repeat. In an LV-only setting, LV pressure is the natural pressure scale. In the RV free wall, RV pressure is the corresponding choice, but it is still worth testing because the RV is thinner, more load-sensitive, and much less studied with tensor-work references. The interventricular septum is different again: it is one piece of tissue with an LV-facing surface and an RV-facing surface. Under healthy loading, LV systolic pressure is several times higher than RV systolic pressure, because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. That pressure ratio is why the RV-side pressure may be a smaller correction in health, although treating the septum as mostly LV-pressure loaded is still a mechanical assumption rather than something Kovacs et al. tested. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, RV pressure rises and the septum flattens as the balance between the two ventricles changes {cite}`humbert2022esc,tello2019tapse,finsberg2019computational`. Then LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure, mean pressure, and through-wall weighted pressure no longer represent the same mechanical assumption.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow a transversely isotropic Holzapfel-Ogden active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. The coupling is bidirectional: the 0D model supplies cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the circulation. This gives physiologically realistic LV and RV pressure-volume dynamics while retaining access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The geometry used throughout is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters.

The thesis computes model-resolved tensor work density throughout this system, decomposes it by anatomical region, and compares it with pressure choices for the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy. For the free walls, the natural proxy uses the adjacent chamber pressure. For the septum, the tested choices include LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, mean pressure, and a through-wall weighted pressure. This is deliberately a controlled computational comparison. The pressure sweep should not be read as a full simulation of clinical PAH progression, because real disease changes pressure, volume, wall thickness, geometry, stiffness, activation, and remodelling together. Here the purpose is narrower: isolate the pressure-for-stress approximation and ask where it remains mechanically meaningful.

The main result is that the pressure-strain proxy behaves best where its mechanical assumptions are simplest. In the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio reasonably well. In the septum, the pressure choice is not a harmless detail. LV pressure can rank cases well along one corrected pressure-loading sweep, but it does not give the most stable septal density estimate. Transmural pressure captures the pressure difference that helps determine septal flattening, but it performs poorly when the question is how much septal work density is present relative to the free walls. Two-sided pressure choices such as mean pressure or a through-wall weighted pressure better reflect the septum as shared tissue loaded by both cavities. The pressure sweep should therefore be read as a loading-path sensitivity test, not as a direct simulation of clinical PAH progression.

This introduction motivates the computational comparison and explains why the RV free wall and septum are the key biventricular tests of the pressure-strain approximation.
