---
title: Pressure Proxies for Biventricular Myocardial Work
---

+++{"part" : "abstract"}
Clinical pressure-strain work indices are increasingly used to interpret regional ventricular function, but their mechanical meaning in the right ventricle (RV) and interventricular septum is uncertain. This thesis uses a coupled finite element and zero-dimensional circulation model as a controlled test system for the pressure-strain approximation. The model provides the stress and strain fields that are hidden in patients, allowing myocardial work density to be computed from the tensor contraction between second Piola-Kirchhoff stress and Green-Lagrange strain. This model-resolved tensor work density is compared with clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain indices in the left-ventricular (LV) free wall, RV free wall, and septum during a controlled RV pressure-loading sweep. The free walls test the setting in which the proxy should work best: one wall loaded mainly by one adjacent cavity pressure. The septum tests the harder biventricular case, because it is shared tissue loaded by LV pressure on one side and RV pressure on the other. The results show that adjacent cavity pressure gives a mechanically meaningful proxy in the free walls, including the RV free wall, where the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index tracks model-resolved tensor work density far better than longitudinal strain alone. The septum is different. Its tensor work contains a larger non-fibre contribution than the free walls, and the pressure assigned to the septum is not a harmless convention. LV pressure can rank one corrected pressure sweep well, but two-sided pressure choices, especially mean or through-wall weighted pressure, preserve septal work-density magnitudes more robustly. Transmural pressure is meaningful for septal force balance and shape, but it is a poor proxy for septal work-density magnitude in these simulations. The main conclusion is therefore not a universal septal pressure formula, but a mechanical distinction: pressure-strain indices are most defensible in one-pressure free walls, while septal work in RV pressure overload should be interpreted as a shared-wall problem whose proxy ranking depends on the loading path and on the quantity being validated.

# Introduction

The heart is, at the most reductive level, a mechanical pump. Each beat displaces roughly 70 mL of blood against an arterial pressure near 100 mmHg, which amounts to about one joule of mechanical work per stroke. At a resting heart rate the ventricle does something on the order of $10^5$ J of work over the course of a day, continuously, for a lifetime. Understanding how that work is produced, how much of it there is, and how it is distributed across the muscle that produces it is therefore not a peripheral question about cardiac function. It is close to the centre of what the heart, as an organ, is doing.

Before reducing that beat to equations, it is useful to fix the anatomical language used throughout the thesis. Figure {numref}`fig-anatomy` shows the four-chamber heart, with the left ventricle, right ventricle, and interventricular septum forming the biventricular regions that are central here.

The heart wall is layered. The *endocardium* lines the blood-facing cavity surface, the *epicardium* covers the outside, and the *myocardium* is the thick muscular layer between them {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. When this thesis refers to myocardial work, it means work in that muscular wall tissue. The thin endocardial and epicardial layers are important anatomical surfaces, but they are not treated as separate volumetric materials in the finite-element model; they appear as boundary surfaces where pressure, support conditions, and through-wall fibre directions are defined.

With those labels in place, the object modeled in this thesis is not only a pressure-volume circuit. It is a deforming biventricular finite-element heart, driven through filling, contraction, ejection, and relaxation by a coupled circulation model. From the circulation model we obtain chamber pressures and volumes; from the finite-element model we also obtain the stress and strain fields inside the myocardial wall.

Returning to the heart as a pump, the basic mechanical calculation is pressure-volume work. This way of seeing work began in engineering before it entered cardiology. The pressure-volume (PV) diagram—originally called an "indicator diagram"—was invented in 1796 by James Watt and his engineer John Southern to measure the efficiency of steam engines. The diagram was valuable because it showed how much useful work the engine produced during a stroke. Because this tool provided such a massive competitive advantage, Watt kept it a closely guarded trade secret for decades {cite}`cardwell1971fromwatt`. Otto Frank later brought the same pressure-volume idea into cardiac physiology through his volume-pressure description of ventricular work {cite}`frank1899grundform,lohff1999first`.

The beat starts in the bottom right corner of {numref}`fig-intro-lv-pv-loop` and moves counter-clockwise. The left ventricle (LV), the main chamber that pumps oxygenated blood to the body, first fills at low pressure. It then contracts at nearly fixed volume, so pressure rises rapidly until the aortic valve opens. Blood is ejected into the systemic circulation as volume falls, and finally the ventricle relaxes at low volume before the next filling phase begins. The positive area enclosed by this loop is the left-ventricular stroke work,

$$
SW = \left|\oint P\,dV\right|,
$$

which is the mechanical work transferred from the ventricular wall to the blood during one beat. This is a chamber-level number: it tells us how much work the ventricle did as a pump, but not where in the wall that work was carried.

The finite-element model lets us ask the corresponding local question. At each point in the myocardium, mechanical power is computed from stress and strain rate: the force state carried by the tissue, and the local change in its length or shape. Integrated through the wall and over one beat, this gives the model-resolved internal work,

$$
W_\text{int} = \int_0^T \!\! \int_\Omega \mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}} \, d\mathbf{X} \, dt,
$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor and $\mathbf{E}$ is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor {cite}`holzapfel2000nonlinear`. This particular stress-strain pair is not arbitrary: it is the natural energy-conjugate pair for the reference-mesh finite-element formulation used here. The next chapter explains why from the stress-power balance. For the introduction, the important point is simpler: this integral is the stress-strain work computed from the full three-dimensional model.

This field-resolved work is the reference quantity for the thesis. It is the regional counterpart of pressure-volume work: instead of asking how much work a whole chamber exchanges with the blood, it asks how that work is distributed through the myocardial wall.

Clinically, however, this is exactly the quantity that is hard to see. Direct measurement of myocardial stress would require force sensors throughout the wall, at multiple depths and orientations; such instrumentation would damage the tissue and alter the mechanics it was meant to measure. The full three-dimensional strain tensor is also not a routine clinical measurement. It contains several directional stretches and shear components through the wall, not one shortening curve. What is usually available is much more limited: a measured or estimated cavity pressure, and a reduced deformation signal, most often longitudinal strain.

The clinical pressure-strain proxy is attractive because it uses these measurable pieces. Building on earlier pressure-length loop ideas {cite}`forrester1974pressure_length,tyberg1974segmental`, the established pressure-strain myocardial-work method was developed mainly for the LV: LV pressure, measured or estimated, is combined with longitudinal strain from strain Doppler or speckle tracking {cite}`urheim2005regional,russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`. Recent RV applications follow the same basic pressure-longitudinal-strain construction, using RV pressure and RV longitudinal or free-wall longitudinal strain {cite}`wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`. The resulting loop is a practical regional work index, but it carries two mechanical substitutions: chamber pressure stands in for local myocardial stress, and one measured strain component stands in for the full deformation state. The pressure substitution is not a small notational change. Cavity pressure is a scalar boundary traction, while myocardial stress is a tensor field inside a curved, thick, actively contracting wall; some internal stress components can therefore be several times larger than the cavity pressure without violating force balance. The central question is therefore: how much of the model-resolved regional stress-strain work does this measurable pressure-strain approximation retain?

This is where the finite-element model becomes useful. The point is not to replace clinical measurement with a more elaborate black box. It is to test the proxy in a setting where the hidden mechanical variables are known. The model used here is idealized, but it is mechanically consistent: the same coupled system produces the pressures, volumes, strains, stresses, and internal work. This makes it a favourable test of the pressure-strain approximation. If the proxy cannot reproduce the model-resolved work-density distribution in this controlled setting, where there is no image-tracking error, no uncertain pressure scaling, and no patient-to-patient variation in geometry or material properties, then its interpretation as regional tissue work should be treated cautiously in vivo. Conversely, if it performs well in a region, the model identifies where the proxy's mechanical assumptions are at least internally plausible.

The relevant background therefore has two strands. On the clinical side, pressure-strain methods make regional myocardial work measurable from pressure and longitudinal deformation, with a mature LV literature and a newer RV literature in pulmonary hypertension {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive,wang2022apply,lakatos2024right`. On the modeling side, finite-element studies have already used fibre or tensor stress-strain loops to compute regional myocardial work in LV, biventricular, and dyssynchrony settings {cite}`finsberg2019assessment,wang2012myocardial,ahmadbakir2018multiphysics,craine2024successful`. The gap addressed here is the bridge between these strands: how a clinical-style pressure-longitudinal-strain proxy behaves when it is checked against model-resolved tensor work in the RV free wall and in the septum. The RV free wall is important because RV pressure-strain indices are now being proposed clinically as more informative than RV strain alone {cite}`lakatos2024right`. The septum is important because no single chamber pressure is an unambiguous stress surrogate there.

To create this test, the simulations include a sequence in which RV systolic pressure is increased while the same biventricular geometry and material model are retained. This pressure-loading sequence is motivated by pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), a disease in which the pulmonary circulation becomes abnormally high-resistance and the RV must pump against a higher pressure load {cite}`humbert2022esc`. As RV pressure rises, the balance between the two ventricles changes and the septum can flatten or shift toward the LV {cite}`tello2019tapse,finsberg2019computational`. The sequence should therefore be read as an isolated mechanical feature of PAH, not as a simulation of full clinical PAH progression.

Those two regional questions are what make the problem biventricular rather than simply an LV repeat. In an LV-only setting, LV pressure is the natural pressure scale. In the RV free wall, RV pressure is the corresponding choice, but it is still worth testing because the RV is thinner, more load-sensitive, and much less studied with tensor-work references. The interventricular septum is different again: it is one piece of tissue with an LV-facing surface and an RV-facing surface. Under healthy loading, LV systolic pressure is several times higher than RV systolic pressure, because the pulmonary circulation is normally a low-pressure system {cite}`kovacs2009pulmonary`. As RV pressure is raised, LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure, mean pressure, and through-wall weighted pressure no longer represent the same mechanical assumption.

The computational framework couples a three-dimensional finite element model of the biventricular myocardium to a zero-dimensional closed-loop model of the full cardiovascular circulation {cite}`regazzoni2022cardiac`. The 3D solver uses the FEniCSx library {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx` through the fenicsx-pulse package {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, and the mechanics follow a transversely isotropic Holzapfel-Ogden active-stress formulation for cardiac tissue {cite}`holzapfel2009constitutive`. The coupling is bidirectional: the 0D model supplies cavity volumes that the 3D mechanics must match, and the resulting cavity pressures advance the circulation. This gives physiologically realistic LV and RV pressure-volume dynamics while retaining access to the stress and strain fields inside the tissue. The geometry used throughout is a synthetic biventricular baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`, and different loading regimes are imposed through the circulation parameters.

The thesis computes model-resolved tensor work density throughout this system, decomposes it by anatomical region, and compares it with pressure choices for the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy. For the free walls, the natural proxy uses the adjacent chamber pressure. For the septum, the tested choices include LV pressure, RV pressure, transmural pressure $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, mean pressure, and a through-wall weighted pressure. Because geometry, material properties, and activation are held fixed, the comparison isolates the pressure-for-stress approximation and asks where it remains mechanically meaningful.

The main result is that the pressure-strain proxy behaves best where its mechanical assumptions are simplest. In the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV work-density ratio reasonably well. In the septum, the pressure choice is not a harmless detail. LV pressure can rank cases well along one corrected pressure-loading sweep, but it does not give the most stable septal density estimate. Transmural pressure captures the pressure difference that helps determine septal flattening, but it performs poorly when the question is how much septal work density is present relative to the free walls. Two-sided pressure choices such as mean pressure or a through-wall weighted pressure better reflect the septum as shared tissue loaded by both cavities. The pressure sweep should therefore be read as a loading-path sensitivity test, not as a direct simulation of clinical PAH progression.

The document is organized as follows. This introduction motivates the computational comparison and explains why the RV free wall and septum are the key biventricular tests of the pressure-strain approximation. The chapter on mechanical work and the scientific question gives the background for the pressure-strain proxy, states the comparison in mathematical form, separates total tensor work from work density, and derives the whole-heart energy identity used to verify the computation. The model chapters describe the geometry, fibre architecture, Holzapfel-Ogden hyperelastic law, active stress formulation, zero-dimensional circulation model, and coupling strategy. The implementation chapter recounts the numerical and software challenges encountered in building the model. The calibration chapter describes the parameter identification used to generate the controlled pressure-loading path. The results chapter compares pressure choices for the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy with the model-resolved tensor work density. The discussion chapter interprets the findings and their limitations, and the final chapter gives the conclusion and future work.
