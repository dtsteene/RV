# Discussion

The main result of this thesis has two parts. First, within the model, the pressure-longitudinal-strain index behaves sensibly in the ventricular free walls, including the RV free wall where clinical validation is much less developed than in the LV. Second, the same idea becomes pressure-choice dependent in the interventricular septum, which is shared by two cavities. This distinction explains why the LV and RV free-wall ratios were captured reasonably well by adjacent pressure, while the septal result depended on what was meant by "best". If the target was case ordering along one pressure-loading sweep, transmural and LV-like pressure could perform well. If the target was preserving septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls, the final tangent-longitudinal result moved away from transmural pressure and toward LV pressure and nearby LV--RV mixtures.

This is a more useful conclusion than the simpler statement that transmural pressure is best for the septum. Transmural pressure, $p_\text{LV}-p_\text{RV}$, is mechanically meaningful. It describes the pressure difference across the septum and is therefore relevant for septal displacement, curvature, and the D-shaped left ventricle seen in RV pressure overload {cite}`ryan1985echocardiographic,vonk2013right`. Tissue work density, however, is not determined only by the net pressure difference across the wall. Work density is local stress times local strain, and the septum is loaded and constrained by both ventricles. A small pressure difference does not imply that the septal tissue is unloaded; it can mean that both sides are pressurized. The results of this thesis support that interpretation: transmural pressure ranked the final tangent-longitudinal sweep best, but it was the poorest fixed candidate for septum/free-wall work-density magnitude.

## What The Free-Wall Result Means

The free-wall result is the clearest support for the pressure-strain idea in this model. For the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserved the LV/RV work-density ratio better than the alternatives. This is exactly where the clinical pressure-strain approximation should be expected to work best. A free wall has one blood pressure acting on its endocardial surface, one main cavity whose volume change it contributes to, and no second cavity pressing on the opposite side. Cavity pressure is still not a local stress tensor, but it is at least a physically sensible scalar load.

The large absolute difference between pressure-strain loop area and stress-strain work density should therefore not be read as a numerical pathology. Even in an idealized pressurized cylinder, wall stress is a geometry-scaled internal stress, roughly $p r/h$ for a thin cylindrical wall, not simply the cavity pressure. In the cardiac model, active fibre stress and anisotropic wall constraint make the difference larger and more component-dependent. The important question is not whether pressure equals fibre stress. It does not. The question is whether adjacent pressure is the best available scalar load for comparing otherwise similar free-wall regions. On that more modest test, it performed well.

This result also prevents the discussion from becoming too negative. The simulations do not show that pressure-strain loops lack mechanical value. They show that the method has a domain where its assumptions are reasonable. In the free walls, adjacent pressure and longitudinal strain gave a useful model-side estimate of relative regional work-density balance, even though the absolute magnitude of the proxy remained different from the finite-element stress-strain work density. This is consistent with the clinical use of pressure-strain myocardial work as a comparative index rather than a direct measurement of regional energy in joules {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`.

The RV free-wall result is especially useful in light of the recent clinical study by Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right`. Their study used invasive pressure-volume analysis as the reference and found that RV pressure-strain myocardial work reflected RV contractility better than RV GLS or ejection fraction. This thesis cannot reproduce that exact endpoint, because the pressure sweep is not an invasive contractility experiment. What it can do is the finite-element analogue: compare the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index with finite-element RV free-wall stress-strain work density. On that model-side test, the pressure-strain index tracked the work-density reference far better than strain-only metrics. This gives the RV part of the thesis its own motivation, separate from the septal pressure-assignment problem, but it does not validate the clinical endpoint.

The bulk geometry correction tested here did not improve on adjacent pressure. The idea was to multiply pressure by a simple chamber geometry term, $\mathcal{V}_\text{cavity}/\mathcal{V}_\text{wall}$, as a clinically measurable analogue of a Laplace-type stress scale. It was a reasonable hypothesis, but in this model it either changed little or made the ratio worse. That does not mean geometry is irrelevant. It means that this particular global correction is too crude. If geometry is to improve pressure-strain work-density estimates, it likely needs to be more local: wall thickness, curvature, and possibly fibre direction need to enter, especially in the RV and septum.

## How To Understand The Septum

The septum is the harder pressure-assignment problem. It is not an LV segment that happens to sit near the RV, and it is not an RV free wall. It is shared tissue. The LV pressure acts on one side, the RV pressure acts on the other, and the deformation of the septum is constrained by both free walls and by the basal attachments. A one-pressure proxy collapses this two-sided object into a scalar.

Laplace's law helps clarify the issue rather than solve it. For a thin free wall, a common stress scale is pressure times radius divided by wall thickness. For the septum, applying the same idea with the pressure difference is natural if the question is force balance or curvature: the net pressure difference contributes to whether the septum bows into the RV or into the LV. The finite-element work density, however, is not a curvature measure. It is the stress-strain work density inside the tissue. A septum can have a reduced pressure difference and still carry substantial tissue work density because both cavities are pressurized and because the surrounding myocardium pulls and constrains it.

This is why the ratio analysis is valuable. It separates two things that are easily mixed. Transmural pressure may help explain septal shape, but it did not preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. With the final tangent-longitudinal definition, LV pressure gave the closest fixed-candidate magnitude result, and mean and through-wall weighted pressure were close behind. These are not arbitrary fitted formulas; they follow from the shared-wall picture. The simplest two-sided pressure is the average of the two cavity pressures. A slightly more detailed version uses the septal transventricular coordinate to move from LV pressure on the LV side toward RV pressure on the RV side. Their similar performance in the magnitude tests suggests that, for this question, moving back into the physical LV--RV pressure interval mattered more than the exact weighting. It does not imply a universal septal pressure formula: in the corrected sweep, transmural pressure still gave the strongest correlation for case ordering.

The through-wall analysis should still be read cautiously. The mesh used here was not designed as a high-resolution septal-layer model. In places the septum is only a few elements thick, so dividing it into LV-side, middle, and RV-side layers is a diagnostic tool rather than a definitive transmural measurement. A higher-resolution septal mesh would be needed to make strong claims about the detailed stress profile through the septal wall. The current result is stronger at the coarser level: the septum behaves as shared tissue, and one-sided pressure choices should be interpreted as metric-dependent simplifications rather than complete local stress surrogates.

## Why The Pressure Sweep Is A Sensitivity Test

The pressure sweep should not be described as simulated PAH progression. That would overstate what was done. Real PAH changes many quantities together: pulmonary vascular resistance, RV pressure, RV volume, wall thickness, septal curvature, passive stiffness, fibrosis, contractility, activation timing, and possibly valve function. The main sweep in this thesis varies the circulation while keeping the anatomy and material parameters fixed. It is an isolated loading-path experiment.

The inverse-unloading results make this distinction sharper rather than merely semantic. The unloaded reference is inferred by asking which stress-free shape reproduces the image-derived end-diastolic mesh under the specified end-diastolic pressures. That is the appropriate construction for an image mesh acquired at end diastole, but it becomes demanding when severe RV pressure is imposed on the same non-remodelled baseline anatomy. With no RV hypertrophy or regional stiffening available, the severe cases can be explained only by a larger deformation from the unloaded reference to end diastole. Because Green-Lagrange strain is measured relative to that reference, and because the Holzapfel-Ogden material stiffens exponentially with stretch, this mainly biases the high-pressure RV and RV-side septal stress-strain magnitudes upward. It does not make the pressure-strain proxy comparison meaningless; it makes the absolute severe-case RV tissue-work scale conditional on the fixed-reference model.

This does not make the sweep uninformative. It makes its interpretation narrower and cleaner. The sweep answers the question: if one fixed biventricular model is exposed to a range of RV pressure loadings, how sensitive are the pressure-strain proxy rankings to that loading path? The answer is that they are sensitive. In the final corrected sweep, transmural pressure ranked the tangent-longitudinal septal stress-strain work best, while LV pressure remained close enough to carry much of the same loading trend and gave the better magnitude result. This is not a failure of the finite element model; it is a warning about correlation-based validation.

The ratio results were less fragile in the main respect that matters for the septum: transmural pressure was poor at preserving septum/free-wall work-density magnitudes. The exact non-transmural winner did depend on strain direction. For tangent-longitudinal strain, LV pressure was best and mean or through-wall weighted pressure was close; for the fibre-aligned diagnostic, mean and through-wall weighted pressure were best. This is why the magnitude analysis should be given more weight than the sweep ranking. Correlations across a pressure sweep depend on the path through parameter space. A regional ratio inside one simulation asks a more direct question about the proxy's mechanical meaning.

## Why A Simplified Model Still Helps

The fixed-geometry model is not a clinical cohort, but it is still informative. It is a controlled test of one approximation in a hierarchy of approximations. Pressure-strain work reduces regional mechanics to cavity pressure and longitudinal strain. Laplace-type reasoning reminds us that even a passive wall stress scale should include geometry and thickness. The finite-element model then resolves the additional mechanics that simple pressure or Laplace descriptions cannot: thick-wall equilibrium, fibre anisotropy, active fibre stress, support conditions, irregular RV geometry, and septal sharing between two cavities.

In the model, everything around the pressure-for-stress replacement is known: the pressure waveforms are exact model outputs, the strain fields are computed on the same mesh, the region labels are fixed, and the stress-strain work density is computed from the same stress and strain fields. There is no echocardiographic tracking error, no uncertain cuff-pressure scaling, no missing RV pressure estimate, and no patient-to-patient variation in stiffness or wall thickness. If adjacent pressure works in the free walls under these conditions, that supports the mechanical idea behind the proxy. If the septal pressure choice remains ambiguous under these conditions, that is also meaningful. It says the ambiguity is not only a measurement problem and not only a patient-selection problem. It is already present in the mechanics of replacing a two-sided stress state with one scalar pressure. Real patients add more complexity; they do not remove this one.

## What The Energy Balance Proves

The pressure-volume loop has a strong mechanical foundation even before oxygen consumption enters the discussion. The loop area is the external work done on the blood. Suga's pressure-volume area framework adds potential energy in a time-varying elastance model and relates that whole-ventricle mechanical quantity to oxygen consumption {cite}`suga1979total`. For this thesis, however, the pressure-volume connection should not be asked to do too much. A simple model can also have a closed energy budget. Energy consistency is necessary, but it is not by itself a reason to believe a regional stress field is realistic.

The energy balance in this thesis proves a narrower and important point: the stress-strain work postprocessing is consistent with the finite-element boundary-value problem that generated the pressures and deformations. Over the whole model heart, internal stress power and boundary pressure-volume power are two ways of writing the same mechanical power balance, up to the small support terms. That makes stress-strain work a coherent model-side reference for regional decomposition. It does not prove that the model has captured every biological source of energetic cost, every patient-specific material property, or the uniquely correct in-vivo stress field.

This is also the cleanest way to read the Finsberg work that this thesis extends. Finsberg's thesis {cite}`finsberg2017phd` asked for a regional work measure that, when summed over the ventricle, agrees with stroke work from the pressure-volume loop. The finite-element stress-strain work came close to that whole-ventricle accounting, while the pressure-strain loops were much smaller in absolute magnitude. At the same time, the pressure-strain loops still gave useful relative information about regional efficiency and wasted work. That distinction is central here. A proxy can be useful for ranking regions or cases without being a literal regional energy in joules. This is why {ref}`chap-results` separates correlation along a sweep from preservation of regional work-density ratios inside a simulation.

Pressure-strain work indices, PV-loop work, pressure-volume area, Laplace wall-stress estimates, and finite-element stress-strain work are related ideas, but they answer different questions. The value of the finite-element model is that it exposes where the clinical simplification is mechanically aligned with the model and where it is not.

## Clinical Meaning

Clinically, the most defensible message is conservative but not negative. For the RV free wall, adjacent RV pressure-strain work appears mechanically reasonable within the model as a relative regional work-density measure, which is consistent with recent clinical interest in RV myocardial work indices. For the septum, especially in RV pressure overload, the pressure assigned to the tissue is a modelling choice with measurable consequences. Transmural pressure may rank one loading sweep well, while LV pressure or LV--RV mixtures may better preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. This is not a clinical replacement rule, and it does not mean that one septal pressure formula should immediately replace the clinical convention. It means that RV free-wall pressure-strain work is a meaningful target for further validation, while septal myocardial work indices in PAH should be interpreted with caution because the pressure choice is not a harmless implementation detail.

A clinical workflow also has measurement limits. LV pressure can be approximated from cuff systolic blood pressure and a normalized pressure waveform in common pressure-strain workflows {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`. RV systolic pressure can often be estimated from tricuspid regurgitation velocity, but the estimate is not always available and can be inaccurate {cite}`humbert2022esc`. Wall thickness and curvature are measurable in principle, especially by CMR, but adding them to a proxy may introduce enough measurement noise to erase the gain from a more physical formula. The failure of the simple bulk geometry correction in this thesis is a useful reminder: a more complicated proxy is not automatically better.

## Limitations

The pressure-loading sweep should be interpreted as a loading sensitivity experiment, not as simulated PAH progression. Only the circulation parameters were varied, while geometry, wall thickness, material parameters, activation, and boundary conditions were held fixed. Real PAH involves remodelling: RV hypertrophy, chamber dilation, septal flattening, fibrosis, altered contractility, and changes in activation may all occur together {cite}`vonk2013right,vonk2017relationship,humbert2022esc`. These changes would alter both the stress-strain work distribution and the performance of any pressure-strain proxy. The sweep therefore tests the pressure-for-stress approximation under controlled loading changes; it does not predict how myocardial work evolves clinically during disease progression. The late unloading checks add a more specific version of this limitation: in the severe fixed-geometry cases, the inferred unloaded RV reference can become too small, so absolute RV and RV-side septal stress-strain work densities are probably biased upward.

Two sensitivity checks support that direction of bias. First, an unloading-only regional stiffness sweep made the RV reference state more plausible without changing the fixed ED target: with the strongest tested regional scaling, RV unloaded volume increased from 55.6% to 80.2% of ED volume in sPAP22, from 34.3% to 63.3% in sPAP60, and from 20.9% to 48.6% in sPAP95. The severe case remained far from a fully convincing reference state, so this should not be treated as a corrected production model. It shows that regional passive properties matter and that uniform fixed material exaggerates the RV reference deformation under high pressure.

Second, exploratory patient-geometry simulations reinforced why fixed anatomy is a restrictive assumption. The PAH mesh had smaller cavities but a larger wall volume than the healthy mesh: RV EDV was 74.2 mL instead of 94.4 mL, while wall volume was 165.7 mL instead of 129.1 mL. At nearly identical RV systolic pressure in the sPAP55 pair, RV work density was lower in the PAH mesh than in the healthy mesh, 3.19 kPa versus 5.77 kPa. At the sPAP65 label, the PAH mesh reached a higher RV systolic pressure than the healthy mesh but still had lower RV work density, 4.08 kPa versus 4.75 kPa. These runs are not clean enough to replace the main result: the healthy high-pressure cases did not all complete, the selected cases used end-diastolic region tagging, and geometry, loading, and numerical robustness changed together. Their value is as a direction check. They suggest that realistic PAH remodelling can reduce the RV tissue-work density that the fixed UKB sweep assigns to severe RV loading, while leaving the qualitative proxy message intact: RV free-wall adjacent pressure remains meaningful, and septal magnitude still behaves like a two-sided loading problem rather than a transmural-pressure-only problem.

The finite-element stress-strain work is an internally consistent reference, not a patient-level ground truth. It depends on the chosen Holzapfel-Ogden material parameters, fibre architecture, active tension law, and activation timing. Holding these fixed is useful because it isolates the pressure-assignment question, but it also removes biological variation that could change regional stress and work. The results should therefore be read as a mechanical test of proxy structure within one model family, not as a claim that the absolute stress or work fields are uniquely determined in real myocardium.

The basal support remains a modelling limitation. The production setup used Robin springs together with a partial basal displacement constraint, rather than a fully free or anatomically complete atrioventricular support. The constraint was deliberately weaker than a full clamp, and displacement-field checks indicate that tangential sliding remained. However, the tested Robin-only production variants did not converge during end-diastolic inflation, so this study does not quantify how alternative stable basal support formulations would change the regional work fields. The small net Robin work suggests that the springs are not the dominant energy term, but the boundary condition should still be treated as part of the model definition.

Septal conclusions should be separated into coarse regional and through-wall claims. The integrated septal work-density comparisons are supported by the mesh study, although high-pressure septal values were the most mesh-sensitive quantities. The through-wall and nearest-side pressure diagnostics are less definitive: the septum is only a few elements thick in places, so layer-wise interpretation is diagnostic rather than a resolved transmural stress measurement. A refined septal mesh would be needed before making strong claims about local septal stress gradients.

The finite-element model removes several clinical measurement errors, but it does not replace clinical validation. It provides a known mechanical environment in which pressure-strain proxies can be compared with finite-element stress-strain work. That is useful for identifying where the proxy is mechanically aligned with the model and where it becomes ambiguous. It does not show that the same proxy predicts oxygen consumption, invasive contractility, symptoms, or outcomes in patients. Those require separate clinical validation with measured or well-estimated RV pressure.

Finally, the pressure-strain proxy itself uses longitudinal strain averaged over regions. Clinical speckle tracking can report several anatomical strain directions, but the myocardial-work workflows tested here are based on longitudinal strain {cite}`voigt2015definitions,sade2023current,abawi2022noninvasive`. The fibre-aligned calculations in the results should therefore be read only as mechanics diagnostics: they help separate the pressure-choice question from the strain-direction question, but they are not routine clinical measurements.

The principal-strain diagnostic sharpens this limitation. In the model, longitudinal, radial, and circumferential directions were made into an orthonormal anatomical frame before the alternative pressure-strain proxies were computed. Even in that favourable setting, the three scalar normal strains do not reconstruct the full strain tensor, because the shear terms in that frame are missing. Orthogonalizing the directions can define a clean frame, but it cannot infer those missing tensor components. A true principal-strain proxy would require full local three-dimensional strain information.

As supplementary model-side checks, geometric radial, circumferential, and principal-shortening pressure-strain proxies were evaluated in the high-resolution sweep. They did not provide a clean replacement for longitudinal strain. Radial strain preserved the free-wall LV/RV magnitude ratio well, but it correlated poorly with RV free-wall stress-strain work across the sweep and only modestly with septal stress-strain work. Circumferential strain behaved reasonably in the free walls, but in the septum its signed pressure-strain density often had the opposite sign from the free-wall circumferential proxy. Principal shortening showed that the dominant deformation direction is closer to fibre than to longitudinal strain in the septum, but using that scalar did not remove the pressure-choice problem. This supports a conservative interpretation: richer strain measurements are valuable, but choosing another fixed clinical strain component is not enough to recover the tensor deformation field or remove the pressure-for-stress and shared-septum problems.
