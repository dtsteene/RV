# Discussion

The main result of this thesis has two parts. First, the pressure-longitudinal-strain index behaves sensibly in the ventricular free walls, including the RV free wall where clinical validation is much less developed than in the LV. Second, the same idea becomes pressure-choice dependent in the interventricular septum, which is shared by two cavities. This distinction explains why the LV and RV free-wall ratios were captured reasonably well by adjacent pressure, while the septal result depended on what was meant by "best". If the target was case ordering along one pressure-loading sweep, LV-like pressure could perform well. If the target was preserving septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls, a two-sided pressure scale such as mean or through-wall weighted pressure was more stable.

This is a more useful conclusion than the simpler statement that transmural pressure is best for the septum. Transmural pressure, $P_\text{LV}-P_\text{RV}$, is mechanically meaningful. It describes the pressure difference across the septum and is therefore relevant for septal displacement, curvature, and the D-shaped left ventricle seen in RV pressure overload. But tissue work density is not determined only by the net pressure difference across the wall. Work density is local stress times local strain, and the septum is loaded and constrained by both ventricles. A small pressure difference does not imply that the septal tissue is unloaded; it can mean that both sides are pressurized. The results of this thesis support that interpretation: transmural pressure was a poor proxy for septum/free-wall work-density magnitudes in both the old and corrected datasets, while mean or through-wall weighted pressure was consistently better.

## What The Free-Wall Result Means

The free-wall result is the clearest support for the pressure-strain idea in this model. For the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserved the LV/RV work-density ratio better than the alternatives. This is exactly where the clinical pressure-strain approximation should be expected to work best. A free wall has one blood pressure acting on its endocardial surface, one main cavity whose volume change it contributes to, and no second cavity pressing on the opposite side. Cavity pressure is still not a true local stress tensor, but it is at least a physically sensible scalar load.

This result also matters because it prevents the discussion from becoming too negative. The simulations do not show that pressure-strain loops are useless. They show that the method has a domain where its assumptions are reasonable. In the free walls, adjacent pressure and longitudinal strain gave a useful estimate of regional work-density balance, even though the absolute magnitude of the proxy remained different from the model-resolved tensor work density. This agrees with the general clinical use of myocardial work imaging: the value is often not the absolute energy in joules, but the comparison between regions or between patients.

The RV free-wall result is especially useful in light of the recent clinical study by Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right`. Their study used invasive pressure-volume analysis as the reference and found that RV pressure-strain myocardial work reflected RV contractility better than RV GLS or ejection fraction. This thesis cannot reproduce that exact endpoint, because the pressure sweep is not an invasive contractility experiment. What it can do is the finite-element analogue: compare the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index with model-resolved RV free-wall tensor work density. On that test, the pressure-strain index tracked the tensor-density reference far better than strain-only metrics. This gives the RV part of the thesis its own motivation, separate from the septal pressure-assignment problem.

The bulk geometry correction tested here did not improve on adjacent pressure. The idea was to multiply pressure by a simple chamber geometry term, $V_\text{cavity}/V_\text{wall}$, as a clinically measurable analogue of a Laplace-type stress scale. It was a reasonable hypothesis, but in this model it either changed little or made the ratio worse. That does not mean geometry is irrelevant. It means that this particular global correction is too crude. If geometry is to improve pressure-strain work-density estimates, it likely needs to be more local: wall thickness, curvature, and possibly fibre direction need to enter, especially in the RV and septum.

## How To Understand The Septum

The septum is the harder pressure-assignment problem. It is not an LV segment that happens to sit near the RV, and it is not an RV free wall. It is shared tissue. The LV pressure acts on one side, the RV pressure acts on the other, and the deformation of the septum is constrained by both free walls and by the basal attachments. A one-pressure proxy collapses this two-sided object into a scalar.

Laplace's law helps clarify the issue rather than solve it. For a thin free wall, a common stress scale is pressure times radius divided by wall thickness. For the septum, applying the same idea with the pressure difference is natural if the question is force balance or curvature: the net pressure difference contributes to whether the septum bows into the RV or into the LV. But the model-resolved work density is not a curvature measure. It is the stress-strain work density inside the tissue. A septum can have a reduced pressure difference and still carry substantial tissue work density because both cavities are pressurized and because the surrounding myocardium pulls and constrains it.

This is why the ratio analysis is valuable. It separates two things that are easily mixed. Transmural pressure may help explain septal shape, but it did not preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. Mean pressure and through-wall weighted pressure did better. These are not arbitrary fitted formulas; they follow from the shared-wall picture. The simplest two-sided pressure is the average of the two cavity pressures. A slightly more detailed version uses the septal transventricular coordinate to move from LV pressure on the LV side toward RV pressure on the RV side. Both performed similarly in the magnitude tests, which suggests that the important step is not the exact weighting but the inclusion of both pressures.

The through-wall analysis should still be read cautiously. The mesh used here was not designed as a high-resolution septal-layer model. In places the septum is only a few elements thick, so dividing it into LV-side, middle, and RV-side layers is a diagnostic tool rather than a definitive transmural measurement. A higher-resolution septal mesh would be needed to make strong claims about the detailed stress profile through the septal wall. The current result is stronger at the coarser level: the septum behaves as shared tissue, and a pressure choice that ignores one side of the wall is mechanically incomplete.

## Why The Pressure Sweep Is A Sensitivity Test

The pressure sweep should not be described as simulated PAH progression. That would overstate what was done. Real PAH changes many quantities together: pulmonary vascular resistance, RV pressure, RV volume, wall thickness, septal curvature, passive stiffness, fibrosis, contractility, activation timing, and possibly valve function. The main sweep in this thesis varies the circulation while keeping the anatomy and material parameters fixed. It is an isolated loading-path experiment.

This does not make the sweep useless. It makes its interpretation narrower and cleaner. The sweep answers the question: if one fixed biventricular model is exposed to a range of RV pressure loadings, how sensitive are the pressure-strain proxy rankings to that loading path? The answer is that they are sensitive. The old handover data, where LV pressure fell with RV pressure, made the transmural longitudinal proxy look best for septal correlations. The corrected data, where LV pressure was more stable, made the LV-pressure longitudinal proxy correlate best. This flip is not a failure of the finite element model; it is a warning about correlation-based validation.

The ratio results were less fragile. In both old and corrected data, transmural pressure was poor at preserving septum/free-wall work-density magnitudes, while mean or through-wall weighted pressure was better. This is why the magnitude analysis should be given more weight than the sweep ranking. Correlations across a pressure sweep depend on the path through parameter space. A regional ratio inside one simulation asks a more direct question about the proxy's mechanical meaning.

## Why A Simplified Model Still Helps

The fixed-geometry model is not a clinical cohort, but it is still informative. It is a controlled test of one approximation. The pressure-strain proxy replaces a local stress tensor with a chamber pressure. In the model, everything around that replacement is known: the pressure waveforms are exact model outputs, the strain fields are computed on the same mesh, the region labels are fixed, and the tensor work density is computed from the same stress and strain fields. There is no echocardiographic tracking error, no uncertain cuff-pressure scaling, no missing RV pressure estimate, and no patient-to-patient variation in stiffness or wall thickness.

That means the model gives the proxy a cleaner setting than the clinic does. If adjacent pressure works in the free walls under these conditions, that supports the mechanical idea behind the proxy. If the septal pressure choice remains ambiguous under these conditions, that is also meaningful. It says the ambiguity is not only a measurement problem and not only a patient-selection problem. It is already present in the mechanics of replacing a two-sided stress state with one scalar pressure. Real patients add more complexity; they do not remove this one.

## Relation To Pressure-Volume Work And Suga's Framework

The pressure-volume loop has a strong mechanical foundation even before oxygen consumption enters the discussion. The loop area is the external work done on the blood. Suga's pressure-volume area framework adds potential energy in a time-varying elastance model and relates that whole-ventricle mechanical quantity to oxygen consumption {cite}`suga1979total`. In the original pooled analysis this relation was high but not perfect, with $r \approx 0.89$. For this thesis, the correlation with oxygen consumption is supporting context rather than the central reason to care about stress-strain work. The central reason is mechanical: over the whole heart, the boundary pressure-volume work and the internal stress-strain work are two ways of writing the same power balance.

This is also the cleanest way to read the Finsberg study that this thesis extends. Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` asked for a regional work measure that, when summed over the ventricle, agrees with the stroke work from the pressure-volume loop. Their finite-element stress-strain work came close to that whole-ventricle accounting, while the pressure-strain loops were much smaller in absolute magnitude. At the same time, the pressure-strain loops still gave useful relative information about regional efficiency and wasted work. That distinction is central here. A proxy can be useful for ranking regions or cases without being a literal regional energy in joules. This is why the results chapter separates correlation along a sweep from preservation of regional work-density ratios inside a simulation.

This distinction is important for the thesis. The model-resolved tensor work density used for regional comparisons is computed by the finite element model from its stress and strain fields, after dividing total regional tensor work by the known regional volume. That makes it the appropriate reference for testing pressure-strain approximations inside the same model, but it does not prove that the model has captured every biological source of energetic cost. Pressure-strain work indices, PV-loop work, pressure-volume area, and tensor stress-strain work are related ideas, but they are not interchangeable. The value of the finite element model is that it exposes where the clinical simplification is mechanically well aligned with the model and where it is not.

## Clinical Meaning

Clinically, the most defensible message is conservative but not negative. For the RV free wall, adjacent RV pressure-strain work appears mechanically reasonable as a relative regional work-density measure, which supports the recent clinical interest in RV myocardial work indices. For the septum, especially in RV pressure overload, assigning a single chamber pressure is not enough to describe the mechanical setting. A two-sided septal pressure may be more appropriate when the aim is to compare septal work-density magnitude with free-wall work density. This does not mean that mean pressure should immediately replace the clinical convention. It means that RV free-wall pressure-strain work is a meaningful target for further validation, while septal myocardial work indices in PAH should be interpreted with caution because the pressure assigned to the septum is not a harmless implementation detail.

A clinical workflow also has measurement limits. LV pressure can be approximated from cuff systolic blood pressure and a normalized pressure waveform. RV systolic pressure can often be estimated from tricuspid regurgitation velocity, but the estimate is not always available and can be inaccurate. Wall thickness and curvature are measurable in principle, especially by CMR, but adding them to a proxy may introduce enough measurement noise to erase the gain from a more physical formula. The failure of the simple bulk geometry correction in this thesis is a useful reminder: a more complicated proxy is not automatically better.

## Limitations

Several limitations follow directly from the modelling choices. The main pressure-loading sweep used one synthetic biventricular anatomy. This was useful for isolating the effect of circulation, but it is not the same as following real PAH patients across disease severity. Patient anatomy, wall thickness, cavity size, stiffness, and contractility all covary with disease. The patient-specific meshes available in this project are therefore valuable future tests, not because they automatically make the study clinical, but because they let the free-wall and septal conclusions be tested under different geometry.

The material model and activation model are also simplified. The Holzapfel-Ogden law uses fixed transversely isotropic parameters across the cases, while real PAH myocardium may be hypertrophied, fibrotic, and regionally heterogeneous. The active tension model uses a prescribed peak amplitude and a common activation waveform, rather than a detailed electrophysiology model or patient-specific contractility. These simplifications are acceptable for the controlled mechanical comparisons made here, but they limit direct clinical translation.

The basal boundary condition is another modelling limitation. The simulations used a partial basal constraint that fixes only the base-normal displacement component, together with Robin springs on the base and epicardium. This was deliberately less restrictive than a full basal clamp, and the production runs did not use a full Dirichlet condition. However, the condition is still artificial. A sensitivity test that removed the basal Dirichlet constraint entirely, while keeping the same Robin springs, was not numerically viable in the production setup: all three tested pressure cases failed during end-diastolic inflation. This supports the interpretation that the partial basal constraint is required for numerical stability. The cycle-integrated net Robin work was small compared with cavity pressure-volume work, so the support springs are unlikely to be the main driver of the regional work-density trends, but the direct effect of alternative stable basal support formulations was not quantified here.

The septal through-wall analysis is limited by mesh resolution. The current mesh is adequate for integrated regional work, but a detailed engineering study of septal stress through the wall would need a refined septal mesh or a dedicated septal idealization. The present through-wall and nearest-side pressure tests should be read as mechanistic diagnostics, not as final measurements of transmural septal stress.

Finally, the pressure-strain proxy itself uses longitudinal strain averaged over regions. Clinical speckle tracking measures longitudinal strain, not fibre strain, and clinical segment definitions may not match the finite element regions used here. The fibre-aligned calculations in the results should therefore be read only as mechanics diagnostics: they help separate the pressure-choice question from the strain-direction question, but the clinical interpretation rests on the longitudinal-strain proxy.
