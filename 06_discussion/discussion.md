# Discussion

The clinical pressure-strain work proxy works as a regional index in the ventricular free walls of this model and fails in the interventricular septum. The free walls each face one pressurized cavity, so adjacent cavity pressure is a reasonable scalar load; the septum sits between two pressurized cavities, so no single cavity pressure represents its loading. The free-wall finding also extends a clinical method better established for the LV to the less-validated RV. In the primary capped-reference sweep, transmural pressure was not a good septal work-density proxy: it correlated negatively with septal stress-strain work density along the loading sweep and gave the largest septum/free-wall magnitude error. LV pressure gave the best fixed-candidate longitudinal magnitude result, while the model-side fibre diagnostic favoured mean and through-wall weighted pressure.

There is no single right pressure for the septum, and that is the more honest answer. Transmural pressure, $p_\text{LV}-p_\text{RV}$, is mechanically meaningful — it is the pressure *difference* across the wall, which is what drives septal flattening and the D-shaped left ventricle seen in RV pressure overload {cite}`ryan1985echocardiographic,vonk2013right`. But the pressure difference is not the same thing as the load on the septal tissue. Work density is local stress times local strain, and the septum has both cavities pushing on it. A small pressure difference can mean the septum is unloaded; it can equally well mean both sides are pressurised about equally, which loads the muscle hard. Transmural pressure fails as a tissue-work proxy precisely because it confuses these two cases.

The fixed-geometry setup is the point of the experiment, not a limitation of it. Echo tracking error, cuff-pressure scaling, missing RV pressure estimates, patient-to-patient stiffness and wall-thickness variation — everything that confounds a clinical comparison is gone. So if adjacent pressure works in the free walls here, that is evidence for the mechanical idea behind the proxy; if the septal pressure choice stays ambiguous here, the ambiguity isn't a measurement problem, it is already in the mechanics of replacing a two-sided stress state with one scalar. Real patients add complexity on top of this; they don't remove it.

## What The Free-Wall Result Means

The free-wall result is the clearest support for the pressure-strain idea in this model. For the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserved the LV/RV work-density ratio better than the alternatives. This is exactly where the clinical pressure-strain approximation should be expected to work best. A free wall has one blood pressure acting on its endocardial surface, one main cavity whose volume change it contributes to, and no second cavity pressing on the opposite side. Cavity pressure is still not a local stress tensor, but it is at least a physically sensible scalar load.

The pressure-strain proxy gives smaller absolute numbers than the finite-element stress-strain work density does. That is not a problem with the model — wall stress isn't cavity pressure even for a simple inflated cylinder, where the textbook formula $pr/h$ already scales wall stress by geometry, and in the heart active fibre stress and anisotropic stiffness make the difference larger still. The question is not whether pressure equals fibre stress (it does not), but whether adjacent pressure is the best available scalar load for comparing otherwise similar free-wall regions. On that more modest test it performed well.

None of this says pressure-strain loops are mechanically empty. They have a domain where their assumptions are reasonable — the free walls — and in that domain adjacent pressure and longitudinal strain gave a useful model-side estimate of regional work-density balance, even though absolute magnitudes differed from the finite-element reference. That matches how pressure-strain myocardial work is used clinically: as a comparative regional index, not a direct measurement of energy in joules {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`.

Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right` did the patient version of this comparison. They used invasive pressure-volume analysis as their reference and found that RV pressure-strain work tracked RV contractility better than RV GLS or ejection fraction. We cannot reproduce that exact endpoint here, because the sweep is not an invasive contractility experiment, but we can do the FE analogue: compare the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index against FE RV free-wall stress-strain work density. On that test the pressure-strain index tracked the work-density reference far better than strain alone. The endpoints are not the same, but the clinical and model-side results point in the same direction, which gives the RV part of the thesis its own motivation independent of the septum.

## How To Understand The Septum

The septum is the harder pressure-assignment problem. It is not an LV segment that happens to sit near the RV, and it is not an RV free wall. It is shared tissue. The LV pressure acts on one side, the RV pressure acts on the other, and the deformation of the septum is constrained by both free walls and by the basal attachments. A one-pressure proxy collapses this two-sided object into a scalar.

Laplace's law gives intuition but doesn't answer the question. For a thin free wall, wall stress scales as $pr/h$. For the septum, applying the same idea with the pressure *difference* across the wall is natural if the question is force balance or curvature — that difference is what determines whether the septum bows into the RV or into the LV. But the FE work density is not a curvature measure. It is the stress-strain work inside the tissue, and a septum with a small pressure difference can still be carrying substantial tissue work because both cavities are pressurised and the surrounding myocardium is pulling on it.

The ratio analysis was designed to separate these two things: pressure difference (curvature) from absolute pressure (tissue loading). Transmural pressure helps explain septal *shape*, but it did not preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. With the tangent-longitudinal definition, LV pressure was the best single-pressure choice; with model-side fibre strain, mean pressure and a through-wall weighted pressure pulled closer. These are not fitted formulas — they are the simplest ways of keeping both cavities in the picture, either as their average or as a smooth interpolation across the septum's thickness. What that says is just: the important step is to keep the septum inside the two-cavity problem rather than collapsing it to a pressure difference. It does not say one of these is the right septal pressure.

The through-wall analysis should still be read cautiously. The mesh used here was not designed as a high-resolution septal-layer model. In places the septum is only a few elements thick, so dividing it into LV-side, middle, and RV-side layers is a diagnostic tool rather than a definitive transmural measurement. A higher-resolution septal mesh would be needed to make strong claims about the detailed stress profile through the septal wall. The current result is stronger at the coarser level: the septum behaves as shared tissue, and one-sided pressure choices should be interpreted as metric-dependent simplifications rather than complete local stress surrogates.

## Why The Uncapped Transmural Correlation Was An Artefact

The septum result is not as fragile as the pre-cap-versus-capped transmural sign flip first suggests. Across five sweep designs that vary the optimizer, the chamber EDPVR form, the calibration target set, and the reference-state strategy ({ref}`chap-appendix-reference-state`), the free-wall LV/RV adjacent-pressure result and the septum/free-wall magnitude pattern reproduce in every design. Adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV ratio with LV correlation above 0.96 and RV correlation above 0.85 in every sweep, and transmural pressure gives the largest septum/free-wall magnitude error in every sweep. The septum *cross-case correlation*, however, varies systematically with the inferred unloaded reference, and the variation has a clean mechanical explanation.

Inverse unloading at high RV end-diastolic pressure on a fixed UKB anatomy infers an implausibly small unloaded RV cavity, so the septum begins the cycle pre-bulged toward the RV side of the unloaded geometry. Inflating that reference back to the imaged end-diastolic shape requires a large transmural stretch of the septum, which enters the exponential stiffening regime of the Holzapfel-Ogden law. The septum then deforms much less than it otherwise would during the beat, the work it generates falls, and the deformation that does occur is dominated by the transmural direction — exactly the regime visible in the work-component decomposition where the septum's fibre share drifts downward and the sheet-normal share rises in the severe uncapped cases. In those severe cases the transmural pressure is also low because RV pressure has approached LV pressure, so a low transmural pressure and a low septal stress-strain work density co-vary across the sweep and produce the apparent positive transmural correlation. The cleanest test of this is the shared-unloaded sweep, which uses one well-behaved reference for every case and so removes the per-case reference-state variation entirely. In that sweep the transmural correlation collapses to $r=+0.006$ — indistinguishable from zero — while LV pressure and the two-sided choices retain positive correlations of about 0.68 to 0.78. The negative transmural correlation in the production capped sweep is therefore the lower bound of an artefactual range. The honest interpretation is not that the cap inverts a real signal but that the strongly positive uncapped transmural correlation was always a reference-state confound — transmural pressure simply does not track septal stress-strain work density once the inverse-unloading step is prevented from driving case-to-case reference collapse.

## A Cross-Geometry Direction Check

Exploratory simulations on a patient-specific PAH mesh — smaller cavities (RV EDV 74.2 mL versus the UKB-baseline 94.4 mL) and larger total wall volume (165.7 mL versus 129.1 mL) — test whether the fixed-anatomy result holds in a remodelled geometry. In the cap=5 shared-mask mini-sweep, the PAH geometry carried lower septal stress-strain work density than the healthy geometry at all three selected pressure levels, despite similar or higher achieved RV systolic pressure. The RV free wall was less monotone: PAH was lower at low and mid pressure, but higher in the severe case where the achieved RV pressure was also much higher. The pressure-strain proxies did not attenuate in the same way; the per-case comparison is in {ref}`chap-appendix-patient-geometry`. This is not a second proof of the production result. It is a warning that geometry can change finite-element tissue-work density in ways that a pressure-strain proxy does not automatically follow. It also suggests a compensation hypothesis worth testing in larger patient-specific simulations: PAH remodelling may reduce regional tissue work density by adding wall volume, changing curvature, and changing the reference state, while pressure-strain indices continue to track pressure and longitudinal shortening. The qualitative message of the production sweep — adjacent pressure works in the free walls, the septum is a shared-wall problem — is unchanged, but cross-geometry proxy validation is a more demanding test than the fixed-geometry sweep can give on its own.

## What The Energy Balance Proves

The whole-heart energy balance proves a narrow but important point: the stress-strain work postprocessing is consistent with the finite-element boundary-value problem that generated the pressures and deformations. Internal stress power and boundary pressure-volume power are two ways of writing the same mechanical balance, up to the small support terms. That makes stress-strain work a coherent model-side reference for regional decomposition. It does not prove that the model has captured every biological source of energetic cost, every patient-specific material property, or the uniquely correct in-vivo stress field.

This is the cleanest way to read the Finsberg work extended here {cite}`finsberg2017phd`: the finite-element stress-strain work came close to whole-ventricle accounting against stroke work, while pressure-strain loops were much smaller in absolute magnitude but still informative as relative regional indices. Pressure-strain work, PV-loop work, pressure-volume area {cite}`suga1979total`, Laplace wall-stress estimates, and finite-element stress-strain work are related ideas that answer different questions. The value of the finite-element model is that it exposes where the clinical simplification is mechanically aligned and where it is not.

## Limitations

The inverse-unloading step adds its own scope limit. The unloaded reference is the stress-free shape that the prescribed end-diastolic pressures would inflate back to the imaged geometry — a clean construction for a single end-diastolic mesh, but a demanding one when severe RV pressure is imposed on the same non-remodelled anatomy. Without RV hypertrophy or regional stiffening to share the load, the severe cases can only be explained by a larger deformation from the unloaded reference to end diastole. Green-Lagrange strain is measured relative to that reference and the Holzapfel-Ogden material stiffens exponentially with stretch, so the high-pressure RV and RV-side septal work-density magnitudes depend on which reference state is inferred.

The bias is not one-directional. Capping the RV unloading pressure reduces the inferred reference collapse, but it raises the severe-case RV and septal work densities rather than lowering them. The proxy comparison is not invalidated; the absolute severe-case RV tissue-work scale is just conditional on the fixed-reference model.

The chosen correction keeps the end-diastolic mesh as the unloading target but caps the RV end-diastolic pressure used during unloading at 5 mmHg. This bounds the case-to-case calibration drift that the uncapped severe cases would otherwise produce in the inferred unloaded RV. The round-trip check makes the effect concrete: with the cap applied, the severe case (peak RV systolic about 100 mmHg) recovers the imaged ED shape to within 3.7 mm whole-mesh mean (4.3 mm at the septum); without the cap the same case falls to 9.4 mm whole-mesh (13.9 mm at the septum, maximum 32 mm). The cap therefore restores round-trip consistency between the inverse-unloading and forward-inflation problems while leaving the beat simulation and proxy comparison in the same fixed-geometry framework.

Two alternatives were tested and rejected. Regional RV and septal stiffening reduces the unloaded collapse, but without patient-specific calibration the stiffness scale becomes an unconstrained tuning knob. Fixed-reference acute loading — reusing one unloaded reference while raising RV pressure — went non-finite on the high-pressure cases. The capped path is the cleanest bounded option: not a fully remodelled PAH model, but a calibration choice that keeps the sixteen cases comparable on the cycle side.

The regional stiffness check is a useful supporting datum here. An unloading-only regional stiffness sweep made the RV reference state more plausible without changing the fixed ED target: with the strongest tested regional scaling, RV unloaded volume increased from 55.6% to 80.2% of ED volume in the lowest-pressure case, from 34.3% to 63.3% in a mid-pressure case (peak RV systolic about 66 mmHg), and from 20.9% to 48.6% in the severe case (peak RV systolic about 100 mmHg). The severe case remained far from a fully convincing reference state, so this should not be treated as a corrected production model. It shows that regional passive properties matter and that uniform fixed material exaggerates the RV reference deformation under high pressure.

The finite-element stress-strain work is an internally consistent reference, not a patient-level ground truth. It depends on the chosen Holzapfel-Ogden material parameters, fibre architecture, active tension law, and activation timing. Holding these fixed isolates the pressure-assignment question but also removes biological variation that could change regional stress and work. The results should therefore be read as a mechanical test of proxy structure within one model family, not as a claim that the absolute stress or work fields are uniquely determined in real myocardium.

The active-tension law is also deliberately simple. It prescribes the same time course and peak active tension regardless of local fibre stretch, so it does not include the Frank-Starling mechanism by which a more filled or stretched myocardium can generate more force. This matters most for patient-specific or remodelling simulations, where geometry, preload, and contractile response should ideally change together.

The basal support remains a modelling limitation. The production setup used Robin springs together with a partial basal displacement constraint rather than a fully free or anatomically complete atrioventricular support. The constraint was deliberately weaker than a full clamp, and displacement-field checks indicate that tangential sliding remained. However, the tested Robin-only production variants did not converge during end-diastolic inflation, so alternative stable basal-support formulations were not quantified as a sensitivity for regional work fields. The small net Robin work suggests that the springs are not the dominant energy term, but the boundary condition should still be treated as part of the model definition.

Septal conclusions should be separated into coarse regional and through-wall claims. The integrated septal work-density comparisons are supported by the mesh study, although high-pressure septal values were the most mesh-sensitive quantities. The through-wall and nearest-side pressure diagnostics are less definitive: the septum is only a few elements thick in places, so layer-wise interpretation is diagnostic rather than a resolved transmural stress measurement. A refined septal mesh would be needed before making strong claims about local septal stress gradients.

The finite-element model removes several clinical measurement errors, but it does not replace clinical validation. It provides a known mechanical environment in which pressure-strain proxies can be compared with finite-element stress-strain work. That is useful for identifying where the proxy is mechanically aligned with the model and where it becomes ambiguous. It does not show that the same proxy predicts oxygen consumption, invasive contractility, symptoms, or outcomes in patients. Those require separate clinical validation with measured or well-estimated RV pressure.

Finally, the pressure-strain proxy itself uses longitudinal strain averaged over regions. Clinical speckle tracking can report several anatomical strain directions, but the myocardial-work workflows tested here are based on longitudinal strain {cite}`voigt2015definitions,sade2023current,abawi2022noninvasive`, so the fibre-aligned calculations in the results should be read as mechanics diagnostics rather than routine clinical measurements. Fibre-aligned strain improved the septal pressure comparison, especially for mean and through-wall weighted pressures, but that does not make fibre strain a clinical replacement: it shows that the longitudinal proxy loses information about the dominant tissue deformation direction. A true local strain-resolution strategy would require full three-dimensional strain information, including shear, not only a different scalar component.

## What This Thesis Shows

The thesis is a controlled experiment on one heart. The geometry is fixed throughout; only the circulation around it varies, sixteen times, in a way that takes the right ventricle from healthy loading up to severe pressure overload. The question is whether a clinical imaging method — pressure-strain work — recovers the regional work density that the finite-element model computes from stress and strain directly.

In the free walls it does. Adjacent cavity pressure (LV pressure for the LV wall, RV pressure for the RV wall) gives back the LV-to-RV work-density ratio across the whole sweep, which is exactly where this clinical method should be expected to work: each wall has one cavity pushing on it, so the pressure does represent the load. The RV piece of that is the new bit — clinical validation for RV pressure-strain work is much thinner than for the LV — and within this model it survives. For the clinic that translates into a conservative but not negative message: adjacent-pressure RV free-wall pressure-strain work is a meaningful target for further validation, in line with recent clinical interest in RV myocardial work indices.

The septum is where the method breaks. The septum sits between two pressurised cavities, and no single cavity pressure represents the load. The natural clinical pick, transmural pressure $p_\text{LV} - p_\text{RV}$, is the *worst* of the candidates — because that is a pressure *difference*, not a load, and what loads the septal muscle is the absolute pressure on each side. Two large pressures with a small difference between them still load the tissue hard, which is exactly the regime where transmural pressure underestimates the work and reverses the trend across the sweep. The honest clinical reading is that septal pressure-strain work, especially in the kind of fixed-anatomy RV pressure loading tested here, is a modelling choice with measurable consequences and should not be treated as if the pressure assignment were a harmless implementation detail.

The scope is narrower than a statement about pulmonary arterial hypertension. The heart in this simulation is one fixed anatomy across all sixteen cases — no RV hypertrophy, no septal flattening, no fibrosis, no regional stiffening — so what the experiment really tests is how the proxy responds when the circulation around an un-remodelled heart changes. Whether the same picture holds in a remodelled PAH heart is a separate and harder question that this work does not answer.

## Where This Could Go

The closest clinical setting to what the thesis actually simulates is not chronic PAH at all but acute pulmonary embolism: a fast rise in RV afterload on an essentially un-remodelled right heart, before the chronic adaptations of long-standing pulmonary hypertension have had time to happen. That match is part of why thinking about an embolism follow-up is interesting — the thesis's mechanical fingerprint (geometry held fixed, only loading changes) lines up with the clinical fingerprint of an embolism more cleanly than it does with the slow co-evolution of pressure and anatomy in PAH. Repeating the proxy comparison with circulation parameters fitted to a real PE loading transient — one patient whose RV load actually changed across a clinical event — would let the proxy be tested on a loading path the patient's own heart followed, instead of one constructed parametrically. It would also be a sharper test of the clinical question pressure-strain RV myocardial work is supposed to answer.

Patient-specific PAH simulations are a different and harder extension, because anatomy and loading change together. The exploratory cross-geometry comparison in {ref}`sec-discussion-patient-geometry` already shows that absolute work densities shift with geometry; separating that from the loading-only effect needs healthy and PAH circulations on both healthy and PAH meshes, treated as a controlled comparison rather than a paired case study. Until that is done, the present thesis stays a fixed-geometry result.

A higher-resolution septal mesh is the third natural extension. The current mesh resolves integrated regional septal work density well, but is only a few elements thick through the septum in places, which is why the through-wall and nearest-side pressure choices were treated as diagnostics rather than resolved transmural measurements. With a refined septum, the through-wall weighted pressure could be tested as a true local approximation, not a coarse average. Invasive RV pressure-volume validation, in the spirit of Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right`, is the final missing link between the RV free-wall pressure-strain proxy here and the clinical contractility question it is ultimately meant to answer.
