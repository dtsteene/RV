# Discussion

The main result has two parts. First, within the model, the pressure-longitudinal-strain index behaves sensibly in the ventricular free walls, including the RV free wall where clinical validation is much less developed than in the LV. Second, the same idea becomes pressure-choice dependent in the interventricular septum, which is shared by two cavities. This distinction explains why the LV and RV free-wall ratios were captured reasonably well by adjacent pressure, while the septal result depended on how the two-sided wall was reduced to one pressure. In the primary capped-reference sweep, transmural pressure was not a good septal work-density proxy: it correlated negatively with septal stress-strain work density along the loading sweep and gave the largest septum/free-wall magnitude error. LV pressure gave the best fixed-candidate longitudinal magnitude result, while the model-side fibre diagnostic favoured mean and through-wall weighted pressure.

This is a more useful conclusion than the simpler statement that one septal pressure is best. Transmural pressure, $p_\text{LV}-p_\text{RV}$, is mechanically meaningful. It describes the pressure difference across the septum and is therefore relevant for septal displacement, curvature, and the D-shaped left ventricle seen in RV pressure overload {cite}`ryan1985echocardiographic,vonk2013right`. Tissue work density, however, is not determined only by the net pressure difference across the wall. Work density is local stress times local strain, and the septum is loaded and constrained by both ventricles. A small pressure difference does not imply that the septal tissue is unloaded; it can mean that both sides are pressurized. The primary capped-reference result supports that interpretation: the pressure-difference proxy failed precisely where a shared-wall tissue-work proxy should account for two-sided loading.

The fixed-geometry model used here is not a clinical cohort, but it is informative because of that constraint rather than despite it. Everything around the pressure-for-stress replacement is known: the pressure waveforms are exact model outputs, the strain fields are computed on the same mesh, the region labels are fixed, and the stress-strain work density is computed from the same stress and strain fields. There is no echocardiographic tracking error, no uncertain cuff-pressure scaling, no missing RV pressure estimate, and no patient-to-patient variation in stiffness or wall thickness. If adjacent pressure works in the free walls under these conditions, that supports the mechanical idea behind the proxy; if the septal pressure choice remains ambiguous under these conditions, that ambiguity is not only a measurement problem but is already present in the mechanics of replacing a two-sided stress state with one scalar pressure. Real patients add more complexity; they do not remove this one.

## What The Free-Wall Result Means

The free-wall result is the clearest support for the pressure-strain idea in this model. For the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserved the LV/RV work-density ratio better than the alternatives. This is exactly where the clinical pressure-strain approximation should be expected to work best. A free wall has one blood pressure acting on its endocardial surface, one main cavity whose volume change it contributes to, and no second cavity pressing on the opposite side. Cavity pressure is still not a local stress tensor, but it is at least a physically sensible scalar load.

The large absolute difference between pressure-strain loop area and stress-strain work density should therefore not be read as a numerical pathology. Even in an idealized pressurized cylinder, wall stress is a geometry-scaled internal stress, roughly $p r/h$ for a thin cylindrical wall, not simply the cavity pressure. In the cardiac model, active fibre stress and anisotropic wall constraint make the difference larger and more component-dependent. The important question is not whether pressure equals fibre stress. It does not. The question is whether adjacent pressure is the best available scalar load for comparing otherwise similar free-wall regions. On that more modest test, it performed well.

This result also prevents the discussion from becoming too negative. The simulations do not show that pressure-strain loops lack mechanical value. They show that the method has a domain where its assumptions are reasonable. In the free walls, adjacent pressure and longitudinal strain gave a useful model-side estimate of relative regional work-density balance, even though the absolute magnitude of the proxy remained different from the finite-element stress-strain work density. This is consistent with the clinical use of pressure-strain myocardial work as a comparative index rather than a direct measurement of regional energy in joules {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`.

The RV free-wall result is especially useful in light of the recent clinical study by Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right`. Their study used invasive pressure-volume analysis as the reference and found that RV pressure-strain myocardial work reflected RV contractility better than RV GLS or ejection fraction. This thesis cannot reproduce that exact endpoint, because the pressure sweep is not an invasive contractility experiment. What it can do is the finite-element analogue: compare the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index with finite-element RV free-wall stress-strain work density. On that model-side test, the pressure-strain index tracked the work-density reference far better than strain-only metrics. This gives the RV part of the thesis its own motivation, separate from the septal pressure-assignment problem, but it does not validate the clinical endpoint.

## How To Understand The Septum

The septum is the harder pressure-assignment problem. It is not an LV segment that happens to sit near the RV, and it is not an RV free wall. It is shared tissue. The LV pressure acts on one side, the RV pressure acts on the other, and the deformation of the septum is constrained by both free walls and by the basal attachments. A one-pressure proxy collapses this two-sided object into a scalar.

Laplace's law helps clarify the issue rather than solve it. For a thin free wall, a common stress scale is pressure times radius divided by wall thickness. For the septum, applying the same idea with the pressure difference is natural if the question is force balance or curvature: the net pressure difference contributes to whether the septum bows into the RV or into the LV. The finite-element work density, however, is not a curvature measure. It is the stress-strain work density inside the tissue. A septum can have a reduced pressure difference and still carry substantial tissue work density because both cavities are pressurized and because the surrounding myocardium pulls and constrains it.

This is why the ratio analysis is valuable. It separates two things that are easily mixed. Transmural pressure may help explain septal shape, but it did not preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. With the final tangent-longitudinal definition, LV pressure gave the closest fixed-candidate magnitude result. With model-side fibre strain, mean and through-wall weighted pressure became the closest choices. These are not arbitrary fitted formulas; they follow from the shared-wall picture. The simplest two-sided pressure is the average of the two cavity pressures. A slightly more detailed version uses the septal transventricular coordinate to move from LV pressure on the LV side toward RV pressure on the RV side. Their performance suggests that the important step is to keep the septum inside the two-cavity loading problem rather than reducing it to pressure difference alone. It does not imply a universal septal pressure formula.

The through-wall analysis should still be read cautiously. The mesh used here was not designed as a high-resolution septal-layer model. In places the septum is only a few elements thick, so dividing it into LV-side, middle, and RV-side layers is a diagnostic tool rather than a definitive transmural measurement. A higher-resolution septal mesh would be needed to make strong claims about the detailed stress profile through the septal wall. The current result is stronger at the coarser level: the septum behaves as shared tissue, and one-sided pressure choices should be interpreted as metric-dependent simplifications rather than complete local stress surrogates.

## A Cross-Geometry Direction Check

The fixed-anatomy production sweep deliberately holds geometry constant to isolate the pressure-assignment question. Exploratory simulations on a different geometry — a patient-specific PAH mesh with smaller cavities (RV EDV 74.2 mL versus the UKB-baseline 94.4 mL) and larger total wall volume (165.7 mL versus 129.1 mL) — provide a direction check that the fixed-anatomy result cannot do on its own. These same-label PAH/healthy comparisons used the older per-case canonical tagging rather than the reference-tag postprocessing of the production sweep, so they are not numerically comparable case-by-case; their value is as an independent direction signal.

Finite-element stress-strain work density was consistently lower in the thicker PAH mesh than in the healthy mesh, even at similar or higher RV pressure. At nearly identical RV systolic pressures around 57–58 mmHg (57.6 versus 57.8 mmHg in healthy versus PAH), RV free-wall work density fell from 5.77 to 3.19 kPa and septal work density fell from 5.20 to 3.48 kPa. In a higher-pressure pair where the PAH mesh reached 72.8 mmHg RV systolic pressure against 61.5 mmHg in the healthy mesh, the PAH simulation still showed lower RV work density (4.08 versus 4.75 kPa) and lower septal work density (3.73 versus 5.08 kPa).

The pressure-strain proxies did not show the same attenuation. In the 57-mmHg pair the adjacent RV-pressure longitudinal proxy rose from 0.55 to 0.63 kPa while RV stress-strain work density fell. In the higher-pressure pair the RV proxy rose from 0.54 to 0.79 kPa even though RV work density remained lower in the PAH mesh. The septal mean-pressure proxy was less discordant but still attenuated much less than the stress-strain work density.

This suggests a compensation hypothesis worth testing in larger patient-specific simulations: PAH remodelling may reduce tissue work density by adding wall volume, changing curvature, and changing the reference state, while pressure-strain indices continue to track pressure and longitudinal shortening. If that holds, cross-geometry proxy validation is harder than the fixed-geometry sweep tested here. The qualitative message survives either way — adjacent pressure remains meaningful in the free walls within a fixed model, and septal magnitude still behaves like a two-sided loading problem rather than a transmural-only one.

## What The Energy Balance Proves

The whole-heart energy balance proves a narrow but important point: the stress-strain work postprocessing is consistent with the finite-element boundary-value problem that generated the pressures and deformations. Internal stress power and boundary pressure-volume power are two ways of writing the same mechanical balance, up to the small support terms. That makes stress-strain work a coherent model-side reference for regional decomposition. It does not prove that the model has captured every biological source of energetic cost, every patient-specific material property, or the uniquely correct in-vivo stress field.

This is the cleanest way to read the Finsberg work extended here {cite}`finsberg2017phd`: the finite-element stress-strain work came close to whole-ventricle accounting against stroke work, while pressure-strain loops were much smaller in absolute magnitude but still informative as relative regional indices. Pressure-strain work, PV-loop work, pressure-volume area {cite}`suga1979total`, Laplace wall-stress estimates, and finite-element stress-strain work are related ideas that answer different questions. The value of the finite-element model is that it exposes where the clinical simplification is mechanically aligned and where it is not.

## Clinical Meaning

Clinically, the most defensible message is conservative but not negative. For the RV free wall, adjacent RV pressure-strain work appears mechanically reasonable within the model as a relative regional work-density measure, which is consistent with recent clinical interest in RV myocardial work indices. For the septum, especially in RV pressure overload, the pressure assigned to the tissue is a modelling choice with measurable consequences. Transmural pressure may help describe septal shape, but it should not be assumed to describe septal tissue-work density. This is not a clinical replacement rule, and it does not mean that one septal pressure formula should immediately replace the clinical convention. It means that RV free-wall pressure-strain work is a meaningful target for further validation, while septal myocardial work indices in PAH should be interpreted with caution because the pressure choice is not a harmless implementation detail.

A clinical workflow also has measurement limits. LV pressure can be approximated from cuff systolic blood pressure and a normalized pressure waveform in common pressure-strain workflows {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`. RV systolic pressure can often be estimated from tricuspid regurgitation velocity, but the estimate is not always available and can be inaccurate {cite}`humbert2022esc`. Wall thickness and curvature are measurable in principle, especially by CMR, but adding them to a proxy may introduce enough measurement noise to erase any theoretical gain from a more physical formula. A more complicated proxy is not automatically better.

## Limitations

The pressure-loading sweep should be interpreted as a loading sensitivity experiment, not as simulated PAH progression. Only the circulation parameters were varied; geometry, wall thickness, material parameters, activation, and boundary conditions were held fixed. Real PAH involves remodelling — RV hypertrophy, chamber dilation, septal flattening, fibrosis, altered contractility, and changes in activation may all occur together {cite}`vonk2013right,vonk2017relationship,humbert2022esc` — and these changes would alter both the stress-strain work distribution and the performance of any pressure-strain proxy. The sweep tests the pressure-for-stress approximation under controlled loading changes; it does not predict how myocardial work evolves clinically during disease progression.

The inverse-unloading step adds its own version of this restriction. The unloaded reference is the stress-free shape that the prescribed end-diastolic pressures would inflate back to the imaged geometry — a clean construction for a single end-diastolic mesh, but a demanding one when severe RV pressure is imposed on the same non-remodelled anatomy. Without RV hypertrophy or regional stiffening to share the load, the severe cases can only be explained by a larger deformation from the unloaded reference to end diastole. Green-Lagrange strain is measured relative to that reference and the Holzapfel-Ogden material stiffens exponentially with stretch, so the high-pressure RV and RV-side septal work-density magnitudes depend on which reference state is inferred.

The bias is not one-directional. Capping the RV unloading pressure reduces the inferred reference collapse, but it raises the severe-case RV and septal work densities rather than lowering them. The proxy comparison is not invalidated; the absolute severe-case RV tissue-work scale is just conditional on the fixed-reference model.

The chosen correction keeps the end-diastolic mesh as the unloading target but caps the RV end-diastolic pressure used during unloading at 5 mmHg. This bounds the case-to-case calibration drift that the uncapped severe cases would otherwise produce in the inferred unloaded RV. The round-trip check makes the effect concrete: capped sPAP95 recovers the imaged ED shape to within 3.7 mm whole-mesh mean (4.3 mm at the septum), while uncapped sPAP95 falls to 9.4 mm whole-mesh (13.9 mm at the septum, maximum 32 mm). The cap therefore restores round-trip consistency between the inverse-unloading and forward-inflation problems while leaving the beat simulation and proxy comparison in the same fixed-geometry framework.

Two alternatives were tested and rejected. Regional RV and septal stiffening reduces the unloaded collapse, but without patient-specific calibration the stiffness scale becomes an unconstrained tuning knob. Fixed-reference acute loading — reusing one unloaded reference while raising RV pressure — went non-finite on the high-pressure cases. The capped path is the cleanest bounded option: not a fully remodelled PAH model, but a calibration choice that keeps the sixteen cases comparable on the cycle side.

The regional stiffness check is a useful supporting datum here. An unloading-only regional stiffness sweep made the RV reference state more plausible without changing the fixed ED target: with the strongest tested regional scaling, RV unloaded volume increased from 55.6% to 80.2% of ED volume in sPAP22, from 34.3% to 63.3% in sPAP60, and from 20.9% to 48.6% in sPAP95. The severe case remained far from a fully convincing reference state, so this should not be treated as a corrected production model. It shows that regional passive properties matter and that uniform fixed material exaggerates the RV reference deformation under high pressure.

The exploratory cross-geometry direction check discussed in {ref}`sec-discussion-patient-geometry` exposes a separate limitation of the fixed-anatomy production sweep: PAH-typical remodelling may attenuate finite-element tissue work density in ways the production sweep cannot test, because the geometry and material parameters are held constant. The same data motivates patient-specific simulations as future work.

The finite-element stress-strain work is an internally consistent reference, not a patient-level ground truth. It depends on the chosen Holzapfel-Ogden material parameters, fibre architecture, active tension law, and activation timing. Holding these fixed isolates the pressure-assignment question but also removes biological variation that could change regional stress and work. The results should therefore be read as a mechanical test of proxy structure within one model family, not as a claim that the absolute stress or work fields are uniquely determined in real myocardium.

The basal support remains a modelling limitation. The production setup used Robin springs together with a partial basal displacement constraint rather than a fully free or anatomically complete atrioventricular support. The constraint was deliberately weaker than a full clamp, and displacement-field checks indicate that tangential sliding remained. However, the tested Robin-only production variants did not converge during end-diastolic inflation, so alternative stable basal-support formulations were not quantified as a sensitivity for regional work fields. The small net Robin work suggests that the springs are not the dominant energy term, but the boundary condition should still be treated as part of the model definition.

Septal conclusions should be separated into coarse regional and through-wall claims. The integrated septal work-density comparisons are supported by the mesh study, although high-pressure septal values were the most mesh-sensitive quantities. The through-wall and nearest-side pressure diagnostics are less definitive: the septum is only a few elements thick in places, so layer-wise interpretation is diagnostic rather than a resolved transmural stress measurement. A refined septal mesh would be needed before making strong claims about local septal stress gradients.

The finite-element model removes several clinical measurement errors, but it does not replace clinical validation. It provides a known mechanical environment in which pressure-strain proxies can be compared with finite-element stress-strain work. That is useful for identifying where the proxy is mechanically aligned with the model and where it becomes ambiguous. It does not show that the same proxy predicts oxygen consumption, invasive contractility, symptoms, or outcomes in patients. Those require separate clinical validation with measured or well-estimated RV pressure.

Finally, the pressure-strain proxy itself uses longitudinal strain averaged over regions. Clinical speckle tracking can report several anatomical strain directions, but the myocardial-work workflows tested here are based on longitudinal strain {cite}`voigt2015definitions,sade2023current,abawi2022noninvasive`, so the fibre-aligned calculations in the results should be read as mechanics diagnostics rather than routine clinical measurements. Fibre-aligned strain improved the septal pressure comparison, especially for mean and through-wall weighted pressures, but that does not make fibre strain a clinical replacement: it shows that the longitudinal proxy loses information about the dominant tissue deformation direction. A true local strain-resolution strategy would require full three-dimensional strain information, including shear, not only a different scalar component.

## Synthesis And Outlook

The sweep is informative in a specifically constrained way. It answers what a fixed biventricular model exposed to a range of RV pressure loadings, without disease remodelling, can say about pressure-strain proxy behaviour. The free-wall adjacent-pressure result is stable and intuitive. The septal result is more conditional: LV, RV, mean, nearest-side, and through-wall weighted pressures all gave stronger ranking correlations than transmural pressure, and LV pressure best preserved the longitudinal magnitude ratios. This is not a failure of the finite-element model; it is a warning about correlation-based validation and about reducing a two-sided wall to one pressure.

The ratio results were less fragile in the main respect that matters for the septum: transmural pressure was poor at preserving septum/free-wall work-density magnitudes. The exact non-transmural winner depended on strain direction — LV pressure for tangent-longitudinal strain, mean and through-wall weighted pressure for the fibre-aligned diagnostic — but the qualitative magnitude ranking, with transmural worst and LV best among the single-sided choices, survived both strain conventions. This is why magnitude preservation should be given more weight than sweep ranking: correlations depend on the path through parameter space, while a regional ratio inside one simulation asks a more direct question about the proxy's mechanical meaning.

Three lines of follow-up work would extend the result. Patient-specific simulations with remodelled geometry would test the compensation hypothesis from {ref}`sec-discussion-patient-geometry`: if PAH remodelling really reduces tissue work density while pressure-strain proxies continue to track pressure and measured shortening, cross-geometry validation is the demanding test. A higher-resolution septal mesh would let through-wall pressure assignments be tested as resolved transmural quantities rather than as coarse-layer diagnostics. And invasive RV pressure-volume validation, beyond the model-side bridge to Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right` already drawn in the results, remains the missing link between the RV free-wall pressure-strain proxy and the clinical contractility question it is meant to answer.
