# Discussion and Conclusion

## Interpretation of the Main Finding

The central result of this thesis is that the transmural pressure $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ provides a better proxy for septal internal work than left ventricular pressure alone, and that the improvement is clinically meaningful in pulmonary arterial hypertension. This finding is not surprising from a continuum mechanics perspective — the septum is loaded from both sides, so the net transmural load is the natural scalar characterization of the mechanical environment — but it has practical implications for how myocardial work imaging is interpreted in PAH patients.

The magnitude of the error introduced by using $P_\text{LV}$ alone depends on the ratio $P_\text{RV} / P_\text{LV}$. In healthy physiology, this ratio is approximately 0.2, and the correction from the transmural proxy is correspondingly small. In the PAH case studied here, the ratio rises to approximately 0.5, and the LV proxy overestimates septal work by a margin that could affect clinical interpretation — particularly if one is comparing septal work against free wall work to assess ventricular synchrony or to identify candidates for device therapy. The threshold at which the correction becomes clinically important is a question for clinical validation studies, but the simulation provides a clear quantitative prediction of the direction and magnitude of the effect.

It is worth noting what this result does not imply. It does not mean that the pressure-strain proxy is an accurate measure of absolute septal work. The full tensor internal work, $\int \mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E} \, dV$, includes contributions from all stress and strain components — fiber, sheet, normal, and shear — while the pressure-strain proxy uses only the fiber-direction strain component scaled by a single scalar pressure. The proxy systematically underestimates absolute work, as previously documented by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` for the LV. What the transmural correction achieves is not absolute accuracy but improved relative accuracy: the transmural proxy tracks the true septal work more faithfully over time and across conditions than the LV proxy does. For clinical applications where the interest lies in comparing regional work patterns rather than measuring absolute energy in joules, this relative improvement is the relevant metric.

## Relationship to Prior Work

This study was directly motivated by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`, who developed the LV-only finite element framework for assessing regional myocardial work and explicitly identified biventricular extension as future work. Our results are consistent with their findings in the domains where the studies overlap: the full tensor work agrees with the PV-loop stroke work for the whole ventricle, the fiber-direction work accounts for roughly 60% of the total, and the pressure-strain proxy captures relative trends in regional work despite underestimating absolute magnitudes. The biventricular extension reveals the additional complication that arises from the shared septal wall — a complication that was absent from the LV-only model by construction.

The clinical pressure-strain loop method of Russell et al. {cite}`russell2012novel` assigns LV pressure to all left heart segments, including the septum, and this convention is followed in current implementations of myocardial work imaging. Our results suggest that this convention introduces a systematic overestimation of septal work in PAH, and that a transmural correction — subtracting an estimate of RV pressure — would improve the accuracy of septal work estimation in this population. Whether such a correction is feasible in clinical practice depends on the availability of RV pressure estimates, which can be obtained non-invasively from the tricuspid regurgitation jet velocity using Doppler echocardiography.

## Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged, and they fall into three categories: limitations of the model itself, limitations of the parameter calibration, and limitations of the comparison framework.

The constitutive model uses the Holzapfel-Ogden strain energy function with transversely isotropic parameters that are identical across all three geometries. In reality, the myocardial material properties vary between individuals and between healthy and diseased tissue; in PAH, the RV free wall undergoes fibrosis and hypertrophy that alter its passive stiffness and contractile properties. Our model captures the altered hemodynamics of PAH through the circulation parameters and the active tension magnitudes but does not modify the passive constitutive parameters of the tissue. This is a simplification that could be addressed in future work by incorporating patient-specific material parameter identification.

The activation model assumes simultaneous, spatially uniform activation within each anatomical region (LV free wall, RV free wall, septum), with the only spatial variation being the peak tension amplitude. In reality, electrical activation propagates as a wave from the apex upward with conduction delays of tens of milliseconds. For the integrated cycle work computed here, the effect of activation timing heterogeneity is secondary to the effect of peak tension and hemodynamic loading, but it could become significant in studies of dyssynchrony or in comparisons involving patients with conduction abnormalities.

The 0D circulation model is a lumped-parameter system with effective constants that have no direct anatomical counterpart. The calibrated parameters reproduce the correct macroscopic hemodynamics but do not represent the actual vascular physiology of any specific patient. The PAH case is modeled by increasing the pulmonary vascular resistance and adjusting the RV properties, but the model does not capture the spatial heterogeneity of vascular remodeling or the progressive nature of the disease. The conclusions apply to the hemodynamic regime simulated, not necessarily to all PAH patients.

The meshes used in this study are generated from either a statistical atlas or from clinical MRI with automated segmentation. The mesh resolution (approximately 5 mm characteristic length) is sufficient to resolve the main features of the stress field but does not capture fine-scale anatomical structures such as papillary muscles or trabeculations. The septal region is defined by cell-level tags that were manually inspected and corrected, but the precise boundary between the septum and the free walls is inherently somewhat arbitrary.

Finally, this study compares three simulation cases. While these span the range from healthy to severe PAH and include both synthetic and patient-specific geometries, a larger cohort study would be needed to quantify the statistical relationship between RV pressure elevation and septal proxy accuracy with confidence. The present results should be understood as a proof of concept — a computational demonstration that the transmural pressure correction is meaningful in a realistic simulation of PAH — rather than as a population-level clinical finding.

## Conclusion

The interventricular septum, as a wall shared between two pressure-loaded cavities, presents a genuine ambiguity for pressure-strain-based myocardial work estimation. In health, where the right ventricular pressure is small relative to the left, this ambiguity is numerically insignificant and the standard convention of assigning LV pressure to septal segments is adequate. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, where RV pressure is substantially elevated, the ambiguity becomes quantitatively important: LV pressure alone overestimates septal work, and the transmural pressure difference $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ provides a closer approximation to the ground-truth finite element internal work.

The computational framework developed for this study — a biventricular finite element model coupled to a closed-loop circulation model, with the full stress-strain tensor available at every quadrature point and time step — provides the resolution needed to evaluate these pressure proxies against a rigorous mechanical ground truth. The implementation challenges encountered and resolved during the development of this framework are documented in the preceding chapters because they are relevant to anyone attempting similar coupled cardiac mechanics simulations.

The practical implication of this work is that myocardial work imaging in PAH patients may benefit from incorporating a transmural pressure correction for septal segments. Non-invasive estimates of RV systolic pressure are routinely available from Doppler echocardiography and could be used to construct the transmural pressure from standard clinical measurements. Whether this correction improves the clinical utility of myocardial work imaging in PAH — in terms of diagnostic accuracy, prognostic value, or treatment guidance — is a question for clinical validation that this computational study motivates but cannot answer.
