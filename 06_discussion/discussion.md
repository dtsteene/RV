# Discussion and Conclusion

## Interpretation of the Main Finding

The central result of this thesis is that the transmural pressure $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ provides a better proxy for septal internal work than left ventricular pressure alone, and that the improvement is clinically meaningful in pulmonary arterial hypertension. This finding is not surprising from a continuum mechanics perspective — the septum is loaded from both sides, so the net transmural load is the natural scalar characterization of the mechanical environment — but it has practical implications for how myocardial work imaging is interpreted in PAH patients.

The magnitude of the error introduced by using $P_\text{LV}$ alone depends on the ratio $P_\text{RV} / P_\text{LV}$. In healthy physiology, this ratio is approximately 0.2, and the correction from the transmural proxy is correspondingly small. In the PAH case studied here, the ratio rises to approximately 0.5, and the LV proxy overestimates septal work by a margin that could affect clinical interpretation — particularly if one is comparing septal work against free wall work to assess ventricular synchrony or to identify candidates for device therapy. The threshold at which the correction becomes clinically important is a question for clinical validation studies, but the simulation provides a clear quantitative prediction of the direction and magnitude of the effect.

What this result does not imply is also worth stating. It does not mean that the pressure-strain proxy is an accurate measure of absolute septal work. The full tensor internal work, $\int \mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E} \, dV$, includes contributions from all stress and strain components — fiber, sheet, normal, and shear — while the pressure-strain proxy uses only the fiber-direction strain component scaled by a single scalar pressure. The proxy systematically underestimates absolute work, as previously documented by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment` for the LV. What the transmural correction achieves is not absolute accuracy but improved relative accuracy: the transmural proxy tracks the true septal work more faithfully over time and across conditions than the LV proxy does. For clinical applications where the interest lies in comparing regional work patterns rather than measuring absolute energy in joules, this relative improvement is the relevant metric.

## Relationship to Prior Work

This study was directly motivated by Finsberg et al. {cite}`finsberg2019assessment`, who developed the LV-only finite element framework for assessing regional myocardial work and explicitly identified biventricular extension as future work. Our results are consistent with their findings in the domains where the studies overlap: the full tensor work agrees with the PV-loop stroke work for the whole ventricle, the fiber-direction work accounts for roughly 60% of the total, and the pressure-strain proxy captures relative trends in regional work despite underestimating absolute magnitudes. The biventricular extension reveals the additional complication that arises from the shared septal wall — a complication that was absent from the LV-only model by construction.

The clinical pressure-strain loop method of Russell et al. {cite}`russell2012novel` assigns LV pressure to all left heart segments, including the septum, and this convention is followed in current implementations of myocardial work imaging. Our results suggest that this convention introduces a systematic overestimation of septal work in PAH, and that a transmural correction — subtracting an estimate of RV pressure — would improve the accuracy of septal work estimation in this population. Whether such a correction is feasible in clinical practice depends on the availability of RV pressure estimates, which can be obtained non-invasively from the tricuspid regurgitation jet velocity using Doppler echocardiography.

## Anatomy of the Proxy Approximation

The pressure-strain proxy replaces a tensor field quantity — the double contraction $\mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E}$ integrated over the myocardial volume — with the product of a single scalar pressure and a single scalar strain increment. It is worth examining this substitution step by step, because each step introduces a distinct and quantifiable approximation, and the chain of approximations reveals both why the proxy works at all and how crude it is as a measure of absolute work.

### Step 1: Full tensor to fiber-only work

The internal work $W_\text{int} = \int \mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E} \, dV$ decomposes in the fiber coordinate system as

$$
\mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E} = S_{ff} \, dE_{ff} + S_{ss} \, dE_{ss} + S_{nn} \, dE_{nn} + \text{(shear terms)}.
$$

Retaining only the fiber-direction component $S_{ff} \, dE_{ff}$ discards the sheet, sheet-normal, and shear contributions. In the simulations presented here, the fiber work accounts for approximately 85–90% of the total in the LV free wall. The remaining 10–15% is distributed across the sheet and normal directions, with shear contributing a small fraction. This first approximation is well-conditioned: the fiber direction carries the active contraction, and the transverse directions respond passively to the fiber shortening. The ratio of fiber work to total work is stable across the cardiac cycle and across disease states — the PAH-to-healthy ratio of fiber work (0.787) tracks the same ratio of total work (0.780) closely — so the directional decomposition does not distort relative comparisons.

### Step 2: Fiber stress to cavity pressure

The critical step is replacing the spatially varying fiber stress $S_{ff}(\mathbf{X})$ with the cavity pressure $P$. This substitution is not mechanically consistent — $P$ is not a component of the stress tensor — but it has a physical basis in the thin-wall approximation for the transmural stress. The Cauchy stress normal to the wall satisfies the traction boundary conditions at the endocardial and epicardial surfaces:

$$
\sigma_{nn}\big|_\text{endo} = -P_\text{cavity}, \qquad \sigma_{nn}\big|_\text{epi} \approx 0.
$$

For a wall with roughly linear transmural stress variation, the volume-averaged transmural Cauchy stress is

$$
\bar{\sigma}_{nn} \approx \frac{-P + 0}{2} = -\frac{P}{2}.
$$

The simulations confirm this scaling: for the LV free wall, the ratio of the volume-averaged transmural Cauchy stress magnitude to the peak cavity pressure is 0.464, close to the predicted 0.5. The deviation reflects wall curvature (the Laplace effect), nonlinear stress distribution through the wall, and the small Robin traction at the epicardium. For the RV free wall, the ratio is 0.775 — higher because the thinner RV wall has a more uniform transmural stress profile, pushing the average closer to the endocardial boundary value.

This means the pressure-strain proxy is, at its core, tracking the *transmural* work component $S_{nn} \, dE_{nn}$ rather than the fiber component $S_{ff} \, dE_{ff}$, despite being paired with fiber strain. The proxy nevertheless captures the temporal pattern of the total work because all stress components — fiber, sheet, normal — are driven by the same hemodynamic loading cycle and rise and fall together. The magnitude discrepancy between the proxy and the true work reflects this directional mismatch: the proxy uses a transmural-like pressure with a circumferential strain, mixing mechanical directions in a way that is physically inconsistent but empirically effective for tracking relative changes.

### Step 3: Fiber strain to longitudinal strain

The final step toward clinical measurement replaces the fiber-direction strain $E_{ff}$ — which requires knowledge of the fiber architecture — with the longitudinal (apex-to-base) strain $E_{ll}$, which is what speckle-tracking echocardiography measures as global longitudinal strain (GLS). In the LDRB fiber system, the fiber direction $\mathbf{f}_0$ is circumferential (helical), while the longitudinal direction $\mathbf{l}_0$ runs from apex to base. These directions are nearly orthogonal, so $E_{ff}$ and $E_{ll}$ represent different projections of the same strain tensor.

The substitution works because both strain components are driven by the same active contraction and respond to the same pressure loading. During systole, the heart shortens in both circumferential and longitudinal directions simultaneously, and the temporal correlation between $dE_{ff}$ and $dE_{ll}$ is high. Since the proxy is an integral $\sum P \, d\varepsilon$ over the cardiac cycle, what matters for relative comparisons is the temporal pattern of strain change relative to pressure — when the strain is changing most rapidly and in which direction relative to the pressure rise — rather than the absolute direction of the strain.

### The full cascade

The complete chain of approximations, with the loss at each step quantified from the simulations, is:

$$
\underbrace{\int \mathbf{S} : d\mathbf{E} \, dV}_{\text{full tensor work}}
\;\xrightarrow{\;\times 0.85\text{--}0.90\;}
\underbrace{\int S_{ff} \, dE_{ff} \, dV}_{\text{fiber-only work}}
\;\xrightarrow{\;P \;\text{for}\; S\;}
\underbrace{P \cdot V \cdot d\bar{E}_{ff}}_{\text{PS proxy (fiber)}}
\;\xrightarrow{\;E_{ll} \;\text{for}\; E_{ff}\;}
\underbrace{P \cdot V \cdot d\bar{E}_{ll}}_{\text{PS proxy (GLS)}}.
$$

Each arrow represents a measurable loss of information. The first discards 10–15% of the work by dropping non-fiber components. The second replaces a spatially heterogeneous tensor field with a single scalar, introducing a magnitude error that depends on wall geometry and thickness. The third substitutes a different strain direction, changing the magnitude of the strain increments but preserving their temporal pattern. Despite these compounding approximations, the key finding from the simulations is that the sensitivity to disease — the ratio of PAH work to healthy work — is preserved through all levels of the cascade. The proxy detects the same direction and approximate magnitude of work change as the ground truth. This is ultimately why the clinical pressure-strain index is useful: it is not an accurate energy measurement, but it is a reliable comparative indicator. The transmural pressure correction for the septum operates within this framework — it does not make the proxy accurate in absolute terms, but it makes the relative comparison between healthy and PAH more faithful by accounting for the mechanical load that the standard LV-only convention ignores.

## The Septum Boundary Problem

The analysis of the previous chapter took the septum as a discrete anatomical region with a definite boundary, and every reported ratio and correlation was computed over that region. During the spectrum analysis it became clear that this treatment hides a methodological fragility that is worth articulating directly, because it affects how the results should be read and because it points toward the natural next step of this line of work.

The septum is not, mechanically, a discrete region. It is a transition zone where LV-dominated loading gives way to RV-dominated loading, and any hard boundary drawn across that transition is arbitrary to some degree. The question is how much the choice of boundary affects the numbers one reports. In our pipeline the septum tag was originally produced by thresholding the Laplace scalar fields from the LDRB algorithm — a standard approach in cardiac mechanics that defines the septum as the region where the transmural epi-scalar is small and the interventricular scalar lies in an intermediate range. When this tag was replaced with a purely geometric rule — a cell belongs to the septum if it is closer to both endocardial surfaces than to the epicardium — the septal volume shrank by about forty percent, and the sign of the correlation between the $P_\text{RV}$ proxy and the true septal work flipped from strongly negative to strongly positive. Boundary-independent quantities, by contrast, moved by less than a percent under the same change.

What this sensitivity means is that the septum is carrying two boundaries simultaneously, and the literature is more concerned with one than the other. The first boundary separates the septum from the free walls: is this cell part of the shared wall, or is it facing only one cavity? This boundary is anatomically unambiguous in the sense that nobody disputes what the septum *is*; the left and right halves of a biventricular heart are joined by tissue sandwiched between two cavities, and geometric rules that look at which surfaces a cell is closest to produce sensible answers. The second boundary, which is the source of the sensitivity, lies *inside* the septum: it separates the region where LV-dominated mechanics are a reasonable approximation from the region where RV-dominated mechanics take over. This internal boundary has no crisp definition because the underlying loading is continuous. The proxy comparison, as it is currently framed, asks a discrete question about a quantity whose answer depends on where one draws the continuous boundary.

The computational cardiology literature has developed several approaches to the first boundary and, to varying degrees, acknowledged the second. The AHA 17-segment model {cite}`cerqueira2002standardized` defines the septum by angular sectors in the circumferential direction — segments 2, 3, 8, 9, and 14 — but those segments span the full wall thickness and therefore cannot resolve the transventricular transition at all. The Universal Ventricular Coordinates framework of Bayer et al. {cite}`bayer2018universal` pins the septum boundaries to the anatomical junctions between the LV and RV free walls by Dirichlet boundary conditions on a rotational coordinate; this is more principled than a pure scalar threshold but still produces a discrete boundary. The state of the art is the Cobiveco system of Schüler et al. {cite}`schuler2021cobiveco`, which defines a full symmetric biventricular coordinate system with transventricular, transmural, rotational, and apicobasal coordinates, constructed from the trajectory distances of Laplace solutions rather than from the raw Laplace values themselves. Cobiveco explicitly evaluated and rejected the simple Eikonal quotient that we use for the septal transventricular coordinate in preliminary analysis — their Fig. 2 shows it producing cusps and non-uniform spacing in regions of non-uniform wall thickness — and recommends trajectory distances as the best compromise between linearity and computational cost. Our own comparison between the Eikonal quotient and the Laplace solution on the UKB septal region found that the Laplace solution suffers from a bowing artifact at the basal junction that scrambles the through-wall ordering, while the Eikonal coordinate preserves monotonicity at the cost of non-uniform bin density. Neither was ideal; the Eikonal coordinate was chosen because its failure mode (density variation) is correctable by volume-weighted binning while the Laplace failure mode (wrong order) is not.

The methodological implication is that the proxy comparison becomes more honest if it is presented as a function of the internal septum boundary rather than as a single number. Conceptually, one would compute the proxy ratio $R^{(P)}$ not at a fixed septum definition but across a continuous sweep of definitions, bounded below by the tight geometric rule and above by the generous scalar-field rule. The resulting sensitivity curve $R^{(P)}(\text{septum width})$ can be read like a Rosetta stone: any reader familiar with their preferred septum definition can locate its width on the axis and read off the proxy accuracy it implies. The curve also exposes where the three proxies agree and where they disagree, so that the robust conclusions (the transmural pressure is better than the LV pressure alone in PAH) can be separated from the boundary-dependent ones (the precise magnitude of the overestimation in any single region).

An even cleaner framing is to compute the proxy comparison continuously along a transventricular coordinate. For each cell in the myocardium, one forms a local work density $w(\mathbf{X})$ and local proxy densities $w^{(P)}(\mathbf{X})$, bins the cells by their transventricular position $\tau \in [0, 1]$ running from LV endocardium to RV endocardium across the septum, and plots the ratio $R^{(P)}(\tau) = w^{(P)}(\tau) / w(\tau)$ as a function of $\tau$. The LV free wall appears at $\tau \approx 0$, the RV free wall at $\tau \approx 1$, and the septum fills the middle. The crossover location — where $R^{(P_\text{LV})}$ and $R^{(P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV})}$ swap their ranking — is the mechanical boundary between LV-dominated and transmural-dominated proxy validity, and its position shifts leftward with increasing PAH severity as the RV pressure grows. This is the boundary-free form of the central result, and it turns the question of "what is the septal work?" into the sharper question of "at each point in the myocardium, which pressure proxy best tracks the local work?"

The per-cell work density required for this analysis cannot be obtained by projecting $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ to a piecewise-constant space and multiplying the projections: the DG0 averaging destroys the correlation between the two fields at individual quadrature points, and the resulting per-cell integrals fail to sum to the regional integral. The correct approach, which we used in the preliminary exploration, is to assemble the integrand against a DG0 test function in a single global assembly so that each entry of the resulting vector is the integral of $\mathbf{S} : \dot{\mathbf{E}}$ over one cell, computed at the same quadrature points the solver uses. This gives a per-cell work density that sums exactly to the regional work, satisfying the energy balance check cell-by-cell, and provides the spatial resolution needed for the transventricular profile. Extending the spectrum analysis to this continuous framing is the most natural next step from the work reported in this thesis, and the foundations are in place for doing so.

## Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged, and they fall into three categories: limitations of the model itself, limitations of the parameter calibration, and limitations of the comparison framework.

The constitutive model uses the Holzapfel-Ogden strain energy function with transversely isotropic parameters that are identical across all three geometries. In reality, the myocardial material properties vary between individuals and between healthy and diseased tissue; in PAH, the RV free wall undergoes fibrosis and hypertrophy that alter its passive stiffness and contractile properties. Our model captures the altered hemodynamics of PAH through the circulation parameters and the active tension magnitudes but does not modify the passive constitutive parameters of the tissue. This is a simplification that could be addressed in future work by incorporating patient-specific material parameter identification.

The activation model assumes simultaneous, spatially uniform activation within each anatomical region (LV free wall, RV free wall, septum), with the only spatial variation being the peak tension amplitude. In reality, electrical activation propagates as a wave from the apex upward with conduction delays of tens of milliseconds. For the integrated cycle work computed here, the effect of activation timing heterogeneity is secondary to the effect of peak tension and hemodynamic loading, but it could become significant in studies of dyssynchrony or in comparisons involving patients with conduction abnormalities.

The 0D circulation model is a lumped-parameter system with effective constants that have no direct anatomical counterpart. The calibrated parameters reproduce the correct macroscopic hemodynamics but do not represent the actual vascular physiology of any specific patient. The PAH case is modeled by increasing the pulmonary vascular resistance and adjusting the RV properties, but the model does not capture the spatial heterogeneity of vascular remodeling or the progressive nature of the disease. The conclusions apply to the hemodynamic regime simulated, not necessarily to all PAH patients.

A related limitation concerns the pressure consistency between the 0D and 3D models. As documented in the implementation chapter, the volume-controlled coupling scheme enforces agreement in cavity volumes but not in cavity pressures: the 0D elastance pressure and the 3D Lagrange multiplier pressure differ by 30–60% throughout the cycle. This means that the circulation parameters tuned to reproduce clinical pressure targets in the standalone 0D model do not produce the same pressures when coupled with the 3D finite element model. The clinical targets (e.g., LV peak systolic pressure of 120 mmHg, RV peak systolic of 25 mmHg) are matched by the 0D model in isolation, but the actual cavity pressures generated by the 3D myocardium to achieve the corresponding volumes are systematically different. The disease state being simulated is therefore defined by the volume dynamics and the active tension levels rather than by the cavity pressures directly. For the pressure-strain proxies evaluated in this thesis, the Lagrange multiplier pressures from the 3D solver were used, ensuring that the proxy comparison reflects the actual mechanical loading rather than the 0D approximation. However, this pressure discrepancy should be kept in mind when interpreting the absolute magnitudes of the pressure-strain work estimates or when comparing them to clinical measurements where the pressure is measured directly (e.g., from catheterization).

The meshes used in this study are generated from either a statistical atlas or from clinical MRI with automated segmentation. The mesh resolution (approximately 5 mm characteristic length) is sufficient to resolve the main features of the stress field but does not capture fine-scale anatomical structures such as papillary muscles or trabeculations. The septal region is defined by cell-level tags that were manually inspected and corrected, but the precise boundary between the septum and the free walls is inherently somewhat arbitrary.

Finally, this study compares three simulation cases. While these span the range from healthy to severe PAH and include both synthetic and patient-specific geometries, a larger cohort study would be needed to quantify the statistical relationship between RV pressure elevation and septal proxy accuracy with confidence. The present results should be understood as a proof of concept — a computational demonstration that the transmural pressure correction is meaningful in a realistic simulation of PAH — rather than as a population-level clinical finding.

## Conclusion

The interventricular septum, as a wall shared between two pressure-loaded cavities, presents a genuine ambiguity for pressure-strain-based myocardial work estimation. In health, where the right ventricular pressure is small relative to the left, this ambiguity is numerically insignificant and the standard convention of assigning LV pressure to septal segments is adequate. In pulmonary arterial hypertension, where RV pressure is substantially elevated, the ambiguity becomes quantitatively important: LV pressure alone overestimates septal work, and the transmural pressure difference $P_\text{LV} - P_\text{RV}$ provides a closer approximation to the ground-truth finite element internal work.

The computational framework developed for this study — a biventricular finite element model coupled to a closed-loop circulation model, with the full stress-strain tensor available at every quadrature point and time step — provides the resolution needed to evaluate these pressure proxies against a rigorous mechanical ground truth. The implementation challenges encountered and resolved during the development of this framework are documented in the preceding chapters because they are relevant to anyone attempting similar coupled cardiac mechanics simulations.

The practical implication of this work is that myocardial work imaging in PAH patients may benefit from incorporating a transmural pressure correction for septal segments. Non-invasive estimates of RV systolic pressure are routinely available from Doppler echocardiography and could be used to construct the transmural pressure from standard clinical measurements. Whether this correction improves the clinical utility of myocardial work imaging in PAH — in terms of diagnostic accuracy, prognostic value, or treatment guidance — is a question for clinical validation that this computational study motivates but cannot answer.
