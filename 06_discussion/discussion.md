# Discussion

The clinical pressure-strain proxy works for the LV and RV free walls of this model and fails for the septum. The free walls each face one pressurized cavity, so adjacent cavity pressure is a reasonable scalar load; the septum sits between two pressurized cavities, so no single cavity pressure represents its loading. The free-wall finding also extends a clinical method better established for the LV to the less-validated RV. In the primary capped-reference sweep, transmural pressure was not a good septal work-density proxy: it correlated negatively with septal stress-strain work density along the loading sweep and gave the largest septum/free-wall magnitude error. LV pressure gave the best fixed-candidate longitudinal magnitude result, while the model-side fibre diagnostic favoured mean and through-wall weighted pressure.

There is no single right pressure for the septum, and that is the more honest answer. Transmural pressure, $p_\text{LV}-p_\text{RV}$, is mechanically meaningful — it is the pressure *difference* across the wall, which is what drives septal flattening and the D-shaped left ventricle seen in RV pressure overload {cite}`ryan1985echocardiographic,vonk2013right`. But the pressure difference is not the same thing as the load on the septal tissue. Work density is local stress times local strain, and the septum has both cavities pushing on it. A small pressure difference can mean the septum is unloaded; it can equally well mean both sides are pressurised about equally, which loads the muscle hard. Transmural pressure fails as a tissue-work proxy precisely because it confuses these two cases.

The fixed-geometry setup is the point of the experiment, not a limitation of it. Echo tracking error, cuff-pressure scaling, missing RV pressure estimates, patient-to-patient stiffness and wall-thickness variation — everything that confounds a clinical comparison is gone. So if adjacent pressure works in the free walls here, that is evidence for the mechanical idea behind the proxy; if the septal pressure choice stays ambiguous here, the ambiguity isn't a measurement problem, it is already in the mechanics of replacing a two-sided stress state with one scalar. Real patients add complexity on top of this; they don't remove it.

## What The Free-Wall Result Means

For the LV and RV free walls, adjacent cavity pressure preserved the LV/RV work-density ratio better than the alternatives. A free wall has one blood pressure acting on its endocardial surface, one main cavity whose volume change it contributes to, and no second cavity pressing on the opposite side. Cavity pressure is still not a local stress tensor, but it is at least a physically sensible scalar load.

The pressure-strain proxy gives smaller absolute numbers than the finite-element stress-strain work density does. That is not a problem with the model — wall stress isn't cavity pressure even for a simple inflated cylinder, where Laplace's law $pr/h$ already scales wall stress by geometry, and in the heart active fibre stress and anisotropic stiffness make the difference larger still. The question is not whether pressure equals fibre stress, but whether adjacent pressure is the best available scalar load for comparing otherwise similar free-wall regions. On that more modest test it performed well.

None of this says pressure-strain loops are mechanically empty. They have a domain where their assumptions are reasonable — the free walls — and in that domain adjacent pressure and longitudinal strain gave a useful model-side estimate of regional work-density balance, even though absolute magnitudes differed from the finite-element reference. That matches how pressure-strain myocardial work is used clinically: as a comparative regional index, not a direct measurement of energy in joules {cite}`russell2012novel,abawi2022noninvasive`.

Lakatos et al. {cite}`lakatos2024right` did the patient version of this comparison. They used invasive pressure-volume analysis as their reference and found that RV pressure-strain work tracked RV contractility better than RV GLS or ejection fraction. We cannot reproduce that exact endpoint here, because the sweep is not an invasive contractility experiment, but we can do the FE analogue: compare the RV pressure-longitudinal-strain index against FE RV free-wall stress-strain work density. On that test the pressure-strain index tracked the work-density reference far better than strain alone. The endpoints are not the same, but the clinical and model-side results point in the same direction, which gives the RV part of the thesis its own motivation independent of the septum.

## How To Understand The Septum

The septum is the harder pressure-assignment problem. It is not an LV segment that happens to sit near the RV, and it is not an RV free wall — it is shared tissue. The LV pressure acts on one side, the RV pressure acts on the other, and the deformation of the septum is constrained by both free walls and by the basal attachments. A one-pressure proxy collapses this two-sided object into a scalar.

Laplace's law gives intuition but doesn't answer the question. For a thin free wall, wall stress scales as $pr/h$. For the septum, applying the same idea with the pressure *difference* across the wall is natural if the question is force balance or curvature — that difference is what determines whether the septum bows into the RV or into the LV. But the FE work density is not a curvature measure. It is the stress-strain work inside the tissue, and a septum with a small pressure difference can still be carrying substantial tissue work because both cavities are pressurised and the surrounding myocardium is pulling on it.

The ratio analysis was designed to separate two things that transmural pressure conflates: the *difference* between cavity pressures, which drives septal curvature, and the *absolute* pressure loading each side of the wall. Transmural pressure helps explain septal *shape*, but it did not preserve septal work-density magnitude relative to the free walls. With the tangent-longitudinal definition, LV pressure was the best single-pressure choice; with model-side fibre strain, mean pressure and a through-wall weighted pressure pulled closer. Both of those mixtures keep information from both cavities — one as an average, the other as a smooth interpolation from $p_\text{LV}$ to $p_\text{RV}$ across the wall thickness — instead of discarding it through subtraction. These are not fitted formulas, just the simplest ways of using both cavity pressures together. The takeaway is not that one of these is the right septal pressure, but that proxies which keep both pressures behave better than the pressure difference alone.

The through-wall analysis should still be read cautiously. The mesh used here was not designed as a high-resolution septal-layer model. In places the septum is only a few elements thick, so dividing it into LV-side, middle, and RV-side layers is a diagnostic tool rather than a definitive transmural measurement. A higher-resolution septal mesh would be needed to make strong claims about the detailed stress profile through the septal wall. The current result is stronger at the coarser level: the septum behaves as shared tissue, and one-sided pressure choices should be interpreted as metric-dependent simplifications rather than complete local stress surrogates.

The work decomposition in the local fibre-sheet-normal basis reinforces this picture ({numref}`fig-work-components`). Across the primary sweep the free walls are strongly fibre-dominated — about 89% LV, 94% RV — while the septum's fibre share is smaller at about 80%, with sheet-normal and cross-axis contributions taking up the rest. Whether the multi-directional decomposition drives the proxy gap is a separate question — the chapter's principal-shortening analysis showed longitudinal alignment is not noticeably worse in the septum than in the free walls — but as a descriptive observation it is consistent with the septum behaving as a wall that does not reduce cleanly to a single-direction problem.

## Why The Uncapped Transmural Correlation Was An Artefact

The results chapter rests on a single sweep — sixteen cases with the RV end-diastolic pressure capped at 5 mmHg. An earlier, uncapped version of the same calibration produced a strongly *positive* correlation between transmural pressure and septal stress-strain work density, large enough that transmural pressure looked like a viable septal proxy. The cap inverted it. The natural question is whether the cap removed a real signal or a confound, and the answer turns on what inverse-unloading does to the reference state when RV end-diastolic pressure is allowed to rise.

The free-wall result and the septum/free-wall magnitude pattern are not affected by this debate. Across six sweep designs varying the optimizer, the chamber EDPVR form, the calibration targets, the reference-state strategy, and whether the simple-calibration architecture is capped or uncapped (see {ref}`chap-appendix-reference-state`), adjacent cavity pressure preserves the LV/RV ratio with RV correlation above $0.84$ in every sweep and LV correlation above $0.90$ in five of the six — the exception being the single-knob capped sweep, whose imposed cap holds LV ESP nearly constant and so loses cross-case discriminating power on the LV side. LV pressure delivers the smallest single-pressure log-ratio error in every sweep; transmural pressure ranks among the two worst single-pressure choices in every sweep. What varies systematically between designs is the septum's *cross-case correlation*, and that variation tracks the inferred unloaded reference rather than the underlying mechanics.

Inverse unloading on a fixed UKB anatomy infers an implausibly small unloaded RV cavity when RV end-diastolic pressure is high, so the septum begins the cycle already bulged toward the RV side of the unloaded geometry. Inflating that pre-bulged reference back to the imaged end-diastolic shape requires a large transmural stretch, which pushes the Holzapfel-Ogden law into its exponential stiffening regime. The septum then deforms much less during the beat, the work it generates falls, and the deformation that does occur is dominated by the transmural direction — visible in the work-component decomposition as a downward drift of the septum's fibre share and an upward drift of the sheet-normal share in the severe uncapped cases. In those same cases, transmural pressure is also low because RV pressure has approached LV pressure. Low transmural pressure and low septal work density therefore co-vary across the sweep, producing the spurious positive correlation.

The cleanest test of this story is the shared-unloaded sweep, which uses one well-behaved reference for every case and so eliminates per-case reference-state variation. In that sweep the transmural correlation collapses to $r=+0.006$ — indistinguishable from zero — while LV pressure and the two-sided choices retain positive correlations of about 0.68 to 0.78. The capped sweep's negative transmural correlation is therefore the lower bound of an artefactual range, not the suppression of a real one. Transmural pressure does not track septal stress-strain work density once inverse-unloading is prevented from driving case-to-case reference collapse.

## A Cross-Geometry Direction Check

Exploratory simulations on a patient-specific PAH mesh — smaller cavities (RV EDV 74.2 mL versus the UKB-baseline 94.4 mL) and larger total wall volume (165.7 mL versus 129.1 mL) — test whether the fixed-anatomy result holds in a remodelled geometry. In the cap=5 mini-sweep, the PAH geometry carried lower septal stress-strain work density than the healthy geometry at all three selected pressure levels, despite similar or higher achieved RV systolic pressure. The RV free wall was less monotone: PAH was lower at low and mid pressure, but higher in the severe case where the achieved RV pressure was also much higher. The pressure-strain proxies did not attenuate in the same way; the per-case comparison is in {ref}`chap-appendix-patient-geometry`. This is not a second proof of the production result. It is a warning that geometry can change finite-element tissue-work density in ways that a pressure-strain proxy does not automatically follow. It also suggests a compensation hypothesis worth testing in larger patient-specific simulations: PAH remodelling may reduce regional tissue work density by adding wall volume, changing curvature, and changing the reference state, while pressure-strain indices continue to track pressure and longitudinal shortening. Cross-geometry proxy validation is a more demanding test than the fixed-geometry sweep can give on its own.

## What The Energy Balance Proves

The whole-heart energy balance proves a narrow but important point: the stress-strain work postprocessing is consistent with the finite-element boundary-value problem that generated the pressures and deformations. Internal stress power and boundary pressure-volume power are two ways of writing the same mechanical balance, up to the small support terms. That makes stress-strain work a coherent model-side reference for regional decomposition. It does not prove that the model has captured every biological source of energetic cost, every patient-specific material property, or the uniquely correct in-vivo stress field.

This is the cleanest way to read the work of {cite:t}`finsberg2017phd` extended here: finite-element stress-strain work integrates to the PV-loop boundary work and so functions as a whole-ventricle energy budget, while pressure-strain loops give a much smaller absolute magnitude but still rank regions meaningfully. Pressure-strain work, PV-loop work, pressure-volume area {cite}`suga1979total`, Laplace wall-stress estimates, and finite-element stress-strain work are related ideas that answer different questions. The value of the finite-element model is that it exposes where the clinical simplification is mechanically aligned and where it is not.

## Limitations

The inverse-unloading step adds its own scope limit. The unloaded reference is the stress-free shape that the prescribed end-diastolic pressures would inflate back to the imaged geometry — a clean construction for a single end-diastolic mesh, but a demanding one when severe RV pressure is imposed on the same non-remodelled anatomy. Without RV hypertrophy or regional stiffening to share the load, the severe cases can only be explained by a larger deformation from the unloaded reference to end diastole. Green-Lagrange strain is measured relative to that reference and the Holzapfel-Ogden material stiffens exponentially with stretch, so the high-pressure RV and RV-side septal work-density magnitudes depend on which reference state is inferred.

The bias is not one-directional. Capping the RV unloading pressure reduces the inferred reference collapse, but it *raises* the severe-case RV and septal work densities rather than lowering them — in the highest-RV-pressure cases, mean RV work density rose from 4.68 to 6.20 kPa and septal work density from 2.26 to 5.53 kPa (see {ref}`chap-appendix-reference-state`). The proxy comparison is not invalidated; the absolute severe-case RV tissue-work scale is just conditional on the fixed-reference model.

The chosen correction keeps the end-diastolic mesh as the unloading target but caps the RV end-diastolic pressure used during unloading at 5 mmHg. This bounds the case-to-case calibration drift that the uncapped severe cases would otherwise produce in the inferred unloaded RV. The round-trip check makes the effect concrete: with the cap applied, the severe case (peak RV systolic about 100 mmHg) recovers the imaged ED shape to within 3.7 mm whole-mesh mean (4.3 mm at the septum); without the cap the same case falls to 9.4 mm whole-mesh (13.9 mm at the septum, maximum 32 mm). The cap therefore restores round-trip consistency between the inverse-unloading and forward-inflation problems while leaving the beat simulation and proxy comparison in the same fixed-geometry framework.

One alternative was tested and rejected. Regional RV and septal stiffening reduces the unloaded collapse, but without patient-specific calibration the stiffness scale becomes an unconstrained tuning knob. The capped path is the cleanest bounded option: not a fully remodelled PAH model, but a calibration choice that keeps the sixteen cases comparable on the cycle side.

A small anatomical bias also sits in the mesh itself. The UKB atlas exports the RV cavity as two separately segmented pieces, and the meshing pipeline applies a Laplacian smoothing pass to merge them before GMSH tetrahedralization. As a side effect this thickens the RV wall against the unchanged epicardium by about 1.2 mm on average ({ref}`sec-geometry-anatomical-model`), leaving the production mesh's RV/LV thickness ratio at the upper edge of healthy adult values. The fixed-geometry sweep therefore starts from an RV anatomy slightly thicker than a freshly segmented healthy heart would give — closer to "slightly hypertrophied healthy" than to a clean thin-walled baseline. This does not affect the cross-case proxy rankings, but it does mean the absolute RV work-density values should be read as anatomy-conditional.

The finite-element stress-strain work is an internally consistent reference, not a patient-level ground truth. It depends on the chosen Holzapfel-Ogden material parameters, fibre architecture, active tension law, and activation timing. Holding these fixed isolates the pressure-assignment question but also removes biological variation that could change regional stress and work. The results should therefore be read as a mechanical test of proxy structure within one model family, not as a claim that the absolute stress or work fields are uniquely determined in real myocardium.

The active-tension law is also deliberately simple. It prescribes the same time course and peak active tension regardless of local fibre stretch, so it does not include the Frank-Starling mechanism by which a more filled or stretched myocardium can generate more force.

The basal support remains a modelling limitation. The production setup used Robin springs together with a partial basal displacement constraint rather than a fully free or anatomically complete atrioventricular support. The constraint was deliberately weaker than a full clamp, and displacement-field checks indicate that tangential sliding remained. However, the tested Robin-only production variants did not converge during end-diastolic inflation, so alternative stable basal-support formulations were not quantified as a sensitivity for regional work fields. The small net Robin work suggests that the springs are not the dominant energy term, but the boundary condition should still be treated as part of the model definition.

The finite-element model removes several clinical measurement errors, but it does not replace clinical validation: it does not show that the proxy predicts oxygen consumption, invasive contractility, symptoms, or outcomes in patients. Those require separate clinical validation with measured or well-estimated RV pressure.

Finally, the pressure-strain proxy itself uses longitudinal strain averaged over regions. Clinical speckle tracking can report several anatomical strain directions, but the myocardial-work workflows tested here are based on longitudinal strain {cite}`voigt2015definitions,sade2023current,abawi2022noninvasive`, so the fibre-aligned calculations in the results should be read as mechanics diagnostics rather than routine clinical measurements. Fibre-aligned strain improved the septal pressure comparison, especially for mean and through-wall weighted pressures, but that does not make fibre strain a clinical replacement: it shows that the longitudinal proxy loses information about the dominant tissue deformation direction. A true local strain-resolution strategy would require full three-dimensional strain information, including shear, not only a different scalar component.
