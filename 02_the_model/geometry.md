# Geometry, Simulation Cases, and Software

## The Anatomical Model Used In This Study

The analyses reported in this thesis use one biventricular geometry: a synthetic baseline derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`. The atlas was constructed by Mauger et al. from cardiovascular magnetic resonance images of 4,329 participants in the UK Biobank study, fitting a subdivision surface template to each scan using diffeomorphic registration. A reference sub-cohort of 630 participants with no cardiovascular risk factors was used to define normal reference morphology, and the mean shape of this sub-cohort serves as the baseline geometry here. {numref}`fig-mesh-regions` shows the resulting mesh and gives the reader a concrete object to anchor the geometric and constitutive description that follows.

The geometry used here is not the full anatomical UK Biobank shape but a clipped version of it, restricted to the ventricular myocardium between the apex and a single planar base just below the atrioventricular valves. The atria, the four valves themselves, and the outflow tracts above the valve plane are removed; what remains is two ventricular cavities bounded by the LV free wall, the RV free wall, the septum, and a flat basal cap that the boundary conditions described later will treat with a partial base-normal Dirichlet constraint and a Robin support. This clipping is standard for biventricular FEM cardiac mechanics — the atria and valves are represented in the 0D circulation model rather than in the spatially resolved domain — but it is not anatomically neutral. The RV is affected disproportionately. Its inflow and outflow tracts run roughly parallel to the basal plane and a meaningful fraction of true in vivo RV cavity volume sits above the clip; the LV cavity, by contrast, is more compact below the mitral plane and loses comparatively little volume to the same cut. The end-diastolic cavity volume measured from the mesh therefore underestimates physiological RV volume more strongly than physiological LV volume. The volume-scaling ratio introduced in the 0D circulation section absorbs this offset for the purposes of imposing the correct fractional volume excursion, but the underlying shape of the RV cavity is still that of a clipped crescent — something to keep in mind when reading RV-side numbers in the results.

Using one geometry is a deliberate restriction. It lets the longitudinal pressure-strain proxy be tested under controlled loading changes without simultaneously changing wall thickness, cavity size, septal curvature, or patient-specific segmentation details. This makes the interpretation cleaner: when the proxy changes across the sweep, the change comes from the imposed circulation and the resulting deformation, not from a different anatomy. The cost is that the sweep should not be read as a real PAH disease trajectory. Real PAH remodelling changes geometry and material properties together with pressure. Patient-specific healthy and PAH meshes are available in the wider project and are natural future tests, but they are not part of the main result set reported in this thesis.

## Mesh Generation

Mesh generation is handled by the `cardiac-geometries` library {cite}`cardiac_geometries`. For the UK Biobank case, the function `cardiac_geometries.mesh.ukb()` generates a tetrahedral volume mesh directly from the mean atlas surface geometry, parametrized by a target characteristic element length. We use a characteristic length of 5 mm, which yields a mesh with tens of thousands of tetrahedral elements. At this resolution, the main features of the myocardial stress and strain field are resolved at a scale below the anatomical regions used for integration: LV free wall, RV free wall, and septum. The mesh is not meant to resolve detailed septal transmural layers; this limitation matters for the through-wall septal analyses in the results chapter.

All meshes are generated with node coordinates in millimeters and scaled by a factor of $10^{-3}$ to meters before any finite element computation, consistent with the unit system of the constitutive law parameters (kPa and m). The meshes are rotated after generation so that the outward normal to the basal plane is aligned with the positive $x$-axis, a convention that simplifies the application of the out-of-plane Dirichlet constraint at the base.

The boundary surfaces of the mesh are labeled with integer tags that distinguish the left ventricular endocardium, the right ventricular endocardium, the epicardial surface, and the basal plane. These tags serve multiple purposes downstream. They define the surfaces on which endocardial pressure tractions are applied during the simulation. They identify the epicardial surface and the base for the Robin spring conditions that mimic pericardial and myocardial tethering. They define the surface subject to the Dirichlet constraint that prevents rigid body translation. And they provide the Dirichlet boundary conditions for the Laplace equations used by the fiber assignment algorithm to generate the transmural and apico-basal coordinate fields. The same tagging convention also makes the pipeline portable to patient-specific meshes, which is why those meshes are a useful next step even though they are not used in the main analyses here.

The interventricular septum is defined at the cell level rather than the surface level: each finite element is tagged as belonging to the LV free wall, the RV free wall, or the septum, with the initial assignment generated by the LDRB algorithm from the transmural coordinate fields. In practice, the automatic assignment of the septal boundary requires manual inspection and occasional correction, since the topological boundary between the septum and the free walls is not always captured faithfully by the Laplace-based coordinates alone. The simulation code includes an interactive graphical editor that allows the anatomical region tags to be corrected before the simulation is run, ensuring that the spatial integration domains used for the work computation align with the actual anatomical boundaries. The three-way colouring shown back in {numref}`fig-mesh-regions` is the result of this tagging.

## The FEniCSx Software Ecosystem

The entire simulation is implemented within the FEniCSx software ecosystem {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx`. FEniCSx is the next-generation rewrite of the FEniCS project, built around DOLFINx — a parallel, MPI-distributed finite element library with a C++ core and a Python interface. The mathematical description of the governing equations is expressed using UFL, the Unified Form Language, which provides a domain-specific Python syntax for writing weak forms of partial differential equations in a notation that closely mirrors the mathematical literature. The form compiler FFCx translates UFL expressions into optimized C kernels at runtime, which are just-in-time compiled and linked before the first assembly. This separation between the mathematical specification and the compiled implementation allows the constitutive model to be modified at the UFL level without touching the solver infrastructure — a property that proved practically important when diagnosing the stress computation bug described in the implementation chapter.

The cardiac-specific abstraction is provided by `fenicsx-pulse` {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, a library developed and maintained by Henrik Finsberg at Simula Research Laboratory. It implements the Holzapfel-Ogden constitutive model, the active stress decomposition, cavity pressure boundary conditions, Robin spring terms for the pericardium, and the prestressing algorithm, all within the FEniCSx framework. Using fenicsx-pulse substantially reduces the volume of application-level code required to define the cardiac mechanics problem: the user specifies a material object, an active model, a compressibility formulation, boundary conditions, and a geometry object, and the library assembles and solves the nonlinear system. The MPI-parallel fiber assignment is handled by the `ldrb` library {cite}`bayer2012novel`, which implements the Laplace-Dirichlet Rule-Based algorithm on DOLFINx meshes and is also developed at Simula. Checkpointing of finite element fields across time steps uses `adios4dolfinx`, which writes function data in the ADIOS2 binary format in a way that is consistent with the distributed mesh partitioning and can be read back on a different number of MPI processes. The `scifem` library provides the infrastructure for storing activation on piecewise-varying function spaces whose basis changes according to cell tag.

This project was carried out at Simula Research Laboratory in Oslo, where several of the core developers of FEniCSx are based. Jørgen Dokken, one of the principal contributors to DOLFINx and the author of extensive tutorial material on the library, and Henrik Finsberg, the author of fenicsx-pulse, ldrb, and cardiac-geometries, were directly available as supervisors and collaborators throughout the work. The practical significance of this proximity was considerable. Several of the implementation problems described later — the stress computation bug in the compressible formulation, the quadrature function space projection strategy, and the scifem-based activation infrastructure — were identified and resolved by tracing the library implementations with input from their authors. That context matters because the thesis depends on field-level stress and work quantities, not only on standard pressure-volume outputs.
