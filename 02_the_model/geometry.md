# Geometry, Simulation Cases, and Software

## The Three Simulation Cases

The computational study spans three distinct anatomical configurations. Each represents a different point along the spectrum from idealized to pathological, and together they allow us to assess whether the conclusions about septal work estimation depend on the particular geometry used or are stable across a range of cardiac shapes and disease states.

The first case uses a synthetic biventricular geometry derived from the UK Biobank statistical shape model {cite}`mauger2019right`. The atlas was constructed by Mauger et al. from cardiovascular magnetic resonance images of 4,329 participants in the UK Biobank study, fitting a subdivision surface mesh template to each scan using a diffeomorphic registration algorithm. A reference sub-cohort of 630 participants with no cardiovascular risk factors was identified to define the normal reference morphology, and the mean shape of this healthy sub-cohort serves as the synthetic baseline geometry. The resulting surface is a biventricular structure of physiologically realistic proportions — an end-diastolic LV cavity volume of approximately 140–160 mL, wall thicknesses of 8–10 mm in the LV free wall and 3–5 mm in the RV free wall, and a septal thickness intermediate between the two. Because it represents a population average rather than any specific individual, the geometry lacks the minor anatomical irregularities present in real hearts, making it well-suited as a clean reference case in which the pressure-work relationship can be examined without subject-specific confounds.

The second geometry is patient-specific, obtained from cardiovascular magnetic resonance imaging of a healthy volunteer. The ventricular boundaries were segmented from short-axis and long-axis cine images and converted into a tetrahedral finite element mesh using the same processing pipeline as the synthetic case. This case serves primarily as a validation point: by running the same simulation framework on a real anatomy with known healthy hemodynamics, we verify that the model produces plausible pressure-volume loops, ejection fractions, and peak pressures without systematic failure introduced by anatomical idiosyncrasies.

The third geometry is the disease case that motivates the entire study. It corresponds to a patient with confirmed pulmonary arterial hypertension, obtained from an institutional clinical database alongside the hemodynamic measurements used for model calibration. The PAH anatomy carries the characteristic morphological adaptations of sustained right ventricular pressure overload: the RV free wall is hypertrophied, its cavity has enlarged and become more spherical, and the interventricular septum exhibits the flattening visible on echocardiography as the D-sign. These morphological changes reflect altered mechanical load distribution across the myocardium and make the question of septal pressure attribution directly relevant to clinical assessment in this patient population.

## Mesh Generation

Mesh generation for all three cases is handled by the `cardiac-geometries` library {cite}`cardiac_geometries`. For the UK Biobank case, the function `cardiac_geometries.mesh.ukb()` generates a tetrahedral volume mesh directly from the mean atlas surface geometry, parametrized by a target characteristic element length. We use a characteristic length of 5 mm throughout all three cases, which yields approximately 40,000–50,000 tetrahedral elements per geometry. At this resolution, the main features of the myocardial stress and strain field — the transmural gradient of fiber stress, the septal work distribution, the LV-RV boundary — are resolved at a scale well below the anatomical regions of interest. Patient-specific meshes are loaded from pre-segmented XDMF surface files using the same library, after which the meshing and tagging procedure is identical.

All meshes are generated with node coordinates in millimeters and scaled by a factor of $10^{-3}$ to meters before any finite element computation, consistent with the unit system of the constitutive law parameters (kPa and m). The meshes are rotated after generation so that the outward normal to the basal plane is aligned with the positive $x$-axis, a convention that simplifies the application of the out-of-plane Dirichlet constraint at the base.

The boundary surfaces of the mesh are labeled with integer tags that distinguish the left ventricular endocardium, the right ventricular endocardium, the epicardial surface, and the basal plane. These tags serve multiple purposes downstream. They define the surfaces on which endocardial pressure tractions are applied during the simulation. They identify the epicardial surface and the base for the Robin spring conditions that mimic pericardial and myocardial tethering. They define the surface subject to the Dirichlet constraint that prevents rigid body translation. And they provide the Dirichlet boundary conditions for the Laplace equations used by the fiber assignment algorithm to generate the transmural and apico-basal coordinate fields. The consistency of these tags across all three geometries is what makes the simulation pipeline portable between cases.

The interventricular septum is defined at the cell level rather than the surface level: each finite element is tagged as belonging to the LV free wall, the RV free wall, or the septum, with the initial assignment generated by the LDRB algorithm from the transmural coordinate fields. In practice, the automatic assignment of the septal boundary requires manual inspection and occasional correction, since the topological boundary between the septum and the free walls is not always captured faithfully by the Laplace-based coordinates alone. The simulation code includes an interactive graphical editor that allows the anatomical region tags to be corrected before the simulation is run, ensuring that the spatial integration domains used for the work computation align with the actual anatomical boundaries.

## The FEniCSx Software Ecosystem

The entire simulation is implemented within the FEniCSx software ecosystem {cite}`baratta2023dolfinx,logg2012automated`. FEniCSx is the next-generation rewrite of the FEniCS project, built around DOLFINx — a parallel, MPI-distributed finite element library with a C++ core and a Python interface. The mathematical description of the governing equations is expressed using UFL, the Unified Form Language, which provides a domain-specific Python syntax for writing weak forms of partial differential equations in a notation that closely mirrors the mathematical literature. The form compiler FFCx translates UFL expressions into optimized C kernels at runtime, which are just-in-time compiled and linked before the first assembly. This separation between the mathematical specification and the compiled implementation allows the constitutive model to be modified at the UFL level without touching the solver infrastructure — a property that proved practically important when diagnosing the stress computation bug described in the implementation chapter.

The cardiac-specific abstraction is provided by `fenicsx-pulse` {cite}`fenicsx_pulse`, a library developed and maintained by Henrik Finsberg at Simula Research Laboratory. It implements the Holzapfel-Ogden constitutive model, the active stress decomposition, cavity pressure boundary conditions, Robin spring terms for the pericardium, and the prestressing algorithm, all within the FEniCSx framework. Using fenicsx-pulse substantially reduces the volume of application-level code required to define the cardiac mechanics problem: the user specifies a material object, an active model, a compressibility formulation, boundary conditions, and a geometry object, and the library assembles and solves the nonlinear system. The MPI-parallel fiber assignment is handled by the `ldrb` library {cite}`bayer2012novel`, which implements the Laplace-Dirichlet Rule-Based algorithm on DOLFINx meshes and is also developed at Simula. Checkpointing of finite element fields across time steps uses `adios4dolfinx`, which writes function data in the ADIOS2 binary format in a way that is consistent with the distributed mesh partitioning and can be read back on a different number of MPI processes. The `scifem` library provides the infrastructure for creating piecewise-varying function spaces whose basis changes according to cell tag — the mechanism behind the spatially varying peak active tension.

This project was carried out at Simula Research Laboratory in Oslo, where several of the core developers of FEniCSx are based. Jørgen Dokken, one of the principal contributors to DOLFINx and the author of extensive tutorial material on the library, and Henrik Finsberg, the author of fenicsx-pulse, ldrb, and cardiac-geometries, were directly available as supervisors and collaborators throughout the work. The practical significance of this proximity was considerable. Several of the implementation problems described in Chapter 3 — the stress computation bug in the compressible formulation, the quadrature function space projection strategy, the scifem-based spatially varying activation — were identified and resolved through direct conversation with the library authors rather than through documentation alone. The ability to file a bug report and receive a response from the person who wrote the code, or to raise a question over coffee and have it answered with reference to the implementation, is a resource that does not appear in any methods section but shapes the character of the work profoundly.
