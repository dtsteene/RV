# Active Contraction

The constitutive model described in the previous section governs how the myocardium stores and releases elastic energy in response to passive deformation. Alone, it describes a nonlinearly elastic solid that would deform under pressure but never generate force on its own. What transforms this passive material into a beating heart is the active contraction of the sarcomeres — the molecular motors embedded within each muscle fiber that convert chemical energy into mechanical tension on a timescale of hundreds of milliseconds.

## The Active Stress Decomposition

We model this using the active stress approach, in which the total stress in the tissue is decomposed additively into a passive elastic part, governed by the Holzapfel-Ogden strain energy described previously, and an active part generated by the contracting sarcomeres:

$$
\mathbf{S}_\text{total} = \mathbf{S}_\text{passive} + \mathbf{S}_\text{active}.
$$

The active stress is generated exclusively in the fiber direction, consistent with the observation that sarcomeric contraction acts along the long axis of the myofilaments. It takes the form

$$
\mathbf{S}_\text{active} = T_a \, (\mathbf{f}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0),
$$

where $\mathbf{f}_0$ is the unit fiber vector in the reference configuration and $T_a$ is a scalar active tension, with units of pressure, representing the magnitude of the contractile force per unit reference area. The outer product $\mathbf{f}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0$ is the structural tensor that projects the tension exclusively onto the fiber direction, ensuring that the active contribution to the stress is uniaxial and aligned with the local fiber orientation. This is a straightforward but effective simplification: it captures the dominant mechanical effect of myocardial activation without requiring a detailed biophysical model of the crossbridge dynamics.

## Activation Timing and the Blanco Model

The active tension $T_a$ is a function of time within the cardiac cycle, rising from zero at the onset of systole, reaching a peak, and then decaying as the cardiomyocytes relax. The temporal profile used in this simulation follows the Blanco ventricular activation model {cite}`blanco2010computational`, which parameterizes the activation waveform by three quantities: the time of onset $t_C$, the duration of the contraction phase $T_C$, and the duration of the relaxation phase $T_R$. The waveform rises smoothly from zero during the contraction phase, reaches a peak at $T_a^\text{max}$, and decays back to zero during the relaxation phase, after which it remains at zero through the diastolic filling interval.

The cycle length is determined by the target heart rate. At 75 beats per minute, the RR interval is 0.8 seconds. The activation timing parameters are scaled proportionally: the contraction phase occupies approximately 0.25 s, the relaxation phase approximately 0.40 s, and the remaining time corresponds to diastolic filling. These values are consistent with the physiological durations of isovolumic contraction, ejection, isovolumic relaxation, and filling observed in a resting adult, and they were calibrated iteratively against the pressure-volume loop shapes produced by the coupled simulation.

## Spatially Varying Activation

The heart is not uniformly activated. The left ventricle has a substantially thicker free wall than the right and is responsible for the much higher systolic pressures of the systemic circulation; it requires more force per unit cross-sectional area to do this work, and its sarcomeres generate correspondingly higher peak tensions. The right ventricular free wall, operating against the lower pulmonary pressures, contracts with lower peak tension. The interventricular septum, which shares mechanical load with both chambers, is assigned an intermediate value.

In the simulation, this spatial heterogeneity is implemented by assigning three distinct activation amplitudes to three anatomical zones: the left ventricular free wall, the interventricular septum, and the right ventricular free wall. The zone assignments are determined by cell-level tags on the finite element mesh — tags generated initially by the LDRB algorithm during fiber assignment and subsequently refined manually using an interactive mesh editing tool to ensure that the septal boundary is anatomically reasonable. The maximum active tension assigned to the LV and RV free walls is approximately 100 kPa, while the septum is assigned 70 kPa. Within each zone, the same normalized Blanco waveform is used, scaled by the zone's peak tension.

This approach — spatially varying peak tension with a shared activation waveform — is a simplification of the true electromechanical activation sequence, which propagates as a wave from the apex upward and arrives at different wall segments at slightly different times. For the purposes of this study, which focuses on integrated work over the cardiac cycle rather than the detailed timing of local mechanics, the spatially uniform activation timing within each zone is an acceptable approximation. The primary mechanical effect of heterogeneous activation timing — the distinction between early-activated endocardial and late-activated epicardial regions — is secondary to the pressure-work comparison that constitutes the main scientific question.
